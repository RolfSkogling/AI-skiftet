Models

Anthropic releases Claude Sonnet 5 – near-flagship power at half the price

Anthropic yesterday, June 30, released Claude Sonnet 5, a new mid-tier model it calls its most agentic yet. It can plan, use tools such as browsers and terminals, and run autonomously for extended stretches.

Sonnet 5 lands close to the flagship Opus 4.8 on reasoning, tool use, coding and knowledge work, yet costs less than half as much. On an agentic coding test it scored 63.2 percent, against 69.2 for Opus 4.8 and 58.1 for its predecessor Sonnet 4.6. Introductory pricing is 2 dollars per million input tokens and 10 dollars per million output tokens – a level that holds until August 31, when it rises to 3 and 15 dollars respectively. The model becomes the default for free and Pro users.

For the Nordics, the lower price matters most. A near-top-tier agentic model at under half the cost makes it cheaper for Swedish and Norwegian developers, startups and large firms such as SEB, DNB and Ericsson to run AI agents at scale – and lowers the barrier for public-sector pilots in municipalities and regions that cost has held back. At the same time, dependence on a handful of American model providers keeps growing, a strategic vulnerability regularly flagged in both Stockholm and Oslo.

Source: Anthropic, TechCrunch, MarkTechPost
Physical AI

Amazon passes one million warehouse robots – new AI model steers the fleet

Amazon has passed one million robots in its warehouses worldwide and is at the same time launching a new AI model, DeepFleet, to steer the entire robot fleet. The millionth robot was delivered to a fulfilment centre in Japan, part of a network of more than 300 sites.

DeepFleet is a so-called foundation model that uses generative AI to compute the best travel routes in real time, ease congestion and improve continuously. Amazon says the system cuts the robots' travel time by ten percent, enabling faster and cheaper deliveries. The company also says more than 700,000 employees have been trained for new technical and maintenance roles created by the automation – a figure that also shows how many tasks are changing.

For the Nordics this is close to a preview. Nordic logistics giants such as PostNord, Posten Norge and ICA face the same choices, and the region has its own front-runners: Norway's 1X Technologies (whose NEO robots EQT wants to roll out in up to 10,000 units across its portfolio companies), Swiss-Swedish ABB Robotics and Denmark's Universal Robots and MiR. For Nordic warehouse workers and unions the question becomes how fast the shift comes and who pays for the reskilling.

Source: About Amazon, Supply Chain 24/7, The AI Signal
Labour market

Swedish AI council: jobs are changing faster than they disappear

AI is reshaping the Swedish labour market above all through new tasks and skill requirements – not by making jobs vanish. That is the core message of a new assessment from the Labour Market AI Council, a collaboration led by AI Sweden.

The council, which includes Akavia, Almega, DIK, Fremia, Svensk Handel and Unionen, notes that Sweden is among the countries most exposed to AI, but that most occupations are so far complemented rather than replaced. As AI is woven into workflows, the value of domain knowledge, judgement, communication and the ability to decide when human and machine work best together instead rises. A survey by Unionen shows that two-thirds of member companies have already introduced AI – more than a doubling in two years.

For the Nordics the kinship is clear. The council urges the state to strengthen incentives for employers' skills investment, especially where time and resources for learning are scarce – an issue that applies just as much to Norway, where NHO and LO track the Swedish model. For Nordic workers the report points to a labour market where continuing education, not layoffs, becomes the decisive battle of the coming years.

Source: AI Sweden, Almega, Akavia
Research

Swedish-Norwegian project teaches AI to ease healthcare with safer decisions

A Swedish-Norwegian research project has examined how AI-based decision support can make healthcare more accessible, safe and resource-efficient. The Kontiki project is led by the University of Borås and Østfold University College and runs with support from Interreg Sweden–Norway.

In one sub-project, machine learning shows clearly better accuracy than today's routines in assessing deep vein thrombosis – a blood clot in the deep veins – which can reduce the need for hospital visits and resource-intensive examinations. In another, researchers at Chalmers have built a model that assesses how severely injured a trauma patient is already at the scene, with good accuracy tested in both Sweden and Norway.

For the Nordics the point is that the benefit arises where staff shortages are greatest. Rural areas in both Sweden and Norway struggle with long distances to hospitals and stretched staffing, and accurate decision support can relieve emergency departments and ambulances. That the project is run jointly across the border also shows how the Swedish-Norwegian research axis – with Karolinska, Chalmers, NTNU and Sintef as heavy nodes – offers a way for the Nordics to build their own healthcare AI rather than only import finished systems.

Source: Högskolan i Borås, Interreg Sverige-Norge, Picta

Debate

Anthropic heads for a near-trillion-dollar IPO – Norway's oil fund warns of an AI bubble

AI company Anthropic has filed a confidential IPO registration with the US Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), preparing one of the largest tech listings ever. The filing follows a funding round that valued the company at $965 billion.

The confidential S-1 document was filed on June 1. Behind the valuation lies a Series H round of $65 billion – more than a doubling since February, when a Series G round valued the company at $380 billion. The listing is reportedly targeting Nasdaq in October, with Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan and Morgan Stanley as advisers and proceeds of more than $60 billion. Anthropic says its revenue has passed $47 billion on a run-rate basis, but the company is still loss-making – and critics warn that valuations across the AI sector have raced ahead of actual revenue.

For the Nordics the bill is very concrete. Norway's oil fund – the world's largest sovereign wealth fund, at around $2.1 trillion – has, through its chief executive Nicolai Tangen, warned that an AI bubble combined with geopolitical tension is the biggest threat to global markets, and that a sharp correction could wipe out up to 35 percent of the fund's value. More than half of the fund is invested in the US, with heavy holdings in Nvidia, Microsoft, Apple and Alphabet. The same concentration risk hits Sweden's AP pension funds and Nordic savers: when a handful of AI companies prop up the markets, Anthropic's listing becomes a test of whether the valuations hold.

Source: Fortune, CNBC, TechCrunch, Bloomberg
Hardware

AI's real bottleneck is memory and packaging – not the GPUs

The shortage of AI compute is less and less about the graphics chips themselves and more about two links further down the chain: high-bandwidth memory (HBM) and the advanced packaging that ties the chips together. Both are effectively sold out for 2026.

The next generation of memory, HBM4, is now ramping up. SK Hynix – expected to supply around two-thirds of Nvidia's HBM4 for the coming Vera Rubin platform – has at the same time chosen to slow its HBM4 build-out in order to capture higher margins on conventional DDR5 memory. Samsung is targeting around 250,000 wafers per month by the end of the year, while Micron is exiting consumer memory entirely to prioritize AI data centers. The tightest choke point sits at TSMC, whose advanced CoWoS packaging is fully booked all the way into 2027 – only then is supply expected to meet demand.

For the Nordics these bottlenecks determine how quickly the region's AI factories can be filled with capacity. Stargate Norway in Narvik, Sferical AI in Linköping and EcoDataCenter's facilities are all built around Nvidia chips – and their real build-out pace is governed by when memory and packaging can be had, not by how many GPUs have been ordered. For large users such as Ericsson, Volvo, SEB and Equinor, the tighter supply means the price of compute could stay high well into 2027, slowing Nordic AI investments that compete with US hyperscalers for the same memory capacity.

Source: TrendForce, Tech Times, Datacenter Dynamics
Quantum

The quantum computer moves into the AI factory as Quantinuum and HPE link the technologies

Quantum computers are beginning to be tied together with the supercomputers and AI systems that already drive research. On June 22, Quantinuum and Hewlett Packard Enterprise (HPE) presented a strategic collaboration to integrate quantum computing with high-performance computing and AI.

The collaboration is part of a broader movement. A week earlier, on June 15, HPE brought on eight quantum partners – including Finland's IQM – to expand quantum capacity in its data centers. In parallel, Sandia National Laboratories and Quantinuum validated a trapped-ion machine with 98 qubits, Helios, on June 18, and Duke University and IonQ demonstrated distributed entanglement across a three-node quantum network on June 20. Together the steps point toward quantum computers working with, rather than replacing, today's AI infrastructure.

For the Nordics, quantum is one of the few areas where the region sits at the absolute frontier. Finland's IQM securing a place in HPE's initiative shows that Nordic quantum hardware counts internationally, and Sweden's new national quantum center, led by Chalmers and WACQT, is building exactly the expertise needed as quantum and AI grow together. For Ericsson (quantum-safe encryption), SEB and Handelsbanken (risk calculations) and Saab (sensors), the question is no longer whether quantum is coming, but whether Nordic players connect in time to the European infrastructure now being built – through LUMI-Q among others.

Source: Quantinuum, The Quantum Insider, Quantum Computing Report
Models

The race over AI for science intensifies – Anthropic shows Claude for life science today

Anthropic is holding a livestreamed event today, June 30, called The Briefing: AI for Science, where the company shows how its model Claude is used in research and drug development together with pharma, biotech and university partners.

The event is part of a broader race in which the frontier labs compete to become the tool of research. Anthropic recently recruited John Jumper – the Nobel laureate behind DeepMind's protein-folding system AlphaFold – from Google DeepMind, one of several leading names to switch sides over the past month. At the same time, OpenAI and Google DeepMind are pursuing their own efforts in AI for biology, materials science and mathematics. The very possibility that a model could propose new experiments and hypotheses remains contested, however, and the results must be verified in the lab.

For the Nordics the stakes are high. Nordic life science – AstraZeneca, with heavy research in Gothenburg and Södertälje, Denmark's Novo Nordisk, the Karolinska Institute and Oslo University Hospital – is among Europe's most research-intensive environments and has much to gain from faster AI-driven discovery. At the same time, access becomes a strategic question: if the most powerful research models are released first to selected US partners – as happened with both GPT-5.6 and Anthropic's top models – Nordic universities and pharmaceutical companies risk waiting for the tools that most quickly turn research into results.

Infrastructure

The Nordics emerge as Europe’s data center hub for AI – hydropower lures the gigafactories

The Nordic region is emerging as Europe’s most sought-after location for AI data centers. In a short time, several billion-dollar projects have taken shape: Stargate Norway in Narvik, backed by Nscale, Aker and OpenAI, and the newly launched Fossefall, which aims to build clean AI capacity on Nordic hydropower.

Stargate Norway is set to deploy 100,000 Nvidia GPUs by the end of 2026, with an initial 230-megawatt build-out and possible expansion to 520 megawatts – entirely on renewable power, with direct-to-chip liquid cooling and surplus heat fed to local industry. Fossefall, launched in June with US firm Seekr as a part-owner, is targeting 500 megawatts by 2030 across eleven Norwegian sites. In parallel, Vattenfall and Nscale have entered a partnership for Norwegian AI infrastructure, while Mistral is investing 1.2 billion euros together with EcoDataCenter in Sweden and Microsoft is expanding its Swedish data centers for 3.2 billion dollars.

For the Nordics, the build-out raises both hope and concern. Narvik, Luleå and other towns with surplus hydropower stand to gain investment and jobs, even as local voices and Norway’s grid operator Statnett warn that the data centers push up electricity prices and create congestion on the grid. The question of sovereignty – who controls the capacity when OpenAI and Microsoft are the anchor customers – lands on the governments in Oslo and Stockholm, while Aker and Equinor position themselves as Nordic AI-infrastructure players.

Source: OpenAI, Datacenter Dynamics, Vattenfall
Research

Norwegian research teaches AI to squeeze more value out of hydropower

The research institute Sintef is leading an international project in which AI will help Norwegian hydropower producers make better day-to-day decisions. Today, producers solve almost the same scheduling puzzle from scratch every day – with no memory of yesterday’s solution.

The project, InterOpt, funded by the Research Council of Norway and running from 2024 to 2028, uses reinforcement learning to find relationships between water value, electricity price and inflow in historical data. The model has to weigh the value of releasing water now against saving it for later, while respecting physical limits and environmental requirements such as minimum flow rates. The producers ANEO, Hydro Energi and Å Energi have a seat at the table, alongside research groups from Spain, Canada and Brazil.

For the Nordics, the timing is hardly a coincidence. As AI data centers now compete for the same hydropower, every extra percent squeezed out of the reservoirs becomes valuable. Better scheduling can give Statkraft, Hafslund and Sweden’s Vattenfall higher revenue without new build-out, and strengthens the Nordic power market’s ability to balance the growing demand of both industry and data centers. Sintef and NTNU at the same time cement their role as a Nordic spearhead in AI for energy systems.

Source: Sintef, Research Council of Norway
Regulation

EU Council adopts the AI Act’s major simplification – high-risk rules pushed back

The EU Council of Ministers is expected to formally adopt the Digital Omnibus today, June 29 – the package of amendments that simplifies the AI Act. The European Parliament voted in favor back on June 16, with 423 votes for, 57 against and 174 abstentions.

The biggest change is that the requirements for high-risk systems in Annex III – recruitment, credit scoring and biometric identification – are pushed back from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027. At the same time, AI tools that create non-consensual intimate images and abuse material are now explicitly banned, while the deadlines for watermarking AI-generated content are extended. The text will be published in the EU’s Official Journal before it takes effect – until then, the original AI Act applies.

For the Nordics, the postponement offers breathing room. Sweden’s IMY, which the government appointed as market surveillance authority on June 15, and Norway’s Datatilsynet, which introduces the AI Act (KI-loven) in August, gain more time to build up oversight. SEB, Ericsson, Volvo, Telenor, DNB and Equinor – all of which are mapping internal high-risk systems – escape the tight August deadline, but still have to show progress. Critics in both the Riksdag and the Storting warn that the delay weakens protections just as AI systems are spreading fastest.

Source: European Council, White & Case
Labour market

AI jobs hold up in Sweden and Norway as other postings decline

Two fresh surveys point in the same direction: Nordic workers are rapidly gaining access to AI tools, but fear of mass layoffs is low. PwC’s global AI Jobs Barometer 2026 shows that AI-related roles are holding up in Sweden and Norway even as the total number of job postings falls.

The share of Nordic organizations where at least 40 percent of employees have access to approved AI tools rose from 37 to 56 percent in one year. At the same time, only 45 percent of Nordic employers expect major automation of jobs within ten years, compared with 65 percent globally. The explanation lies in strong collective agreements, high union membership and a tradition of social dialogue in which changes are negotiated rather than imposed.

For the Nordics, the figures sketch the contours of a two-track labor market: AI skills command a premium while broad occupational groups risk falling behind. For Unionen, IF Metall, Sveriges Ingenjörer and Norway’s LO and Fellesforbundet, the task becomes ensuring that the rapidly growing access to AI tools is also matched by training, so that the productivity gains are not concentrated in a few high-wage occupations. The question will be central to the 2026 wage round.

Models

OpenAI releases GPT-5.6 Sol, Terra and Luna – only about twenty partners get early access

OpenAI launched on Friday, June 26, a limited preview of its next-generation model series GPT-5.6: the flagship Sol, the mid-tier Terra and the cheap Luna. The release is restricted to roughly twenty companies approved by the US government, available via Codex and the API.

Sol ships with an "ultra" mode that coordinates sub-agents for complex tasks and according to OpenAI has reinforced safeguards against cyber and bio risks. Terra sits at roughly half the price of GPT-5.5 in the same performance class, while Luna is aimed at high-volume workloads. In the same blog post OpenAI writes that government filters of this kind "should not become the norm in the long run" and that a broader rollout is planned within a few weeks. The decision comes two weeks after the US forced Anthropic to block Fable 5 and Mythos 5 for foreign nationals on June 12 – a pattern where frontier models are released under government oversight first.

For the Nordics the restriction hits Wallenberg AI, Autonomous Systems and Software Program (WASP), AI Sweden and the Glasswing partners not on the US list. SEB, Ericsson, Volvo Group, Equinor and DNB – all of which have evaluated GPT-5.x for internal agent work – have to wait for the broader release. The question simultaneously lands on the desks of the governments in Stockholm and Oslo: should the European Commission and Nordic supervisory authorities (IMY, Datatilsynet) demand equivalent risk assessment before frontier models reach European companies, or will the Nordics end up in tier 2 for access to frontier capacity?

Regulation

Swedish government appoints IMY as national market surveillance authority for the EU AI Act

On June 15 the Swedish government formally tasked the Swedish Authority for Privacy Protection (IMY) with the role of market surveillance authority for the EU AI Act in Sweden. IMY will oversee that high-risk AI systems meet the AI Act requirements and gains a sanction mandate against providers that breach the regulation.

The decision confirms the line the government has signalled since 2025: consolidated supervision rather than fragmentation across sector authorities. IMY already holds the GDPR mandate and now also takes on the Annex III systems – recruitment, credit scoring, biometric identification and critical infrastructure. In parallel, Vinnova and RISE have begun work on standardised templates for compliance documentation, something Swedish AI providers need to handle the new oversight.

For Wallenberg AI Factory and Chalmers compliance projects, IMY’s new role becomes a concrete business opportunity – standardised Annex III documentation could become a European de facto template. Norway is taking a parallel path: Datatilsynet gets an equivalent supervisory role when the KI Act enters into force in August, with KI Norge being established under a new director. SEB, Ericsson, Volvo and Telenor now have to map internal AI systems against the AI Act risk classes before the August deadline. The decision increases pressure on CIO/CDO functions and the banks’ Chief AI Officers to complete model-use inventories before autumn.

Source: IMY, Vinnova
Quantum

Six Swedish universities form national quantum centre – Chalmers coordinates the 2035 push

On June 11, Chalmers, KTH, Lund University, Stockholm University, Linköping University and Uppsala University signed a memorandum of understanding to establish the Swedish Center for Quantum Technology. The centre is coordinated from Chalmers and brings together research, education and infrastructure across the entire value chain. The goal is to make Sweden European leader in quantum technology by 2035.

Roles are sharply divided: Chalmers leads quantum computing and quantum simulation, KTH runs quantum communication and cryptography, and Lund University covers quantum sensors. In late June, nine European parties – Chalmers included – also signed an agreement to acquire and operate a EuroHPC quantum computer within the LUMI-Q consortium. This ties WACQT and the rest of the Nordic quantum ecosystem to the EU infrastructure and accelerates capacity build-out before error-corrected quantum computers are expected to cross threshold values in the second half of the 2020s.

For the Nordics the initiative gives the Knut and Alice Wallenberg Foundation, which has funded WACQT with more than one billion Swedish kronor since 2018, a clear continuation platform. For Ericsson (post-quantum cryptography), SEB and Handelsbanken (financial risk computation) and Saab (quantum sensors for defence) it becomes easier to engage a single coordinated Swedish research partner. The Research Council of Norway and NTNU are tracking the development – several Norwegian quantum groups already have formal collaborations with Chalmers and can be connected via LUMI-Q. The centre strengthens the Nordic position in the European quantum race against the German and French national efforts.

Source: Chalmers, Uppsala University
Physical AI

Figure 03 hits one robot per hour at the BotQ factory – humanoid production shifts gear

Figure AI confirmed during June that Figure 03 production has reached a cadence of one robot per hour at the new BotQ factory – a symbolic but technologically significant threshold. At the same time Boston Dynamics shipped the first commercial units of the electric Atlas to Hyundai and Google DeepMind. Tesla Optimus Gen 3, which began mass production earlier this year, now has several hundred units operating at the Fremont and Giga Texas factories for supervised learning.

The sector is moving from prototypes to volume production. Agility Robotics has Digit units in commercial deployment via Robot-as-a-Service at GXO Logistics, and Unitree is targeting more than 5,500 units for the full year 2026. The underlying driver is that Vision-Language-Action models – such as NVIDIA Isaac GR00T, Figure’s Helix and Google’s RT-X – are beginning to generalise enough for a single policy to handle picking, folding and assembly across different environments without new fine-tuning. That turns humanoids from demo videos into measurable productivity tools.

For Sweden’s Volvo Group, Scania and ABB the scaling means that Tier-1 suppliers can begin to calculate unit economics for humanoids on assembly lines during 2027 rather than 2030. Ericsson and Telenor both have 5G/edge pilots where humanoids connect to private networks in warehouse environments. On the union side the pace raises questions – Unionen, IF Metall, Sveriges Ingenjörer and Norway’s Fellesforbundet need to formulate positions ahead of the 2026 collective bargaining round on how transition clauses should handle physical AI as a substitute for manual tasks. AI Sweden’s new Labour Market AI Council is one of the few Nordic arenas where the question is discussed systematically.

Regulation

Norway readies its AI Act – Nkom to become the country's AI watchdog

Norway is preparing to bring the EU's AI Act into national law through a new AI Act (KI-loven) expected to take effect this summer. The Norwegian Communications Authority (Nkom) is designated as the national coordinating supervisory authority and the contact point toward the EU.

The bill has been in public consultation since 2025. The proposed model lets sector regulators and the Data Protection Authority (Datatilsynet) – the latter for purposes such as law enforcement – share oversight of high-risk systems, while the Norwegian Digitalisation Agency (Digdir) runs KI Norge: a national arena offering guidance and a regulatory sandbox.

For the Nordics, it shows how differently neighbours are solving the same EU requirements. Sweden has chosen to make the Privacy Protection Authority (IMY) its central AI supervisory authority, with PTS, the Financial Supervisory Authority and the Medical Products Agency as sector authorities, while Norway places coordination with Nkom. Both must have their national rules in place as the EU's transparency rules take effect on August 2, 2026, while the strictest high-risk obligations were recently pushed back to 2027–2028. For companies operating across the Nordics – from DNB and Equinor to SEB and Ericsson – it means the same rulebook is interpreted by different authorities on either side of the border.

Source: Regjeringen, Nkom, Datatilsynet
Debate

Anthropic's AI found holes in classified US government systems – within hours

A US official confirmed to the Associated Press that one of Anthropic's AI models, Mythos, identified vulnerabilities in highly sensitive and secure US government computer systems during a testing exercise – some within a matter of hours.

The test was conducted in collaboration between Anthropic and US intelligence agencies under Project Glasswing, an effort to strengthen critical software before powerful AI spreads more widely. The fact that the model could find the holes does not mean it could exploit them in the same time. Anthropic says Mythos has already found thousands of high-severity vulnerabilities, including in every major operating system and web browser, and is now expanding Glasswing to around 150 new organizations across more than 15 countries in power, water, healthcare, communications and hardware.

For the Nordics, it confronts cyber defense with the same duality: the same capability that finds and patches holes can also be used to attack. Sweden's MSB and FRA, along with Norway's National Security Authority and Nkom, are tracking AI-driven threats to power companies such as Vattenfall and Statnett, telecoms such as Telenor and Ericsson, and healthcare. As Glasswing reaches more than 15 countries, the question becomes whether Nordic critical infrastructure should be connected – and who then gains access to a model that can map the weaknesses.

Source: CNBC/AP, TechCrunch, Anthropic, Cybersecurity Dive
Hardware

Qualcomm chases chip star Tenstorrent in billion-dollar fight against Nvidia

Qualcomm is reportedly in advanced talks to acquire AI chip company Tenstorrent – founded by legendary chip architect Jim Keller – for up to $10 billion. The reports were published by The Information and have been picked up by several industry outlets.

Tenstorrent builds AI chips on the open RISC-V standard rather than closed licensing models. Together with its announced purchase of software company Modular, Qualcomm would commit close to $14 billion to a single goal: making it possible to run AI without Nvidia's chips and software. The valuation has roughly tripled in a year, from around $3.2 billion in 2025. The deal is not done, and Intel is also said to be interested.

For the Nordics, it is about how locked the region is into a single chip dependency. The big Nordic AI factories – Stargate Norway in Narvik and Sweden's Sferical AI in Linköping – are built today on Nvidia chips. More credible alternatives could over time lower the price of compute for Swedish and Norwegian companies and reduce vulnerability if export rules or deliveries are cut off. For Ericsson, which weaves AI into telecom networks, a more open chip market could mean more suppliers to choose from.

Hardware

OpenAI unveils its first AI chip, built with Broadcom – aiming to make ChatGPT cheaper to run

OpenAI has unveiled its first in-house AI chip, called Jalapeño. Developed together with Broadcom, the chip is purpose-built for inference – running already-trained models when users ask questions.

The chip went from design to a manufacturing-ready circuit in just nine months, which the companies call one of the fastest development cycles ever for an advanced semiconductor, and OpenAI says it used its own models to speed up the design work. Labour market

OpenAI: AI agents are taking on ever longer tasks – including outside tech

On June 25, OpenAI published a new report on how AI agents are changing work. Its conclusion: the job is shifting from short chat questions to delegating whole, long tasks that the agent works on independently for minutes or hours.

The report is based on usage of OpenAI's coding tool Codex. By May 2026, just over 80 percent of a sample of individual users had made at least one request estimated to equal more than half an hour of human work, and a quarter had made at least one request estimated to equal more than eight hours of human work. Inside OpenAI, Codex has become the most important AI tool in every department – including legal, finance and recruiting – and the number of users without a programming background has, according to the report, multiplied many times over. The figures come from OpenAI's own data and partly rest on model estimates, which should be kept in mind.

For the Nordics, the report points to the same shift that Swedish and Norwegian white-collar workers have already begun to notice: when agents take over routine tasks, the question is less about who holds a job and more about which tasks remain. Unions and labour-market bodies such as Unionen, Akavia and TCO in Sweden and LO and NHO in Norway are pushing for reskilling, while AI Sweden wants to speed up adoption across business. The fact that OpenAI highlights how lawyers and recruiters too are starting to use agents means the shift is reaching professions long considered safe.

Source: OpenAI, The Next Web
Models

Sweden is building its own language model – and must settle how authors are paid

Sweden is taking new steps toward its own large-scale language model for Swedish. The government has appointed Katrin Westling Palm as national coordinator tasked with driving the work, while the development itself sits with the research program WASP.

Behind the model are WASP researchers at several Swedish universities, including KTH, Chalmers and the universities of Linköping, Uppsala and Umeå, with funding from WASP of up to SEK 30 million (up to SEK 40 million in total) and training on the Berzelius supercomputer in Linköping. What makes it distinctive is that authors, book publishers and news media are contributing editorially reviewed Swedish text, so that the model reflects Swedish linguistic, social and legal conditions rather than relying solely on translations and English-language data. Most of the training is to take place during 2026.

For the Nordics this is both a sovereignty and a rights issue. One of the coordinator's most important tasks will be to work out how rights holders should be compensated when protected material is used to train models – a question the Swedish Publishers' Association has pushed hard and which concerns every Nordic language with few speakers. Norway is having a similar discussion about its own language resources, and for both Swedish and Norwegian there is a shared worry: that the largest, most widely used models will understand the Nordic languages less well than English unless the region builds its own.

Source: Swedish Government, WASP, Knut and Alice Wallenberg Foundation, Swedish Publishers' Association
Research

Sweden, Norway and Denmark join forces for a common Nordic voice on AI

The academic AI communities in Sweden, Norway and Denmark have signed a cooperation agreement to speak with a common Nordic voice on how AI affects society. The agreement was signed in Oslo on June 8 and brings together research where the social sciences and humanities are central.

Behind the cooperation are Sweden's WASP-HS (the Wallenberg program for AI, autonomous systems and software – humanities and society), Denmark's CAISA (the national centre for AI in society) and Norway's research consortium NORA. The idea is that the three countries should be able to give coordinated advice to policymakers and ensure that AI is developed in a way anchored in people and democratic values, rather than each country pursuing the issues on its own.

For the Nordics the point carries more weight than it may seem. When AI is driven by a handful of American and Chinese players, a unified Nordic voice gives more influence over how the EU's AI Act is interpreted and how technologies such as language models and decision support are woven into healthcare, schools and public administration. The Nordic countries have small, partly related languages, high trust and similar welfare models, which both makes them a natural group and a possible model for AI that strengthens rather than erodes democracy.

Google loses two top AI names to OpenAI and Anthropic – shares slide

The fight for the sharpest AI minds has reached a new level. Within a few days in mid-June, Google lost two of its most prominent AI researchers to rivals OpenAI and Anthropic – and the market reacted immediately.

Noam Shazeer, co-lead of the Gemini models and co-author of the landmark 2017 paper “Attention Is All You Need,” announced on 18 June that he is leaving for OpenAI – barely two years after Google paid around $2.7 billion to bring him back via Character.AI. The next day, Nobel laureate John Jumper – who won the 2024 Chemistry prize for AlphaFold – confirmed that he is leaving Google DeepMind for Anthropic after nearly nine years. Alphabet shares fell about 6 percent on 22 June, wiping out roughly $250 billion in market value. Analyst Gil Luria at D.A. Davidson warned that “Google is losing the war for talent at the frontier of AI.”

For the Nordics, the talent war reaches all the way here. Sweden’s WASP (the Wallenberg AI program), AI Sweden and universities such as KTH, Chalmers and NTNU compete for the same few sought-after researchers, while the US labs’ salaries pull hardest. For the region, the question becomes how to retain talent – through homegrown computing power such as Sferical AI in Linköping and Telenor’s AI factory, strong research environments and the chance to work on socially useful AI rather than only the largest models. The risk is a brain drain in which the Nordics train and others recruit.

SpaceX rents out AI computing power for billions – the space company becomes a cloud giant

SpaceX has signed a deal to rent out computing power to AI startup Reflection AI. Elon Musk’s space company is thereby turning its enormous Colossus data center into a commercial cloud business – a so-called “neocloud.”

Reflection AI, founded by former DeepMind researchers Misha Laskin and Ioannis Antonoglou, will pay around $150 million a month from 1 July, totaling roughly $6.3 billion through 2029. The company gains immediate access to Nvidia’s top-end GB300 chips at the Colossus facility outside Memphis – the data center SpaceX took over when it bought xAI earlier this year. SpaceX already has similar deals with Anthropic, Google and the coding tool Cursor, and committed revenue from outside customers is now said to exceed $80 billion through 2029. For a space company, it is a remarkable pivot toward selling compute.

For the Nordics, the deal shows how computing power has become the truly scarce – and geopolitical – resource. It sharpens the question of compute sovereignty: Ericsson, Saab, AstraZeneca, SEB and Wallenberg have built Sferical AI in Linköping as their own secure cloud, while Telenor, together with Nvidia and KPMG, has opened Norway’s first sovereign AI factory. The Nordics also attract data centers with cheap hydropower and a cool climate. When the largest computing capacity is locked up by a handful of US players, the Nordic bets on their own capacity look increasingly strategic.

Finland’s IQM unveils a new quantum code – up to 1,000 times fewer errors

The Finnish quantum-computer company IQM has presented a new method that sharply reduces errors in quantum computers. Error correction is one of the biggest obstacles on the road to quantum computers that are practically useful.

The method, called “directional tile codes,” can cut the logical error rate per computing round by up to 1,000 times compared with the widely used surface code – and at roughly the same cost, around 30 physical qubits per logical qubit. The code uses only the nearest-neighbour gates (iSWAP) already built into IQM’s Crystal processors. The work, published on arXiv, was carried out together with researchers at Freie Universität Berlin, the University of Edinburgh and the University of Mainz. IQM, which is planning a Nasdaq listing, is targeting fault-tolerant quantum computing by 2030 and a path toward a million qubits.

For the Nordics, quantum technology is a cutting-edge field. IQM is one of Europe’s leading quantum companies, and in Sweden the Wallenberg Centre for Quantum Technology (WACQT) at Chalmers is building the country’s own quantum computer under the leadership of Göran Johansson, while Denmark has the Niels Bohr Institute and large investments via the Novo Nordisk Foundation. Error correction is precisely where the field decides whether quantum computers become practically useful – and there, Nordic research is at the front.

Physical AI

Norway’s 1X starts mass production of the home robot NEO – but builds in the US

The Norwegian humanoid company 1X has begun large-scale production of its home robot NEO. The launch is a milestone in the race to bring general-purpose humanoids into ordinary homes.

1X, founded by Norwegian Bernt Børnich and backed by OpenAI as an investor, has opened a factory in Hayward, California, with capacity for around 10,000 robots a year and a stated goal of more than 100,000 units by 2027. NEO is meant to handle household chores – vacuuming, folding laundry, tidying and fetching things – and runs on Nvidia’s onboard Jetson Thor computer, which lets the robot perceive and make decisions in real time without leaning on the cloud. The first customer deliveries are expected during 2026, starting with a limited number of early users.

For the Nordics, the case puts a finger on a dilemma: one of the field’s hottest companies is Norwegian – but builds in California. World-class ideas, production and capital are often drawn abroad. At the same time, physical AI is advancing in Nordic industry: Swedish-Swiss ABB showed industrial humanoids at the Automate 2026 trade show, Denmark’s Universal Robots and MiR build collaborative robots, and Sintef and NTNU drive robotics research in Norway. The question is whether the region can keep value creation at home, or mostly becomes a buyer of others’ robots.
