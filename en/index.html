Models

Meta launches Muse Spark — first frontier model from Superintelligence Labs under Alexandr Wang

On April 8, Meta unveiled Muse Spark, the first model from Meta Superintelligence Labs since Alexandr Wang was recruited following the $14 billion Scale AI deal. The model — internally code-named "Avocado" — is the result of nine months of work and is described by Meta as a "ground-up overhaul" of the company's AI stack. On the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index v4.0, Muse Spark lands in fourth place with a score of 52, which Meta says dramatically narrows the gap to OpenAI, Anthropic and Google. The model is multimodal on input (text, voice, image) but produces text only, and an upcoming "Contemplating" mode will let multiple agents work in parallel on the same problem. Muse Spark is available immediately in the Meta AI app and on meta.ai, and will be rolled out across Facebook, Instagram and WhatsApp. Unlike the Llama series, Muse Spark is released as a closed model — Meta says it "hopes" to open future versions, breaking the openness stance that has been Meta's signature.

Source: Meta, Bloomberg, CNBC, TechCrunch, Axios
Regulation

OpenAI, Anthropic and Google form alliance against Chinese model copying — name DeepSeek, Moonshot and MiniMax

The three U.S. frontier labs began this week a formal information-sharing arrangement through the Frontier Model Forum to stop what they call "adversarial distillation" — when competitors copy frontier models by sending large volumes of queries and training on the answers. The companies specifically point to three Chinese actors: DeepSeek, Moonshot AI and MiniMax. Anthropic states that these have together generated more than 16 million queries against Claude through roughly 24,000 fraudulent accounts, in violation of the terms of service. The alliance is a significant step away from the previously cool competitive relationship between the labs and means that technical signatures of suspected distillation will now be shared between them. For European and Nordic AI users, the question becomes which regulatory framework can actually stop cross-border model copying — and what role EU AI Act enforcement will play when the dispute is U.S.-Chinese.

Source: Bloomberg, Japan Times, Incrypted
Research

AI accelerates quantum breakthrough — Oratomic shows utility-scale quantum computer can be built with 10,000 qubits

A research group around the startup Oratomic, with roots at Caltech, has used AI to find an algorithm that encodes a logical qubit in just three neutral atoms — a hundredfold reduction compared to previous estimates of 100–1,000 atoms per qubit. The conclusion, described in Nature and Time on April 7, is that an error-corrected and cryptographically relevant quantum computer can be built with as few as 10,000 atomic qubits instead of millions. The algorithm was developed using OpenEvolve, a tool that lets LLMs such as Google's Gemini and Anthropic's Claude evolve candidate solutions in a "natural selection" loop. The researchers explicitly say AI was decisive in combining results from narrow sub-disciplines into a new solution. For the security industry, the message is sharp: the timeline for "Q-day" — when today's RSA encryption can be broken — may have been considerably shortened, and post-quantum migration becomes urgent for Swedish and Norwegian authorities as well.

Source: Oratomic, Nature, HPCwire, Quantum Insider
Labour

AI scribes drive up U.S. health care costs — insurers and providers agree behind closed doors

STAT News reported on April 8 that U.S. health insurers and providers, behind closed doors, agree that so-called ambient AI scribes — which transcribe and code doctor visits automatically — increase total health care costs. Caroline Pearson, executive director of the Peterson Health Technology Institute, says investors, insurers and providers privately acknowledge that "scribes are increasing coding intensity." Publicly, the parties continue to talk past each other: providers highlight reduced burnout and fairer reimbursement, insurers warn of a cost spiral. Health economists call it an "AI coding arms race" — where scribes and autonomous coding tools maximize billable codes while insurers' algorithms try to minimize payouts. For Swedish and Norwegian healthcare administrators, it is an early warning signal of what happens when generative AI is introduced without clear reimbursement models.

Source: STAT News, STAT AI Prognosis, InsuranceNewsNet
Labour

Swedish government tasks Statistics Sweden with mapping how AI affects the job market for university graduates

The Swedish government announced this week that Statistics Sweden (SCB) will receive 2 million kronor in 2026 to analyze how artificial intelligence affects unemployment among university-educated workers. The background is that unemployment among graduates has risen over the past year and that several reports indicate that generative AI is replacing knowledge work faster than physical work — especially for junior roles in administration, law and communications. SCB is to map which professions and educational groups are affected and deliver evidence that can guide future labour-market and education policy. The assignment is one of the few concrete measurement initiatives in the government's AI strategy from February, and puts numbers on the gap that the debate about AI and jobs has so far mostly speculated about.

Source: Arbetsvärlden, Regeringen.se

Models

Anthropic launches Project Glasswing — Claude Mythos Preview finds thousands of zero-days but will not be released publicly

On April 7, Anthropic unveiled Project Glasswing, a cybersecurity initiative together with AWS, Apple, Broadcom, Cisco, CrowdStrike, Google, JPMorganChase, Microsoft and Nvidia, plus roughly forty organisations maintaining critical infrastructure. At its centre is Claude Mythos Preview — a frontier model that Anthropic says is "far ahead of any other AI model in cyber capabilities" and has already identified thousands of previously unknown zero-day vulnerabilities in every major operating system and web browser. Findings include a 27-year-old bug in OpenBSD and a 16-year-old bug in FFmpeg that automated testing missed after five million runs. On SWE-bench Verified, Mythos reaches 93.9 percent (versus 80.8 for Opus 4.6). The model will not be made generally available — Anthropic warns that coming models of the same class could exploit vulnerabilities faster than defenders can respond, and wants to develop safeguards before a broader release.

Source: Anthropic, Fortune, CNBC, TechCrunch
Debate

OpenAI proposes Public Wealth Fund, robot tax and 32-hour workweek in policy paper on the age of superintelligence

On April 6, OpenAI released a 13-page document titled "Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age: Ideas to Keep People First" — a proposal for how society should navigate the transition to superintelligence, presented as a starting point for discussion rather than finished recommendations. Concrete proposals include a Public Wealth Fund where governments and AI companies jointly invest in AI-driven growth and distribute returns to citizens, and a shift of the tax base from wages toward capital and corporate profits, including charges tied to the use of "automated workers". On labour, the paper proposes government-backed pilots of a 32-hour workweek without pay cuts, and portable benefit systems decoupled from individual employers. The document also calls for a new governance structure for frontier AI with incident reporting modelled on aviation safety and international coordination. Critics such as Gizmodo call the vision "vague" — the proposals are ambitious but lack funding details and concrete mandates.

Source: OpenAI, Bloomberg, Quartz, The Next Web
Labour

Automation Anywhere: AI agents resolve 80 percent of IT support tickets — saving large enterprises over 5 million dollars per year

Automation Anywhere reported on April 6 that AI agents now autonomously resolve more than 80 percent of employee IT support tickets on average, across the 70+ enterprise deployments the company analysed. According to the report, this can reduce IT Service Management licensing costs by up to 50 percent and save large enterprises more than 5 million dollars annually. Service desk call volumes drop by 50 percent and the first AI agents deliver value within eight weeks. The numbers come from the vendor itself and should be read cautiously, but they signal a structural shift: enterprise software pricing — for decades based on the number of user seats — is being challenged as agents take over ticket categories that previously required human handling. For Swedish IT departments and the BPO industry, it means the shift from "seat model" to "outcome model" is accelerating already in 2026.

Source: PR Newswire, TechEdge AI
Hardware

Anthropic locks in 3.5 gigawatts of Google TPU capacity via Broadcom — revenue run rate tops 30 billion dollars

On April 6, Anthropic, Google and Broadcom confirmed an expanded partnership that gives Anthropic access to roughly 3.5 gigawatts of new Google TPU capacity starting in 2027, on top of the one gigawatt already being delivered during 2026. Anthropic also disclosed that its annual revenue run rate now exceeds 30 billion dollars, up from around 9 billion at the end of 2025, and that more than a thousand enterprise customers pay over 1 million dollars a year for Claude services. Broadcom signed a separate long-term agreement with Google to design and supply future generations of custom TPU chips and networking components for Google's next-generation AI data racks through 2031. The deal ranks among the largest AI compute agreements to date and underscores how demand for frontier capacity has shifted the industry's centre of gravity toward multi-year chip and power contracts rather than traditional cloud service deals.

Source: Bloomberg, CNBC, TechCrunch, The Register
Models

Meta to partially open-source its next AI generation — internal codenames "Avocado" and "Mango"

According to an Axios report on April 6, Meta is preparing a hybrid strategy for its next AI generation: the company will open-source versions of upcoming frontier models while keeping the largest and most capable variants proprietary. Internally the models go by the codenames Avocado (a language model) and Mango (a multimedia generator). It is the first substantial signal of how Meta is shaping its AI effort under Alexandr Wang, the former Scale AI CEO recently recruited to lead the company's AI work. The decision is a compromise answer to months of speculation that Meta might abandon its open stance after Llama — and at the same time an attempt to match the pressure from Chinese open models such as DeepSeek V4 and Qwen. Critics warn, however, that "partial openness" risks diluting the trust Meta's earlier release policy built up within the research community.

Source: Axios, Gizmodo, SiliconANGLE, The Decoder

Models

DeepSeek V4 launches on Huawei Ascend — one trillion parameters, million-token context, 0.30 dollars per million tokens

Chinese DeepSeek rolls out V4, a Mixture-of-Experts model with approximately one trillion parameters and only 37 billion active per token. The model has a context window of 1 million tokens via a new "Engram" architecture for conditional memory management, reaching 97 per cent Needle-in-a-Haystack accuracy at million scale. Reuters confirmed on 4 April that V4 runs on Huawei's Ascend 950PR chip — the same silicon that ByteDance and Alibaba order. Pricing at 0.30 dollars per million tokens dramatically undercuts US frontier models and shows that export controls are driving forward a parallel Chinese AI ecosystem faster than Washington foresaw.

Source: NxCode, Dataconomy, Geeky Gadgets
Regulation

Utah expands AI prescription renewal to psychiatric pharmaceuticals — Legion Health gets green light in state sandbox

On 3 April, Utah's regulatory sandbox approved Legion Health's AI chatbot for independently renewing prescriptions for 15 low-risk psychiatric medications — an expansion of the pilot that began in January with Doctronic and 190 common drugs for chronic conditions. The programme is one year long and requires human physician oversight of the first 1,250 requests before broader rollout. Controlled substances, ADHD medications and injections remain exempt. Utah thus cements its position as the most experimental US state for AI in direct patient care — and the precedent will be scrutinised by every state considering similar sandboxes.

Source: WinBuzzer, Quarles & Brady, MobiHealthNews
Regulation

Four months to EU AI Act deadline — Digital Omnibus creates uncertainty about high-risk systems

On 2 August 2026, the main body of the EU AI Act comes into force: transparency requirements in Article 50 (marking AI-generated content and deepfakes), the fine regime and the requirement that every member state has at least one operational AI sandbox per Article 57. At the same time, trilog negotiations are ongoing on the Commission's "Digital Omnibus" package, which proposes that high-risk systems' obligations be conditional on harmonised technical standards being in place — which could push them to December 2027 or August 2028. For Swedish and Norwegian companies, the uncertainty means compliance work must be planned for both scenarios in parallel.

Source: Artificial Intelligence Act EU, Kennedys Law, Legal Nodes
Research

Neuro-symbolic hybrid cuts AI energy use 100x — and raises accuracy

A research group published on 5 April a study combining neural networks with human-like symbolic reasoning and reports up to 100-fold reductions in energy consumption alongside higher accuracy on multiple benchmarks. The result points to the straight scaling curve — more parameters and more compute — not being the only way forward. If the results hold in independent reproduction, it could change the economics of datacentres and make advanced inference possible on edge hardware. The work sits in the same wave as Google's TurboQuant: both seek efficiency in architecture rather than gross watts.

Source: ScienceDaily

Models

Google releases Gemma 4 under Apache 2.0 — open model ranks third globally

Google launched on 2 April Gemma 4, its most capable open model family to date — and for the first time under Apache 2.0 licence. The family spans four models: E2B and E4B for edge devices (phones, Raspberry Pi), a 26B Mixture-of-Experts and a 31B Dense ranking third on Arena AI's text leaderboard. With context windows up to 256K tokens, built-in image and audio understanding and support for 140+ languages, Gemma 4 targets agentic workflows and offline code generation. The E models activate only 2–4 billion parameters at inference, saving RAM and battery. The launch is a strategic response to Chinese open models like Qwen and DeepSeek.

Source: Google Blog, 9to5Google, The Register
Research

Google's TurboQuant compresses AI memory 6x without quality loss — presented at ICLR 2026

Google Research presents TurboQuant at ICLR 2026, a two-step algorithm that compresses the KV cache in large language models from 16 to 3 bits per value — a sixfold reduction without measurable quality loss. The first step, PolarQuant, rotates vectors with a random orthogonal transformation that evens out energy and removes outliers. The second step, Quantised Johnson-Lindenstrauss (QJL), reduces remaining residual error to a single sign bit per dimension. On Nvidia H100, 4-bit TurboQuant delivers 8x faster attention computation versus uncompressed 32-bit baselines. The implication: models with millions of tokens in context become practical to run without proportional GPU memory increase.

Source: Google Research, TechCrunch, BigDATAwire

California becomes de facto national AI standard — White House wants to stop state rules

California advances on multiple fronts as the USA's heaviest AI regulator. Newsom's executive order from 30 March, multiple ongoing bills on transparency, algorithmic discrimination and AI-generated content — and the state's size mean tech companies already treat California's rules as a national standard. Meanwhile, the Trump administration is driving a federal framework explicitly meant to replace state AI laws. The tension between state and federal level now defines the direction of American AI regulation. Colorado's AI discrimination law comes into force in June 2026, and a further 15 states have AI legislation under preparation.

DOJ appeals court ruling — Trump's ban on Anthropic in federal agencies continues

The Justice Department announced on 2 April that it is appealing federal judge Rita Lin's decision to block the Trump administration's ban on Anthropic's Claude in government systems. Models

Anthropic leaked Claude Code's entire source code via npm — supply chain attack followed within hours

A misconfigured npm release of Claude Code version 2.1.88 exposed nearly 2,000 TypeScript files and 512,000 lines of code — including LLM call logic, tool chains, feature flags for "persistent assistant" and remote control via phone. The repository got 84,000 stars on GitHub before Anthropic pulled the package and called the incident "a packaging mistake caused by human error". Worse: between 00:21 and 03:29 UTC on 31 March, a trojanised version of the HTTP client axios was distributed via the same update — a supply chain attack that hit everyone who installed Claude Code during that window. It is Anthropic's second security incident in a week, after the Mythos leak.

Source: Fortune, Bloomberg, Cybernews
Research

ICLR 2026: Web-CogReasoner shows AI agents navigating the web via pure pixel perception

Fotor's and several universities' joint research paper "Web-CogReasoner" was accepted as a conference contribution at ICLR 2026. The framework lets AI agents navigate webpages, desktop software and mobile apps by interpreting screen content visually — without relying on underlying HTML code. Using "Knowledge-driven Chain of Thought" and the Web-CogDataset (based on 14 real websites), the model shows stronger generalisation than code-dependent methods. The result points to a future where AI agents interact with digital systems the same way humans do: by seeing and reasoning.

Source: GlobeNewsWire, Encord (ICLR 2026 trends)
Labour

Sweden falls behind in AI race — only 37 per cent feel comfortable with the technology

EY's latest global survey shows that Swedish organisations have lower AI maturity and weaker leadership engagement than the Nordic average, despite ambitious national targets. Only 37 per cent of Swedes feel comfortable with AI (globally: 49 per cent), and 61 per cent say they have received no AI training in the past six months. Meanwhile, Statistics Sweden reports a shortage of over 61,000 qualified staff, and for the first time AI competence is harder to recruit than traditional IT skills. The gap between strategic ambition and operational reality is growing.

Source: EY Sverige, Dagens Teknik
Regulation

Newsom pushes back — California requires AI safety certification independent of Washington

California Governor Gavin Newsom signed on 30 March executive order N-5-26, which requires AI companies wanting to do business with the state to certify their safeguards against misuse, bias and violations of civil rights. The order establishes an AI governance council and requires state agencies to carry out security, privacy and bias audits in AI procurement. In a direct signal to the Trump administration, Newsom announced that California will henceforth independently assess federal risk classifications of AI companies — a clear response to the Pentagon's blacklisting of Anthropic.

Source: Governor's Office, CalMatters, Latham & Watkins

TRUMP AMERICA AI Act — concrete legislative proposal takes shape in Congress

Senator Marsha Blackburn (R-TN) presented on 18 March the draft discussion of the TRUMP AMERICA AI Act, the most concrete federal AI legislation to date. The proposal builds on the White House framework with seven pillars — child protection, intellectual property, data centre licensing, anti-censorship, innovation promotion, workforce training and federal right of first refusal against state laws. California's AI Transparency Act, which came into force 1 January, could become the first state law preempted if the proposal passes.

Source: KJK, Drata

About AI-skiftet

AI-skiftet is an independent essay project on how AI is changing work, industry, politics and society.

The perspective is deliberately industry-near. The texts do not primarily come from seminar rooms or general trend-spotting, but from work in sharp production environments, improvement projects and conversations about what the technology is actually changing when quality, economy and responsibility matter.

The ambition is twofold: to raise the level of the Swedish AI debate and to make the arguments more testable. Therefore the texts distinguish as far as possible between observation, inference, scenario and normative proposal, and each essay has its own source notes.

Rolf Skogling — mechanical engineer and consultant in industrial optimisation. Works with AI in sharp operations within Nordic and European manufacturing industry.
