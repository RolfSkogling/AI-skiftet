Regulation

EU trilogue concludes May 7 — Annex III pushed to December 2, 2027, "nudifier" apps banned December 2, 2026, Swedish MEP Arba Kokalari as co-rapporteur sets the Nordic stamp

On May 7, the European Parliament and the Council of the EU reached a provisional political agreement on the Digital Omnibus on AI after the third trilogue round under the Cypriot presidency — barely nine days after the second trilogue collapsed on April 28. The deal pushes the date of application for stand-alone high-risk systems under Annex III from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027, and AI embedded in regulated Annex I products (medical devices, machinery, toys, lifts, recreational craft) to August 2, 2028. At the same time, the grace period in Article 50(2) for transparency labelling of AI-generated content is shortened from six to three months, with the new deadline set at December 2, 2026. Most striking is the new Article 5 provision banning AI systems that generate non-consensual sexual or intimate content and CSAM — a measure on which Swedish MEP Arba Kokalari (EPP) served as co-rapporteur. "We do not have a list of nudifier apps, but we all know what Grok and X did a few months ago, and we want to send the message that all of this is banned in Europe," Kokalari said after the trilogue. The sandbox provisions are postponed to August 2, 2027. Formal adoption must be completed by both co-legislators before August 2, 2026 to avoid a legal vacuum. For Sweden, the deal has immediate effects on four fronts. For Klarna (Q1 report May 14), H&M's AI recruitment, Volvo Group's autonomous trucks and SEB's credit assessment, the implementation window for Annex III is extended into autumn 2027 — but the "nudifier" ban and watermarking requirements hit on December 2, 2026, in the middle of Q4. For IMY, the Swedish data protection authority must change its 2026 priorities: AI in the public sector (municipalities, regions, Försäkringskassan, Skatteverket) shifts from emergency to structural work, and resources can instead be directed at the CSAM and deepfake track where Swedish child rights law already has sharp requirements via the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child. For Norway's Datatilsynet and Nkom, the deal creates a six-month planning period to sync the KI-loven (out for consultation since 2025) against the EU threshold before KI Norge is established under its new director in August. And for Wallenberg AI Factory, Sferical AI, the Vinnova project at Chalmers (compliance AI) and RISE, the 18-month window becomes a concrete business opportunity for standardised Annex III documentation that has become the European de facto template.

On May 7, the Norwegian Ministry of Digitalisation and Public Administration appointed Hans Christian Holte as the first director of KI Norge — the national organisation launched by the government last year with NOK 10 million in establishment funding and placed within the Norwegian Digitalisation Agency (Digdir). The appointment comes in a hectic week for Norwegian AI policy: on May 7 the EU trilogue reached its agreement on the Digital Omnibus, the December 2025 NHO industrial AI declaration is to be operationalised ahead of the Storting's revised state budget, and Sintef's AID initiative (NTNU/SINTEF) is seeking long-term funding in parallel with Vinnova's NOK 350 million call on the Swedish side. For Norway, the appointment is immediately significant: Holte's NAV background — where he handled the EFTA Court ruling of 2021 on automated decisions and the right to manual review — makes him an operational force for Annex III conformity assessment in the public sector now that the high-risk threshold has been pushed to December 2, 2027 by the EU deal. For Datatilsynet, Nkom, Helsedirektoratet, the Norwegian Armed Forces and municipalities such as Trondheim, Bergen and Oslo, Holte provides a clearer interface partner than the previously fragmented framework. For Equinor, DNB, Telenor, Yara, Aker BP, Kongsberg and Hydro — all running AI pilot programmes on Microsoft Azure, AWS and Google Cloud — KI Norge under Holte becomes the first national negotiating counterparty for sovereign-stack questions. For Sweden, the parallel is striking: AI Sweden under Magnus Lundgren and WASP under Sara Mazur do not have a corresponding regulatory mandate, which makes the Holte model an interesting reference if the Swedish Ministry of Climate and Enterprise eventually considered a Sferical/Sweden AI coordination centre with a clear public-authority mandate. And for WASP-HS's humanities track and Karolinska's new Chief AI Officer role, a concrete Nordic conversation partner now exists for AI governance in the welfare sector.

Anthropic introduces "dreaming" for Claude Managed Agents on May 6 — agents consolidate memory between sessions, Harvey sees 6x higher task completion, persistent memory in public beta

On May 6, Anthropic introduced a research-preview feature called "dreaming" for Claude Managed Agents — a scheduled memory-curation process that, between sessions, reviews the agent's logs, merges duplicates, removes outdated entries, and distils patterns (recurring mistakes, shared team preferences) into plain-text notes and structured "playbooks." The feature does not modify the model weights — it builds an external long-term memory on top of Claude. Users decide on the level of control: dreaming can update memory automatically or require manual review. Legal-AI company Harvey reported a 6x increase in task completion after implementation. At the same time, persistent memory on Managed Agents became generally available in public beta on the Claude Platform, and Anthropic also updated the agents with Lazy Loading and Auto-Prompting. The rollout frames a hectic week: Anthropic–Microsoft 365 finance agents on May 5, the Anthropic–SpaceX Colossus 1 deal on May 6, the Pentagon exclusion still in place from May 1, the CAISI evaluation agreements of May 5, and the EU AI Act trilogue concluded on May 7. For the Nordics, the dreaming feature shifts two concrete debates immediately. For Sweden's Wallenberg AI Factory, WASP-HS and RISE — which run memory- and continuity-research within WARA AI-TRICS and AI for Science — dreaming offers an empirical testbed for exactly what Rolf Skogling's essay "Minnet som saknas" (April 17, 2026) flagged as Claude Opus 4.7's weakness: the lack of persistent contextual memory between sessions. For Klarna (customer service assistant), H&M (recruitment), SEB (M&A), Volvo Group, Karolinska University Hospital (with the I-AID platform) and DNB, the 6x productivity boost becomes immediately operationally critical: accumulated workflows and organisation-specific preferences can now be capitalised on without retraining the model. For IMY and Datatilsynet, the dreaming architecture also raises a new concrete supervisory question ahead of the December 2, 2027 threshold (after the EU deal of May 7): how is memory the agent itself curates documented, and what role does such self-modification play in Annex III risk assessment? And for Lovable (Stockholm), Sana Labs and Sferical AI, a concrete API surface opens up where Swedish-Norwegian builder products can access agent memory on the Claude Platform without having to train their own models.

About AI-skiftet

AI-skiftet is an independent essay project on how AI is changing work, industry, politics and society.

The perspective is deliberately industry-near. The texts do not primarily come from seminar rooms or general trend-spotting, but from work in sharp production environments, improvement projects and conversations about what the technology is actually changing when quality, economy and responsibility matter.

The ambition is twofold: to raise the level of the Swedish AI debate and to make the arguments more testable. Therefore the texts distinguish as far as possible between observation, inference, scenario and normative proposal, and each essay has its own source notes.

Rolf Skogling — mechanical engineer and consultant in industrial optimisation. Works with AI in sharp operations within Nordic and European manufacturing industry.
