Models

Apple launches Gemini-powered Siri – but shuts out EU iPhone users

On June 8 at WWDC, Apple unveiled a completely rebuilt assistant, Siri AI, built on a new in-house foundation model developed together with Google. According to multiple reports, Siri runs on a custom Gemini model under a multi-year deal worth around one billion dollars a year.

At the same time, Apple is opening the platform: with iOS 27 Extensions, users can choose Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini or Grok as their preferred AI in Siri, Writing Tools and Image Playground. Apple thus abandons its previous single-provider model, lets different models have their own voices, and rolls everything out with iOS, iPadOS and macOS 27 in September. Claude and Gemini become the first third-party partners alongside ChatGPT.

For the Nordics, the catch is significant: Apple says that because of the EU's Digital Markets Act it cannot ship Siri AI on iPhone and iPad in the union at launch. Swedish, Danish and Finnish iPhone users will therefore go without the feature, while it is available on Mac, Apple Watch and Vision Pro. For Norwegian users the situation is unclear, since Norway sits outside the EU but often follows via the EEA. The question affects both consumers and Nordic developers building on Apple Intelligence, and again highlights how the DMA and the AI Act shape which AI services Nordic citizens actually get access to.

Source: Apple Newsroom, MacRumors, TechCrunch
Debate

Pentagon wants to replace Claude – Anthropic refused to drop its safety guardrails

The US Department of Defense is now testing models from OpenAI, Google and xAI's Grok to replace Anthropic's Claude, which has been the primary AI in the Pentagon's classified systems and part of the Maven Smart System analysis platform.

The dispute stems from two lines Anthropic has held since its founding: no mass surveillance of Americans and no fully autonomous weapons that attack people without human control. After Anthropic refused to remove the guardrails, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth declared the company a supply-chain risk, and the department had about 25 "power users" begin evaluating the competitors. Anthropic is challenging the decision in court and warns of losses in the billions.

For the Nordics, the question is highly concrete. Saab and Kongsberg are building AI into their systems, and agencies such as FMV, the Swedish Armed Forces and Norway's Forsvaret are buying ever more AI amid the NATO build-up. The case shows how a vendor's ethical guardrails can collide with military demands, and raises the question of how dependent the Nordic defense sector dares to become on US AI models whose terms can change overnight.

Source: Scientific American, Bloomberg, TechTimes
Hardware

Shortage of AI memory and TSMC capacity is slowing the entire AI build-out

Broadcom warns that the limited capacity at contract manufacturer TSMC is holding back the supply of AI accelerators into 2026. TSMC's CEO has said supply is not expected to meet demand until 2027.

The bottleneck is above all memory: HBM, the fast high-bandwidth memory that AI chips need. Samsung, SK Hynix and Micron, which together control more than 95 percent of the world's DRAM production, have shifted capacity to HBM, and prices have, according to industry estimates, risen sharply – some increases of more than 200 percent since early 2025. On top of that, TSMC's advanced packaging, CoWoS, is a second bottleneck.

For the Nordics, the region's AI ambitions rest on the same scarce chips. Telenor's AI factory in Norway, Ericsson, Volvo and the new Nordic data centers all compete for GPUs and HBM. When supply tightens, both Swedish sovereignty efforts – such as the Berzelius supercomputer in Linköping and the national language model – and industry roll-outs risk higher costs and longer lead times. Those who plan compute early win.

Source: Astute Group, Tom’s Hardware
Research

Vinnova invests 350 million to transform Swedish industry with AI

In late May, Sweden's innovation agency Vinnova decided to invest 350 million kronor in six projects in which advanced digital technology and AI are to strengthen Swedish industry and society. The projects tackle challenges such as energy efficiency, climate benefits in the forestry industry and secure digital decision support.

The initiative is part of the Advanced Digitalisation partnership, a collaboration since 2021 between the state and industry in which Vinnova finances up to half of the budget. Industry is represented by ABB, Ericsson, Saab and Teknikföretagen, and among the projects is an Expert Learning Lab run by ABB, Ericsson, Saab and the Volvo Group together with Chalmers, KTH, Linköping University and Örebro University.

For the Nordics, it is a concrete example of how the state, industry and academia can share the bill to keep regional industry competitive as AI moves from pilot to production. The model is directly relevant to jobs and skills in Swedish industrial regions, and resembles the choices facing Norway's NHO, the Research Council and NTNU, as well as Danish counterparts, as AI is to deliver measurable value in manufacturing.

Physical AI

Norway’s 1X opens its own lab for robot world models

1X, the Norwegian-American humanoid company founded by Bernt Børnich, opened the 1X World Model Lab on June 5. The unit will train large-scale world models so that its home robot Neo can understand, predict and act in new environments without task-specific training.

The lab is led by Sam Sinha, a former researcher at Luma AI, and will pretrain on a mix of internet data, first-person video, simulation, teleoperated robot data and real-world data from Neo in people’s homes. Børnich says the fastest path to full autonomy means owning the entire stack, down to your own video foundation models. In parallel, Nvidia is pushing the same track with its next-generation robot foundation model GR00T N2, built on a world-action-model architecture.

For the Nordics, this shifts the contest from hardware to data and talent. That a company with manufacturing in Moss is building its own world-model lab strengthens the region’s standing in physical AI, where Sintef and NTNU research autonomous systems and Sweden’s ABB Robotics and Denmark’s Universal Robots hold large volumes of industrial motion data. At the same time, demand is rising for the ML talent that WASP and Nordic universities train.

Source: 1X, AI Insider, NVIDIA Newsroom
Labor Market

Strong jobs report masks AI’s quiet pressure on entry-level work

The U.S. labour market added 172,000 jobs in May, almost three times more than expected. But on June 5 analysts warned that the strong headline masks a split: AI-driven displacement is accelerating in high-skill occupations while hiring is weakening, especially for young people and entry-level roles.

According to outplacement firm Challenger, Gray & Christmas, about 50,000 of this year’s roughly 300,000 U.S. layoffs can be linked to AI. Goldman Sachs estimates that AI has cut monthly payroll growth by around 16,000 jobs over the past year. Recently Intuit cut 17 percent of its staff, Meta began laying off 8,000 employees and Cisco announced thousands of cuts – all citing AI investments.

The Nordics face the same wave but with a different model. AI Sweden has gathered unions and employers – including Unionen – in a national Labor Market AI Council, and Deloitte’s State of AI in the Nordics 2026 survey shows that only 45 percent of Nordic employers expect major automation within the next decade, against 65 percent globally. The difference is tied to strong collective agreements, high union membership and social dialogue in Sweden, Norway, Denmark and Finland – a model that Sweden’s LO and TCO and Norway’s LO and NHO lean on as they manage reskilling.

Source: Fortune, CBS News, FAIR/Deloitte
Debate

AI chiefs join forces: tighten controls on synthetic DNA

The heads of OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind and Microsoft AI – Sam Altman, Dario Amodei, Demis Hassabis and Mustafa Suleyman – signed a joint open letter to the U.S. Congress on June 5. The message: AI is lowering the barrier to misusing biological material, and controls on synthetic DNA must be tightened.

The rivals propose mandatory screening of every DNA and RNA synthesis order against databases of dangerous sequences, verification of every customer, and recordkeeping of orders and specifications. The letter backs a bipartisan Senate bill that would turn today’s voluntary industry practice into law.

Rivals joining forces raises the pressure beyond the U.S. as well. Nordic life science – SciLifeLab, Karolinska, KTH and Norway’s UiO and NTNU – routinely buys synthetic DNA, and biosecurity touches agencies such as Sweden’s FOI, MSB and the Public Health Agency, along with the EU’s work on dual-use. The question is how the Nordics coordinate supplier requirements without slowing legitimate research.

Source: Fortune, GEN
Research

Stockholm opens an AI sandbox as a testbed for the smart city

Stockholm Digital Sandbox was inaugurated in late May as a shared test and innovation environment where researchers, the city and industry will explore how AI can make the city smarter, safer and more sustainable. At its core is the sharing of data between development projects.

The initiative is run by the research centre Digital Futures – established by KTH, Stockholm University and RISE – with partners including Region Stockholm, Ericsson, Saab, AstraZeneca, Hitachi, Skanska, Traton and Xylem. A first testbed sits on Hornsgatan, where connected traffic signals and cameras measure vehicle type, speed and lane, and from this autumn also listen for studded tyres to steer costly particle-control measures.

“We want to move from opinion to knowledge,” the city’s coordinator Anders Broberg sums up. For the Nordics it is a concrete example of how the public sector, academia and industry can share data in a real environment – a model that cities such as Gothenburg, Oslo and Copenhagen could follow as AI is meant to deliver measurable public value rather than pilot demonstrations.

Source: Dagens Infrastruktur, Digital Futures

Claude outage shows how fragile scheduled AI workflows already are

Anthropic's status page recorded degraded performance for multiple Claude models on Sunday morning. The incident was first investigated at 03:31 UTC, 05:31 in Sweden, the cause was identified ten minutes later, and the issue was marked resolved at 04:28 UTC.

Afterwards the same page showed claude.ai, Console, API, Claude Code and Claude Cowork as operational, but the operational lesson is sharper: scheduled AI workflows can fail quietly when a model or platform becomes unavailable. A green vendor page is not enough; every job needs its own timestamps, exit codes and fallback path.

For Nordic organizations, LLM availability should now be treated like any other critical supplier risk. Teams need to know whether a content flow is actually today's, whether it is reusing yesterday's data, and whether audio, translation or publication is blocked by an earlier step.

Infrastructure

Anthropic expands Project Glasswing as AI cyber becomes critical infrastructure

Anthropic has expanded Project Glasswing from its first group of roughly 50 partners to about 150 new organizations in more than fifteen countries. After meeting security requirements, they gain access to Claude Mythos Preview to scan important codebases for vulnerabilities.

The program now targets power, water, healthcare, communications, hardware and vendors that maintain software many others depend on. Anthropic says the initial partners have already found more than 10,000 high- or critical-severity flaws.

The important signal is the constraint: Anthropic wants to release Mythos-level capabilities more widely over time, but says robust misuse safeguards have not yet been built. The race is therefore not only about stronger cyber models; it is about verification, responsible disclosure and faster patching capacity.

OpenAI wants CAISI to become the US hub for frontier safety

OpenAI published a blueprint on June 3 for democratic governance of frontier AI in the United States. Its core proposal is a federal framework that builds on emerging state AI safety laws, strengthens CAISI as the federal government's central institution for frontier safety, and links AI risk to broader national resilience.

The document is notable because it treats AI governance as technical infrastructure rather than a one-off license or pre-approval process. The system should evolve with model capabilities while helping democratic countries stay coordinated as labs move faster than legislation.

For Europe and the Nordics, the contrast is clear. The US is debating a federal technical hub for frontier risks, while the EU is building enforcement through the AI Office, the AI Act and expert panels. Companies using or selling advanced models will likely need to understand both paths.

Regulation

The EU AI Act now has an expert panel for general-purpose models

The European Commission has appointed a Scientific Panel and an Advisory Forum to support implementation and enforcement of the AI Act. The Scientific Panel brings together 60 independent experts appointed for two-year terms to work with the AI Office and national authorities.

Its mandate focuses on general-purpose AI: systemic risks, model classification, evaluation methods, market surveillance and technical issues such as cyber risk, capability measurement and mitigations. The list includes Nordic names such as Markus Anderljung, Fredrik Heintz, Hjalmar Wijk and Julia Mykhailiuk.

This is where the AI Act becomes more than legal text. Providers of advanced models will face questions about evidence, testing, incidents and technical safeguards, not just policy language.

Quantum

Quantinuum's billion-dollar IPO is the largest quantum listing yet

Honeywell-backed quantum company Quantinuum began trading on the Nasdaq under the ticker QNT on June 4. The company priced its shares at $60 and raised about $1.68 billion – the largest IPO to date for a pure-play quantum computing firm.

The price implied a market value of roughly $14 billion, and the stock closed broadly flat on its debut at around $15.7 billion. That is more than 450 times last year's revenue of just under $31 million, and the company reports heavy losses – making the listing a test of how much investors will pay for the promise of fault-tolerant quantum computing. Quantinuum's technology relies on trapped ions, and in the same week it signed a memorandum of understanding with Mitsubishi Electric to bring quantum computation into industrial design.

For the Nordics, the listing sharpens the contrast between market-driven and publicly funded quantum efforts. Chalmers' WACQT – the Wallenberg Centre for Quantum Technology – is building Sweden's own quantum computer under Göran Johansson, scaling from 25 toward 40 qubits this year and opening more broadly for algorithm testing in 2026, with a target of 100 qubits by 2029. Finland's Aalto has unveiled the 20-qubit AaltoQ20 and IQM is pushing commercially, while Sweden and the United States signed a quantum cooperation memorandum in Helsingborg in May. As quantum capital now flows to the stock market, the question is whether WASP, KAW and Nordic universities can keep pace without the same risk-tolerant money.

Regulation

US Congress wants to freeze state AI laws for three years

Two members of Congress – Republican Jay Obernolte and Democrat Lori Trahan – released a 269-page draft of the Great American Artificial Intelligence Act late on June 4, the most comprehensive federal AI framework put forward in the US so far.

Its most contested provision is a three-year freeze on state AI laws governing the development of frontier models – which would suspend both California's rules and Colorado's AI Act, whose start was recently pushed back to January 2027. In return, companies with more than $500 million in revenue would have to publish safety frameworks, report serious incidents and admit auditors. The labor federation AFL-CIO rejected it outright, calling the bill a giveaway to the AI industry, while industry groups welcomed it.

For the Nordics, the contrast with Europe grows starker. While the US leans toward deregulation, the EU's AI Act is tightening in stages, with the next step in August, and Norway is aiming for its own AI law to take effect in late summer 2026. For Sweden's IMY and Norway's Datatilsynet, and for Nordic companies such as Ericsson, Spotify and DNB operating in the US, it means navigating two increasingly divergent regulatory worlds at once – where what is permitted on one side of the Atlantic may be prohibited on the other.

Source: Roll Call, FedScoop, Obernolte (US House)
Models

ChatGPT starts remembering you automatically – and raises privacy questions

On June 4 OpenAI began rolling out Dreaming V3, its biggest memory upgrade since ChatGPT launched. The new architecture automatically builds a profile of the user – preferences, ongoing projects and time-sensitive context – without anyone having to ask the assistant to remember anything.

Unlike the old system, which waited for explicit instructions, Dreaming V3 runs a background process after each conversation and also understands when information has become outdated. OpenAI says the synthesis requires about five times less compute, which means the feature is also set to roll out to free users. At the same time, a February study found that 96 percent of ChatGPT's saved memories in one sample were created by the system itself, without the user asking.

For the Nordics, the feature lands in the middle of a sensitive privacy question. From August the EU's AI Act begins imposing transparency requirements, and both Sweden's IMY and Norway's Datatilsynet are already scrutinizing automated profiling under the GDPR. For the Nordic public sector – schools, healthcare and agencies testing AI assistants – a system that quietly builds behavioral profiles of citizens and pupils raises the question of where the line runs between helpful memory and surveillance.

Source: OpenAI, Engadget
Infrastructure

Sweden's cloud policy and homegrown AI clouds aim to cut reliance on US giants

While the US leans toward deregulation and the American AI giants keep growing, Sweden is pursuing its own line on digital sovereignty. In late May the government adopted the country's first cloud policy for public administration, intended to strengthen security and reduce dependence on suppliers outside the EU.

The policy does not ban American cloud services, but it requires clearer risk assessments, open standards and plans for switching providers – and the Swedish Post and Telecom Authority (PTS) is tasked with guiding agencies, municipalities and regions. In parallel, Swedish challengers are growing: Stockholm-based Berget AI runs open language models on fossil-free infrastructure where data never leaves Sweden, with the Riksbank as its flagship customer under the motto “the mountain rather than the cloud”.

For the Nordics, this is a shared concern. Norway has already moved data centers to domestic operators and is building hydropower-driven AI halls, and both Datatilsynet and Sweden's IMY are pressing for control over where sensitive data ends up. As Brussels pushes European digital sovereignty and Washington pulls the other way, Nordic electricity, land and trust become a concrete competitive advantage – if the region can build its own clouds faster than the dependence grows.

Models

Google releases Gemma 4 12B — multimodal AI that fits on a 16 GB laptop

Google DeepMind released Gemma 4 12B on June 4, an open multimodal model that runs locally on a standard 16 GB enterprise laptop. The release ships under an Apache 2.0 license and is paired, for the first time, with downloadable macOS desktop applications from Google.

The roughly 12-billion-parameter model uses an encoder-free architecture: images, audio and text feed straight into the language-model backbone instead of passing through heavy separate encoders, which cuts memory and latency. The context window is 256,000 tokens and the model natively ingests speech, allowing Gemma 4 12B to nearly match its twice-as-large 26B sibling on benchmarks. The target audience is developers building agent workflows with speech, screenshots, code and tool calls without sending data to the cloud.

For the Nordics, the release lowers the bar for running modern AI on a private machine — a fit for Telenor's Sovereign AI factory in Norway, Wallenberg-backed Sferical AI in Linköping and the Royal Library's Swedish language-model project, where data protection and national sovereignty are core constraints. Swedish and Norwegian agencies, as well as small and mid-sized industrial firms such as Tomra and Vestre, gain an alternative to hyperscaler APIs when sensitive data must stay on premises.

Source: Google Developers Blog, VentureBeat, The Decoder
Regulation

Trump signs AI cybersecurity order — asks labs to submit frontier models for voluntary federal review

President Donald Trump signed the executive order Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security on June 2. It asks U.S. AI companies to voluntarily submit their most powerful models for federal evaluation for up to 30 days before public release.

The order gives federal agencies 60 days to develop a classified benchmark for measuring advanced cyber capabilities and to set the threshold at which a system counts as a "covered frontier model". Treasury, the National Security Agency and CISA must stand up an AI Cybersecurity Clearinghouse within 30 days to coordinate the discovery, validation and remediation of software vulnerabilities. An earlier draft from May had a 90-day review window, trimmed to 30 days after warnings that it would slow U.S. labs against China. The text explicitly rules out any mandatory licensing regime.

For the Nordics, the friction is concrete. Swedish and Norwegian users of OpenAI frontier models through Microsoft Azure and AWS Bedrock — SEB, Telenor, DNB and Equinor among them — must now plan for the heaviest releases potentially being delayed by a month. At the same time, pressure rises on Europe's path: while the United States picks voluntary review, the EU AI Act's Article 55 makes safety testing mandatory, with Norway's Nkom and Sweden's IMY preparing to take on supervisory roles.

Source: The White House, CNBC, NPR

Anthropic formalizes its consulting market — Services Track splits Claude partners into three tiers

Anthropic launched a Services Track and a Partner Hub inside the Claude Partner Network on June 3 — a structured tier ladder for consulting and systems-integration firms that deploy Claude for enterprise customers, plus a public directory for buyers.

Since the partner programme launched in March 2026 with a $100 million commitment, more than 40,000 firms have applied and over 10,000 consultants have earned individual Claude certifications. The three tiers — Select, Preferred and Global Premier — each require progressively more active certified individuals, joint production customers and public case studies; Global Premier alone demands at least 1,000 certified consultants and 100 joint deployments across three regions. A new MCP connector lets partners query their tier status directly from Claude.

For the Nordics, the impact is direct. With promotion reviews on January 1 and July 1 — plus an extra round on October 1, 2026 — the region's consulting leaders must decide quickly whether to climb the ladder or specialize.

Research

Royal Library final report: Swedish public sector has a major need for adapted Swedish language models

Sweden's National Library (Kungliga biblioteket, KB) delivered its final report to the government on June 1 on the use of language models in the public sector. The conclusion is that demand for tailored Swedish models is large — and that a national, secure compute environment must be built quickly if Sweden is to capture the value.

The report proposes that publicly funded Swedish language models be open and well documented, that a national digitization push target collections of particular relevance for model training and that KB serve as advisor while a secure compute environment accounting for national security needs is built. KB argues the models can streamline work processes, strengthen information retrieval and ease analysis inside agencies — but only if backed by long-term focus, transparency and cooperation.

For the Nordics, this is a flagship regional story. The final report arrives only days after the government, in a press conference with Prime Minister Ulf Kristersson, formally launched the large-scale Swedish language model alongside the Wallenberg foundations and the Berzelius supercomputer at Linköping University. Norwegian counterparts in the NORA.LLM consortium, which brings together eight Norwegian universities around a parallel effort, are watching the KB model closely: if Sweden lands a public, open state model, one of the main excuses for Norway not to follow disappears.

Models

Microsoft launches seven of its own AI models to cut its reliance on OpenAI

At its Build developer conference on June 2, Microsoft unveiled seven in-house AI models under the MAI name. Leading the lineup is MAI-Thinking-1, the company’s first home-grown reasoning model.

MAI-Thinking-1 was trained from scratch on commercially licensed data – with no distillation from OpenAI’s models – and is described as a mid-sized model with about 35 billion active parameters (roughly one trillion in total in a sparse mixture-of-experts) and a 256,000-token context window, enough to take in a 600-page document in one pass. The family also includes the coding model MAI-Code-1-Flash, now rolling out in GitHub Copilot, the image model MAI-Image-2.5 and the speech models MAI-Voice-2 and MAI-Transcribe-1.5, which together cover more than 40 languages. Microsoft’s stated aim is to lower costs for developers and reduce its dependence on OpenAI.

For the Nordics, the launch sharpens both the cost and the sovereignty question. Cheaper models on Azure benefit big Swedish and Norwegian users such as SEB, DNB, Ericsson and Telenor – while raising the pressure on the region’s own efforts, including Telenor’s Sovereign AI Factory and the Wallenberg-owned Sferical AI in Linköping. Broader language support in the MAI models may eventually improve Swedish and Norwegian coverage, but also makes Microsoft a stronger rival in the contest over Nordic language technology.

Quantum

IBM puts more than 10 billion dollars into quantum computing, targeting fault tolerance by 2029

IBM said on June 2 that it will invest more than 10 billion dollars in quantum technology over the next five years. The money is meant to speed the path toward what IBM calls the world’s first large-scale, fault-tolerant quantum computer, planned for 2029.

The investment spans research, manufacturing, partnerships and acquisitions, and rests, the company says, on the industry’s broadest quantum base: the world’s largest fleet of quantum computers and a network of more than 340 organizations already running real workloads. The push is also a statement in the geopolitical race, with IBM stressing US leadership.

The Niels Bohr Institute in Copenhagen and NTNU in Trondheim are also watching how fast fault-tolerant systems reach researchers, and the Wallenberg Foundations that fund WACQT are weighing IBM’s pace into their own quantum bets.

Physical AI

Norway-founded 1X starts delivering its NEO home robot as humanoids reach ordinary homes

2026 is shaping up as the year humanoid robots begin moving into ordinary homes, and out front is 1X with its NEO – a robot built for chores such as laundry and tidying. The company was founded in Norway and still manufactures in Moss.

NEO weighs around 30 kilograms, has roughly four hours of battery life and sells for 20,000 dollars or 499 dollars a month. The entire first year of production sold out within five days of pre-orders opening in the autumn of 2025, and the first deliveries are happening in the United States during 2026 – with Europe, Japan and South Korea following from 2027. 1X was founded in 2014 by roboticist Bernt Øivind Børnich, is backed by investors including the OpenAI Startup Fund and Sweden’s EQT Ventures, and is building a California factory aiming for 100,000 robots a year by the end of 2027.

For the Nordics, NEO is one of the rare cases of a Nordic company leading a global technology race. At the same time, a camera- and microphone-equipped robot in the home raises sharp privacy questions that will land on the desks of Norway’s Datatilsynet and Sweden’s IMY, and it challenges the region’s industrial-robot heavyweights – ABB in Västerås and Denmark’s Universal Robots and MiR – to move their strength from the factory floor into new uses. For Nordic consumers, though, deliveries will not arrive until 2027.

Sweden names a national coordinator to drive a homegrown Swedish language model

In May the Swedish government appointed Katrin Westling Palm as national coordinator, tasked with driving the development of high-quality Swedish language models – models that reflect Swedish culture, society and law.

Westling Palm, formerly director general of the Swedish Tax Agency, is to map what the work requires in terms of data access and to analyze how rights holders can be compensated when copyrighted material is used in training. The model itself is being built with funding from the Wallenberg Foundations and computing power from the Berzelius supercomputer at Linköping University, and is being trained during 2026 on data from public bodies together with Swedish literature and news media. The effort is part of the national AI strategy the government presented in February.

For the Nordics it is a clear move toward sovereignty: rather than leaning on American models, Sweden wants to own technology that understands Swedish and the Swedish context. The step mirrors Norwegian ambitions around homegrown language technology and AI Norway, and the question of compensating media and authors will set a precedent for Norwegian rights holders too. For schools, healthcare and public administration across the Nordics, it is about whose values and language are built into the AI systems citizens will soon meet.

Nvidia moves agentic AI onto the PC with a new superchip at Computex

Nvidia opened Computex in Taipei on June 1. In his keynote, CEO Jensen Huang unveiled a new superchip for personal computers, RTX Spark, and the company’s largest open-weights language model to date, Nemotron 3 Ultra.

RTX Spark pairs an Arm processor with Blackwell graphics and 128 GB of unified memory, and according to Nvidia turns Windows into an “agentic” operating system with powerful AI right on the desktop – with Microsoft, Dell, HP and Lenovo among the first manufacturers. Nemotron 3 Ultra holds around 550 billion parameters (about 55 billion active per call), is described by the independent benchmarker Artificial Analysis as the sharpest US open-weights model and is said by Nvidia to run around five times faster at roughly 30 % lower cost. Nvidia also showed the open physical-AI model Cosmos 3 and the DGX Station workstation.

For the Nordics, cheap AI right on the machine and a powerful open model lower the barrier for Swedish and Norwegian companies. AI on your own hardware keeps sensitive data local – an argument in the sovereignty and GDPR debate that IMY and Datatilsynet are driving – while an open Nemotron gives AI Sweden, the language-model project around the Berzelius supercomputer in Linköping and startups such as Lovable and Legora a free foundation to build on.

EU clarifies which AI systems count as high-risk – consultation open until June 23

The European Commission on May 19 published draft guidelines to determine which AI systems are classified as high-risk under Article 6 and Annex III of the AI Act. The proposal is open for consultation until June 23.

The guidelines give practical examples of what falls “in” or “out” of the high-risk category in areas such as biometrics, education, employment, essential services and law enforcement. One key point: modular and agent-driven systems cannot be carved up to escape the rules – if linked components jointly serve a high-risk purpose, the whole chain is treated as one system. Following the provisional Digital Omnibus agreement, the requirements apply from December 2, 2027 for stand-alone systems and August 2, 2028 for AI embedded in products.

For the Nordics, the classification verdict forces homework right now. Sweden’s IMY and PTS – proposed in SOU 2025:101 to become the coordinating supervisory authority – along with Norway’s Datatilsynet and Nkom must tame the framework within the EEA, while banks such as SEB and DNB, hospitals such as Karolinska and Helse-Norge and agencies such as Försäkringskassan and NAV must now classify their own AI systems before the consultation closes.

Three months to the election – how Sweden is bracing against AI-driven disinformation

On September 13 Sweden holds its general election, and the question of AI-generated disinformation has moved up the agenda. The government is running the “Protecting the 2026 elections” programme, and the Swedish Psychological Defence Agency (MPF) has been given a key role.

MPF is analysing lessons from last year’s European elections to detect influence attempts early, and PTS and MPF maintain an ongoing dialogue with the platforms on how false content can be slowed. The Swedish Agency for the Media has also been tasked with broadly raising both media and AI literacy among the public. The backdrop is that deepfakes took a clear step up in quality during 2026 – and as recently as late May, OpenAI said it was bracing ChatGPT for this year’s elections worldwide.

For the Nordics, the Swedish election is a sharp test with wider implications. Norway, which held its general election in autumn 2025, and the other neighbours are watching how the EU’s platform rules (DSA) and labelling requirements hold up against synthetic content. The Election Authority, MSB and the Swedish parties are in the middle of a trade-off between freedom of expression and protection from interference that touches every voter.

AI agents are being wired in faster than security can keep up

Even as companies wire autonomous AI agents into their systems, fresh industry surveys warn that security is lagging behind. In one survey by the security firm Gravitee, 88 % of organisations say they have had confirmed or suspected security incidents linked to AI agents over the past year.

In a separate survey by the Cloud Security Alliance and Darktrace, 92 % of security professionals say they are concerned about the agents’ impact. Gravitee reports that more than a third of organisations have suffered operational disruptions caused by agents, and that only about 14 % say all their agents went live with full security review – even as Gartner predicts that up to 40 % of enterprise apps will have built-in agents by year’s end. The figures come from vendor-led surveys (Gravitee, Darktrace) with a commercial interest in the field and should be read as indicative rather than independent statistics, but the pattern is unambiguous: adoption is outrunning control.

For the Nordics this is highly concrete. Banks such as SEB, DNB and Nordea and agencies such as Försäkringskassan, Skatteverket and NAV are now rolling out agent-driven workflows, putting pressure on MSB, Datatilsynet and security chiefs to give agents their own identities and clear governance. It also ties into the EU’s fresh high-risk guidelines, which single out agent chains, and to the NIS2 requirements on Nordic critical infrastructure.

Source: Cloud Security Alliance, Darktrace, Gravitee
