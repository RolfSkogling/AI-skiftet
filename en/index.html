Physical AI

Hyundai workers stage history’s first strike over humanoid robots

Around 35,000 workers at Hyundai in South Korea downed tools for four hours a day on July 13–15 in protest against plans to put the humanoid robot Atlas on the factory floor. According to the Wall Street Journal, it is the car industry’s first factory stoppage over humanoid robots.

The union demands that no robot be deployed without a prior labor-management agreement, guarantees against job losses, and a fixed monthly salary that protects incomes if automation cuts working hours; bonus issues are also part of the dispute. The backdrop is that Hyundai, which recently took full ownership of robot maker Boston Dynamics, wants to deploy more than 25,000 robots across Hyundai and Kia plants starting in Georgia in 2028 – just as Atlas was recently shown off as a ball-kicking crowd-pleaser at the FIFA World Cup.

For the Nordics, the conflict is a harbinger. Volvo Cars, Scania, and Hydro are pursuing advanced automation plans, and so far Sweden’s IF Metall and Norway’s Fellesforbundet have met robotization with reskilling and co-determination rather than conflict. The Hyundai strike thus becomes a test case that Nordic industrial unions are likely to study closely ahead of coming bargaining rounds.

Source: Wall Street Journal, Forbes, eWeek
Regulation

OpenAI rolls out age assessment and parental controls in ChatGPT

On Friday, OpenAI presented a comprehensive set of teen protections in ChatGPT: an AI model that assesses the user’s age, expanded parental controls, and more frequent reminders to take breaks.

The age model is now rolling out broadly on consumer plans. If the service is unsure of a user’s age, the under-18 experience is applied by default, and adults can verify their age to unlock the full mode. Parents can set quiet hours, turn off voice mode and image generation, and receive alerts at signs of self-harm. The model rules, the Model Spec, have also been updated with specific teen protections. According to OpenAI, nearly nine in ten teens on the service use it for learning, information seeking, or productivity in a typical week.

For the Nordics, the announcement lands in the middle of an ongoing oversight debate. On July 15, Sweden’s Public Health Agency was tasked by the government with investigating generative AI’s risks to children’s and young people’s mental health, Norway is pushing for age limits on social media, and the EU’s updated AI rules ban AI-generated child sexual abuse material from December. OpenAI’s self-regulation becomes an entry in the debate over whether voluntary safeguards suffice – or whether Sweden’s IMY and Norway’s Medietilsynet and Datatilsynet need sharper tools.

Source: OpenAI, Fox News, Digital Trends
Infrastructure

Inference company Fireworks valued at $17.5 billion

US-based Fireworks AI, which runs and tunes open AI models for companies, has raised $1.5 billion in a Series D round valuing the company at $17.5 billion – one of the largest rounds yet in the fast-growing inference layer.

The round was led by Atreides Management, Index Ventures, and TCV, with Nvidia among the co-investors. The company, founded in 2022 by former Meta engineers with CEO Lin Qiao at the helm, reports an annualized revenue run rate of over one billion dollars – a fivefold increase in a year – and serves over 40 trillion tokens a day to customers such as Uber, Shopify, GitLab, and MongoDB. The business case rests on more and more companies wanting to run specialized, cheaper models instead of paying a premium for the largest closed systems.

For the Nordics, this is precisely the market segment the region’s AI data centers are positioning for: Nscale’s facility in Narvik, Brookfield’s campus in Strängnäs, and EcoDataCenter in Falun are being built to lease out compute with Nordic hydropower as the selling point. And for fast-growing Swedish companies like Lovable, heavy consumers of inference, the price competition is pointing in the right direction.

Source: CNBC, Fireworks, SiliconANGLE
Research

Europe’s frontier-lab contest kicks off – Nordic coalition wants a lab of its own

July marks the start of the build phase of the Next Frontier AI Challenge, the European competition that, with 125 million euros, aims to produce at least three European frontier labs with foundation models of their own. Up to ten teams are funded for 24 months, after the jury made its selection at the end of June.

The program is led by SPRIND, Germany’s federal agency for breakthrough innovation, and was launched by Germany, France, and the European Commission at the EurIPS research conference in Copenhagen in December 2025. Funding is paid out in three stages – up to 3, 8, and 15.5 million euros per team – and in the autumn of 2028 up to three winning labs are to be positioned for funding rounds of up to one billion euros each. No broad public list of the selected teams has yet been published.

For the Nordics, the stakes are concrete. AI Sweden, together with Denmark’s DIREC, the Danish Data Science Academy, and the Nordic AI Partnership network, has mobilized to identify and support Nordic candidate teams, with the goal that at least one of Europe’s coming frontier labs ends up in the Nordics – building on existing infrastructure such as the LUMI supercomputer in Finland and competence hubs like WASP in Sweden and NTNU in Trondheim.

Models

Moonshot’s Kimi K3 becomes the largest open AI model ever

China’s Moonshot AI released Kimi K3 late on Thursday, a model with roughly 2.7–2.8 trillion parameters and a one-million-token context window. That makes it the largest open-track model to date, and Bloomberg describes it as a major step toward narrowing the gap with America’s top models.

K3 is built on a sparse mixture-of-experts architecture with the new Kimi Delta Attention mechanism, has native vision and costs 3 and 15 dollars per million tokens in and out. In blind tests at the evaluator Arena, developers ranked K3 highest on front-end coding – though this is preference voting rather than verified benchmarks, and on broader measures the model is judged to sit just below the best closed systems. The weights are promised as a free download on July 27 – at which point anyone can inspect and run the model locally.

For the Nordics, the July 27 weights release opens a new possibility: research environments like WASP in Sweden and NorwAI at NTNU, as well as public agencies with strict data-sovereignty requirements, can run a frontier-class model on their own infrastructure without data leaving the country. At the same time, it sharpens the question for Nordic companies of what the premium for closed American models actually buys.

Source: Bloomberg, Fortune, Axios

Xi opens Shanghai AI conference and launches global cooperation body

China’s President Xi Jinping delivered the opening keynote at the World AI Conference in Shanghai on Friday – the first time a Chinese head of state has taken the conference stage since it began in 2018. At the same time, according to several international media outlets, China formally launched the World AI Cooperation Organization – a new intergovernmental AI body headquartered in Shanghai.

Xi called for broad global AI efforts at a moment when US export controls are choking China’s access to advanced chips. The conference runs July 17–20 with more than 140 forums and over 1,100 exhibitors, and the offer is aimed particularly at countries in the Global South: a complete Chinese stack of chips, models and rules. No equivalent American proposal for global AI governance is on the table.

For the Nordics, the move poses an uncomfortable question. Sweden and Norway have anchored their AI policy in the EU’s track through the AI Act, and as rule-making power begins to be shared between Washington, Brussels and Shanghai, the Riksdag, the Storting and the Nordic foreign ministries are forced to consider how small export-dependent countries navigate between the blocs.

Source: NPR, Bloomberg, TechTimes

Sweden’s Neko Health raises 700 million dollars for AI health scanning

Stockholm-based Neko Health, co-founded by Spotify’s Daniel Ek and Hjalmar Nilsonne, has raised 700 million dollars in a Series C round valuing the company at nearly seven billion dollars – a fourfold increase since its previous round in January 2025.

The round was led by Lightspeed Venture Partners and O.G. Venture Partners, with names like Mark Zuckerberg and Priscilla Chan among the investors. The money will fund a US expansion, with the first clinic opening in New York later this year. The company combines proprietary sensor technology with AI analysis to detect signs of disease early, operates eight clinics in Sweden and the UK, has completed over 100,000 full-body scans and already has 300,000 people on its American waiting list.

For the Nordics, this is one of the largest AI rounds ever for a Swedish company and proof that Nordic health tech holds its own in the global race for capital. At the same time, the value of health screening for healthy people is contested in medical research – the risk of false positives and overdiagnosis is a well-known problem – a debate that is alive in Swedish and Norwegian healthcare too.

Hardware

Huawei shows giant cluster challenging Nvidia – with no American parts

Huawei this week showed off its Atlas 950 SuperPoD system at the AI conference in Shanghai. The system links 8,192 Ascend processors into what in practice works as a single giant computing unit.

According to Huawei, the cluster delivers 8 exaflops of FP8 compute and 6.7 times the power of Nvidia’s NVL144 system – figures that are self-reported and not yet verified by any independent party. The UnifiedBus 2.0 architecture lets each chip address the others’ memory directly, functioning as a memory fabric rather than a network. The message is strategic: Chinese AI infrastructure can now be built entirely without American components, and the demonstration came the same week as the Kimi K3 launch and Xi’s speech.

For the Nordics, the dependence on American chips is total today: the LUMI supercomputer in Finland is built on AMD, while Sweden’s EcoDataCenter and Google’s Norwegian data center in Skien are filled with Nvidia hardware. A credible Chinese stack fragments the supply chain over time and turns chip choice into a geopolitical question for Nordic procurement departments as well.

Source: Seoul Economic Daily, Digitimes

Google’s Gemini delay wipes $200 billion off Alphabet’s market value

Alphabet shares fell 4.4 percent on Thursday after Bloomberg reported that the flagship Gemini 3.5 Pro model is months behind schedule. Roughly $200 billion of the company’s market value evaporated in a single trading day.

CEO Sundar Pichai originally promised the model for June at the I/O developer conference, and as recently as last week leaks pointed to a broad launch on July 17. According to Bloomberg’s sources, the model’s code generation still falls short of internal targets, just as OpenAI, Anthropic and Meta have recently shown models that have pulled ahead on coding. Inside Google, frustration is growing among engineers and researchers as the gap narrows.

For the Nordics, the sell-off hits pension capital directly: Norway’s Government Pension Fund Global holds Alphabet as one of its largest equity positions, and Sweden’s AP funds own billions of kronor worth of the stock. Nordic developers and companies waiting for the model in Google’s cloud services must also brace for a longer wait.

Physical AI

Japan launches a national physical AI program with Nvidia at the helm

Japan’s government and Nvidia on Thursday launched what they call the world’s first national AI infrastructure for physical AI – robots and machines that understand and move through the real world. Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang described the initiative as a once-in-a-generation opportunity.

The package includes an AI factory that Nvidia is building with Noetra Corp, equipped with 13,750 Vera CPUs and 27,500 Rubin GPUs, along with partnerships with industrial giants Fujitsu, Hitachi, Kawasaki Heavy Industries, Fanuc and Yaskawa. The same week, Nvidia unveiled the Cosmos 3 Edge world model, which lets robots interpret their physical surroundings in real time. Minister of Economy, Trade and Industry Ryosei Akazawa attended the launch, where Huang singled out the physical world as AI’s next frontier.

For the Nordics, the Japanese model is worth studying: Norwegian humanoid maker 1X, ABB Robotics and Denmark’s Universal Robots compete in the same wave of physical AI, yet no state program of the Japanese kind exists in Sweden or Norway. For the robotics groups at NTNU and SINTEF, the open Cosmos family meanwhile becomes a new tool to build on.

Source: Nvidia, CNBC
Labour market

Sixteen Nobel laureates: prepare the economy for the AI shift now

More than 200 economists and AI researchers, including sixteen Nobel laureates, demand in an open letter that policymakers and tech leaders act immediately to prepare the economy for far more capable AI. The letter was released on Monday by Stanford’s Digital Economy Lab.

The signatories warn of a transformation that could be larger than the Industrial Revolution but play out on a dramatically shorter timeline, with large-scale job displacement among the risks and major gains in living standards as the potential upside. The names include Nobel laureates Daron Acemoglu and Simon Johnson, who previously played down expectations for AI’s economic impact but now endorse the call for action. Anton Korinek, one of the organizers, warns that waiting for certainty means arriving too late: strategy and institutions cannot be improvised in the middle of the transformation.

For the Nordics, the letter’s prescription – incentives, guardrails and institutions – lands squarely at the heart of the Nordic model. Safety nets like Sweden’s TRR and Norway’s NAV were built for earlier technology shifts, and the question the letter poses is whether that adjustment capacity holds up when change moves several times faster.

Source: Stanford Digital Economy Lab, Al Jazeera/Reuters

AI boom’s memory crunch raises Ericsson’s network costs as 5G passes three billion

Ericsson’s quarterly report on Tuesday shows how the AI boom is hitting telecoms from two directions: the AI giants’ data centers are vacuuming up the same DRAM memory used in base stations, just as global 5G subscriptions have passed 3.1 billion.

Revenue fell six percent to SEK 52.7 billion, but margins held: the adjusted gross margin rose to 48.4 percent and cloud software grew five percent organically. Earnings nevertheless missed analyst expectations, weighing on the stock after the report. During the quarter the company also demonstrated AI-powered drone detection using existing cell towers at a Texas stadium – an example of new AI revenue from the networks. Net cash stood at SEK 59.8 billion.

For the Nordics, the report sends a dual signal: Ericsson is Sweden’s most important tech exporter, and rising component costs risk making network rollouts more expensive at home as well. At the same time, AI services in the networks open new business for operators like Telia and Telenor.

OpenAI trains its own attacker AI to harden GPT-5.6 against attacks

OpenAI on Wednesday unveiled GPT-Red, an internal AI model trained solely to attack the company’s own models and find security holes. The goal is to close the weaknesses before the models reach users.

GPT-Red is trained through self-play, in which the attacker and a set of defender models are trained at the same time: the attacker is rewarded for successful prompt injections while the defenders are rewarded for resisting them. The result is a highly capable attacker – in an independent test setup it succeeded on 84 percent of attempts against an older model, compared with 13 percent for human experts. OpenAI then fed the attacks into the training of GPT-5.6 Sol, which is now said to fail on just 0.05 percent of GPT-Red’s direct injection attacks and to make six times fewer errors than the previous flagship. The attacker model itself is deliberately kept internal so the capability does not fall into the wrong hands.

For the Nordics, prompt injection is becoming an ever hotter issue as AI agents are connected to email, files and banking services. Sweden’s National Cyber Security Centre at the FRA was tasked earlier in July with becoming a contact point for companies building advanced AI models, and for major banks such as SEB and DNB testing agent AI on customers, OpenAI’s figures bear directly on how safe it is to let a model handle payments and sensitive data.

Source: OpenAI, VentureBeat

Government tasks FRA’s cyber centre with countering AI-driven cyber threats

The Swedish government has tasked the National Cyber Security Centre at the Swedish Defence Radio Agency (FRA) with coordinating and strengthening cybersecurity work linked to artificial intelligence. The decision is meant to meet the threats that follow as AI models now find software vulnerabilities faster than before.

Together with the agencies concerned, the centre is to prevent, detect and counter AI-driven cyberattacks, analyse the risks and dependencies around advanced models, and represent Sweden in international and European forums. It is also to become a contact point for companies that build advanced AI models. – The advance of AI models forces us to accelerate this work further, says Minister for Civil Defence Carl-Oskar Bohlin. The assignment is to be reported orally to the Government Offices no later than 13 November.

For the Nordics the decision reflects a wider unease: as AI can both find vulnerabilities faster and be exploited in attacks, pressure grows to protect the new AI infrastructure. Norway’s Økokrim has warned that data centres can be exploited for money laundering, and with Sweden and Norway in the middle of a data-centre boom from Luleå to Narvik, the question of who protects the facilities is becoming central in both countries.

Source: Regeringen, Nordic Defence Sector
Quantum

Rolls-Royce and Quantinuum test quantum computing on turbines’ hardest calculations

Quantinuum, Rolls-Royce, Riverlane and the University of Edinburgh signed a multi-year agreement on Tuesday to explore whether today’s quantum computers can speed up the fluid-dynamics calculations used to design gas turbines – one of industry’s most computationally demanding problems.

The work will run on Quantinuum’s 98-qubit Helios processor, whose performance was recently confirmed in an independent evaluation. Rolls-Royce contributes industrial design cases, Riverlane contributes error correction, and Edinburgh’s supercomputing centre EPCC contributes hybrid computing in which quantum and supercomputers work side by side. The partners will test building blocks for industrially relevant quantum algorithms and assess how they can scale on planned systems such as Sol and Apollo.

For the Nordics the effort points toward the heavy engineering industries: Siemens Energy in Finspång builds industrial gas turbines and GKN Aerospace in Trollhättan makes engine parts for Rolls-Royce among others – both with the same kind of fluid-dynamics calculations in focus. On the quantum side, Chalmers and the Wallenberg initiative WACQT drive Swedish research that could connect Nordic industry to exactly this kind of hybrid computing.

Source: Quantinuum, HPCwire

Norwegian expert warns against leaning on AI to plan your holiday

Nearly half of holidaymakers now use AI to plan their trip, but a Norwegian expert warns in NRK against blindly trusting the chatbot as a travel guide.

The problem is that language models do not always check whether something has recently changed, but answer based on what there is most text about online – and they can then both invent opening hours and describe hotel rooms that do not match reality. International reporting points the same way: AI can sound entirely confident while being wrong, and the expert advises travellers to double-check its claims against official sources before booking.

For the Nordics the timing is sensitive in the middle of the Nordic holiday month of July, when Swedes and Norwegians are planning trips like never before. Consumer authorities such as Norway’s Forbrukertilsynet and Sweden’s Konsumentverket are watching how misleading AI content affects consumers, and the example shows how quickly the technology has gone from novelty to everyday tool – with the responsibility to fact-check left to the user.

Anthropic gives US teachers free access to Claude

Anthropic on Tuesday launched Claude for Teachers, a free version of Claude for verified K-12 educators in the United States. The offering matches a subscription that normally costs $20 per month.

The service connects Claude to curriculum-aligned academic standards in all 50 states through Learning Commons and to vetted instructional materials, with ready-made tools for lesson planning and for adapting materials to students at different levels. Data is not used for model training, and student information is protected under the US privacy law FERPA. The launch comes in partnership with the American Federation of Teachers, and educators who sign up by June 30, 2027, get a full year of free access. Google, OpenAI, and Khan Academy are making similar pushes into schools.

For the Nordics, the move puts pressure on education authorities: Sweden’s National Agency for Education (Skolverket) has been tracking AI use in Swedish schools since May, and the government has commissioned work to counter AI cheating in upper secondary school. As AI giants start giving away tailored teacher tools for free, pressure grows on Skolverket and Norway’s Directorate for Education (Utdanningsdirektoratet) to clarify which tools belong in Nordic classrooms – and on what terms.

Hardware

AI demand hands TSMC another record quarter

Taiwan’s TSMC, the world’s largest contract chipmaker, reported second-quarter revenue of NT$1.27 trillion – roughly $39.6 billion and a 36 percent increase year over year.

The figure came in slightly above analyst expectations, and June alone grew 67.9 percent compared with the same month last year. TSMC is in practice the only company able to mass-produce the most advanced AI chips, manufacturing for Nvidia and Apple among others, which makes its revenue the industry’s best thermometer for whether all the announced data center investments are converting into actual chip orders. The full quarterly report with profit and guidance is due on Thursday.

For the Nordics, the dependence is direct: chips from Norway’s Nordic Semiconductor in Trondheim and the silicon in Ericsson’s 5G platforms are manufactured at TSMC, sharing both capacity and geopolitical risk with the AI giants. The AI data centers in Luleå, Boden, and Narvik are likewise filled with accelerators from the same Taiwanese fabs – making the concentration in the Taiwan Strait a Nordic supply question too.

Anthropic is committing ten million Canadian dollars in Claude credits to eight Canadian institutions, funding research into beneficial and responsible applications of AI.

Each partner receives one million dollars in credits: the three national AI institutes Mila, Vector, and Amii, the universities of Toronto, Saskatchewan, and Laval, plus the children’s hospital CHEO and the psychiatric hospital CAMH. Swedish health agency to probe AI risks for children and teens

The Swedish government is tasking the Public Health Agency (Folkhälsomyndigheten) with compiling knowledge on how generative AI affects the mental health of children and young people, and with producing guidance and recommendations for children, parents, and relevant organizations.

According to the Swedish Internet Foundation’s report Svenskarna och internet 2025, more than half of all children aged 8 to 19 use AI tools, and many use AI as a companion or everyday adviser. The government points to risks such as reinforced negative thought patterns, weakened capacity for human relationships, sleep disturbances, and the spread of false health information. The agency will consult with the Swedish Consumer Agency, the Swedish Agency for the Media, and the telecom regulator PTS, among others. An interim report is due by June 1, 2027, and the final report by November 15, 2027.

The assignment is among the first in the Nordics to target AI companions and mental health specifically. It builds on the Public Health Agency’s screen-time recommendations and June’s recommendation to wait until age 13 before giving children their own phone – and the Swedish guidelines could become a reference point for Norwegian authorities as similar demands are raised there.

Software giants unite behind agent standard challenging Anthropic's MCP

Google, Microsoft, Salesforce, Snowflake, and ServiceNow have agreed to back a shared protocol for how AI agents connect to enterprise systems and data, The Information reports. The move is openly framed as an attempt to push back against Anthropic and OpenAI in the enterprise market.

The battle is over the plumbing of enterprise AI: the standards that decide how agents reach tools, data, and each other. Anthropic's MCP protocol has become the de facto standard for tool connections over 18 months, and the new alliance gathers companies that together own most of the world's business data and workflows. At the same time, the same companies sit alongside OpenAI and Anthropic in the Linux Foundation's Agentic AI Foundation, which is meant to produce open agent standards – cooperation in the foundation, fierce competition in the market.

For the Nordics, the standards war touches every organization currently building AI agents on top of Microsoft 365, Salesforce, and Snowflake – from SEB and Telia to Equinor and Norwegian municipalities. Which protocol wins determines where the integrations live and how hard the lock-in becomes – a question Sweden's Digg and Norway's Digdir will have to weigh as the public sector procures agent platforms.

Source: The Information, Tom's Hardware
Regulation

EU's new cloud and AI act out for Swedish consultation – replies due October 2

The Swedish government has sent the European Commission's proposed cloud and AI regulation, the Cloud and AI Development Act, out for national consultation and has submitted its explanatory memorandum to the Riksdag. Consultation responses are due by October 2.

The proposal, presented by the Commission on June 3, aims to strengthen Europe's cloud and AI ecosystem on three fronts: research and development of next-generation technology, faster build-out of data centers with a focus on essential public functions, and a single EU-wide framework for assessing sovereignty in cloud services. The goal is to roughly triple the EU's data center capacity within five to seven years. In its memorandum, the government takes a positive view of the ambition to strengthen the EU's capacity, competitiveness, and digital sovereignty.

For the Nordics, the proposal is business-critical: Sweden and Norway compete for AI data centers with abundant power and cooling, from Luleå and Boden to Narvik, and faster permitting across the EU could both boost the build-out and sharpen the competition. Swedish authorities and industry players have until October 2 to respond, and the consultation round lays the groundwork for Sweden's position when negotiations begin in the Council.

Source: Government of Sweden, European Commission

SK Hynix completes the largest foreign listing in US history

South Korean memory maker SK Hynix listed its depositary receipts on the Nasdaq on Friday, raising $26.5 billion – the largest US listing by a foreign company ever. As of today, Monday, the stock trades under its permanent ticker SKHY.

Quantum

Finland's IQM to build the quantum computer wired into the LUMI supercomputer

The LUMI AI Factory and Finland's CSC have selected IQM to deliver a new quantum computer, named LUMI-IQ. The system, an IQM Halocene H4 with 150 qubits, is to be installed in Kajaani, Finland, by 2027.

The quantum computer will be integrated directly with the European supercomputer LUMI so that classical and quantum processors can work in the same computational flow – including for developing hybrid AI models. The deal was announced on July 8 and is one of the largest European orders for a quantum computer intended for hybrid AI operation. IQM received clearance for a Nasdaq listing as recently as June.

For the Nordics: the LUMI consortium includes Sweden through the Swedish Research Council and Norway through Sigma2, so Swedish and Norwegian researchers will get access to the machine. The order also raises the tempo for Chalmers and WACQT, which are building Sweden's own quantum computer in Gothenburg.

The Fed examines AI's impact on jobs – Andreessen leads the task force

The US Federal Reserve is setting up a task force to assess how AI affects productivity, jobs and monetary policy. Venture capitalist Marc Andreessen, Stanford economist Charles I. Jones and Microsoft's Asha Sharma will lead the group.

The "Productivity and Jobs" task force is one of five that Fed Chair Kevin Warsh presented on July 9 as part of a broad review of how the central bank conducts monetary policy. The mandate is to assess the economic impact of new general-purpose technologies such as AI: if the technology lifts productivity, the economy can grow faster without inflation – but if it displaces jobs at scale, the central bank faces far harder trade-offs. The choice of Andreessen, one of the AI industry's biggest investors, has already raised questions about conflicts of interest. Jones is currently on leave for a stint at Anthropic.

For the Nordics: the Riksbank and Norges Bank are wrestling with the same question – how AI should factor into interest-rate and inflation assessments. The Fed's conclusions tend to set the tone for smaller central banks, and the analyses will feed the debate on AI and employment already under way at Statistics Sweden, NAV and the social partners.

Models

OpenAI's new voice models listen and speak at the same time

OpenAI has launched GPT-Live, a new generation of voice models that can listen and speak simultaneously. The technique, known as full duplex, makes conversations more human-like: the model can interject an "mhm" or stay quiet while the user thinks.

GPT-Live-1 mini replaces the previous advanced voice mode in ChatGPT by default, while paying users get access to the larger GPT-Live-1. For harder questions, the voice model hands the task to a top-tier model in the background – GPT-5.5 at launch – and reads out the answer when it is ready. The models have been rolling out globally since July 8.

For the Nordics: more natural AI voices affect everything from Telenor's and Telia's customer service to voice-driven services at banks such as SEB and DNB. How well the duplex technique handles Swedish and Norwegian remains untested – a recurring dilemma when voice AI is optimized for English first.

Source: OpenAI, TechCrunch
