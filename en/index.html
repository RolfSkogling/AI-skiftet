Models

Anthropic launches Claude Opus 4.8 – faster, cheaper and with dynamic sub-agents

Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.8 on May 28, an upgrade of its flagship model with stronger performance in coding, agent orchestration, financial analysis and knowledge work – at the same price as the previous version.

The model has a fast mode that runs 2.5 times faster and three times cheaper than previous versions, and introduces a “dynamic workflow” capability that lets multiple sub-agents run in parallel under a single controller. A new control panel lets users decide how much effort Claude puts into each response. Opus 4.8 is available immediately on the Anthropic API, AWS Bedrock and Google Vertex AI, and became generally available (GA) for GitHub Copilot the same day. Anthropic notes that the model now performs close to its internal Mythos model on prosocial-trait benchmarks; the company plans a public Mythos release “in the coming weeks” once additional safeguards are in place.

For the Nordics, the upgrade lands directly inside the developer stack. KTH, Chalmers, NTNU and Uppsala University use Claude in coursework and research projects, while Klarna, SEB, DNB and Telia run Claude APIs via AWS Bedrock in the Stockholm region. With GA on GitHub Copilot, Opus 4.8 also reaches the developer teams at Ericsson, Volvo Cars and Equinor, and the lower per-token price lowers the bar for start-ups such as Swedish Lovable and Sana and Norwegian Cognigy that build on the Anthropic stack.

Source: Axios, TechCrunch, GitHub Changelog
Quantum

Chalmers gathers 150 quantum researchers at WACQT’s annual meeting – IBM stack moves into Swedish infrastructure

The Wallenberg Centre for Quantum Technology (WACQT) held its eighth May Meeting in Gothenburg on May 19–21. Over three days, more than 150 researchers, doctoral students and industry partners from six universities reviewed the centre’s ongoing work on quantum computers, sensors and communications.

The programme featured around fifty talks spanning hardware development, system integration, algorithms, quantum communications and sensing. The centre has since last year been an IBM Quantum Innovation Center, with IBM’s Heron and Eagle processors connected to Swedish infrastructure via the Quantum Technology Testbed at MC2 in Gothenburg. The Wallenberg foundations’ ten-year commitment of SEK 1.3 billion runs through 2027.

For the Nordics, the meeting anchors a shared roadmap. KTH, Linköping University, Lund and Stockholm University reported from their WACQT projects, while Saab, Ericsson, Volvo and AstraZeneca followed industrial-use discussions. Norway’s Sintef and the Niels Bohr Institute in Copenhagen ran their own sessions on quantum communications and error correction – and with IBM’s new quantum data centers in Poughkeepsie and Ehningen, the WACQT testbed becomes the natural Nordic access point for Berzelius and NorwAI teams that want to prototype hybrid quantum-AI workflows.

Hardware

AMD and Nvidia kick off the 2026 AI-chip duel – MI400 and Rubin hit the market in the second half

Competition for the next generation of AI accelerators is entering a decisive phase. AMD confirmed the entire MI400 series for second-half 2026 delivery at CES in January, while Nvidia in parallel has promised the first Vera Rubin chips in the same window – both with ramp-up rolling into 2027.

AMD’s MI400X comes with up to 432 GB of HBM4 memory and 19.6 TB/s of memory bandwidth, which the company claims is ten times faster than the MI300X on select AI workloads. Rubin keeps 288 GB per GPU but raises bandwidth to 13 TB/s; Rubin Ultra doubles memory again in the second half of 2027. Both companies remain heavily dependent on TSMC’s CoWoS packaging – a bottleneck the AI industry itself has flagged throughout 2026.

For the Nordics, the chip duel is decisive for the hyperscale build-out. The Stargate Norway project in Narvik is set to roll in 100,000 Nvidia GPUs this year according to OpenAI, Nscale and Aker, while the Berzelius system in Linköping, AI Sweden in Kista and Sweden’s Sferical AI factory are negotiating upgrade windows for Rubin and MI400 deliveries. EcoDataCenter in Falun, Norway’s Lefdal Mine and Sweden’s Hydro66 in Boden are already seeing customer requests for dedicated AMD clusters, and Volvo, Saab and Ericsson have begun evaluating whether to build their own in-house training clusters or rent capacity from GPU-as-a-service providers such as CoreWeave and Lambda.

Source: TechRadar, TweakTown, Datacenter Forum
Labour market

Almega report: Three years with AI – Swedish services sector keeps jobs despite automation pressure

The Swedish service-sector employers’ federation Almega published in May the report “Three years with AI – what has happened to the jobs?”. It concludes that the Swedish labour market has adapted to generative AI without the mass unemployment that several AI companies warned about after the launch of ChatGPT in 2022.

Drawing on data from Statistics Sweden (SCB) and forecasts from the National Institute of Economic Research, Almega finds that employment in highly AI-exposed service occupations has not fallen overall – but that 22–25-year-olds in the same occupations have seen employment drop by around 5.5 per cent compared with less exposed colleagues at the same employers. The finding lines up with the Unionen union’s autumn 2025 chapter survey, where 67 per cent of chapters report that their companies have introduced AI in operations, a doubling in two years. The report lands the same week as OpenAI’s Sam Altman and Anthropic’s Dario Amodei walk back their most dramatic “AI erases entry-level” forecasts.

For the Nordics, Almega’s figures provide concrete negotiating ground. LO, TCO and Saco can argue against short-term AI-driven layoffs, even as SEB, Handelsbanken and Telia have announced heavy AI cost programmes. On the Norwegian side, LO, Akademikerne and Tekna are following the debate closely – and both the Trygderetten in Oslo and Sweden’s Arbetsförmedlingen face decisions about how AI support should be used in case handling without creating new bias. Vinnova and the Research Council of Norway have already opened coordination tracks for transition funding that universities, RISE and Sintef can apply for this autumn.

Source: Almega, Arbetsvärlden, AI Sweden

Debate

China requires exit approval for AI researchers at Alibaba and DeepSeek

China is imposing formal travel curbs on senior AI researchers at private firms such as Alibaba and DeepSeek. According to Bloomberg on 26 May, key staff in advanced AI work must now obtain prior approval from authorities before any overseas travel, regardless of destination.

The restrictions cover founders, researchers and executives in strategically important AI work – not all employees. As recently as March 2025 Beijing was still issuing informal guidance, aimed mainly at US trips. Since then the rules have hardened into a formal pre-approval regime. The drivers are dual: protect domestic tech from leaks, while making it harder for talent to defect to US companies such as SpaceX/xAI or the frontier labs. Decrypt and TechTimes also report that several Chinese AI labs now require staff to hand in their passports.

For the Nordics the talent map is shifting. KTH, Chalmers, Linköping, NTNU, Aalto and the University of Copenhagen have long taken in Chinese doctoral students under the WASP, NorwAI and AI Sweden tracks, while Ericsson, ABB and Volvo run research offices in Hangzhou and Shanghai. With Beijing’s exit rules, both recruiting from China and Nordic-China collaborations become harder to promise – and MSB, NSM and the Norwegian Defence Research Establishment have fresh reason to look closely at dual-use AI crossing the line between academic and industrial research.

Source: Bloomberg, Decrypt, TechTimes
Research

Sweden opens a Vinnova track for SEK 380 million into EU AI and semiconductor projects

The Swedish government has tasked Vinnova with funding Sweden’s participation in two large IPCEI projects – Important Projects of Common European Interest – on artificial intelligence and advanced semiconductors. The mandate was published on 19 May.

The financial envelope covers SEK 20 million in 2027, SEK 80 million in 2028, SEK 100 million a year in 2029–2030 and SEK 80 million in 2031 – just under SEK 380 million in total. Vinnova will pick Swedish firms that clear EU state-aid rules and tie them into consortia already rolled out on the continent, with a first interim report to the government on 30 March 2027 and the final report due in 2032. The push complements the national AI strategy from February (SEK 479 m) and Vinnova’s own Advanced Digitalisation programme of SEK 570 m for 2026.

For the Nordics the IPCEI track opens a clear industrial-policy door. Ericsson, ABB, AstraZeneca and Saab can plug their existing AI factory bets (Sferical AI, Berzelius, WASP) into the European consortia, while the semiconductor track gives Mycronic, RISE and Swedish–Finnish suppliers a way into the European advanced-packaging stack. Norway is outside IPCEI directly – but the Research Council, NTNU and NORA already run EEA-aligned arrangements that let Aker, Equinor and Yara come along via Swedish consortia.

Source: Regeringen.se, Vinnova
Physical AI

Tesla closes the Model S line in Fremont – Optimus takes over as the humanoid race heats up

Tesla is ending Model S and Model X production in Fremont, California, during the second quarter of 2026 and converting the factory to its Optimus humanoid robot. In parallel, the company is breaking ground on a second-generation site at Giga Texas.

On the Q1 earnings call CEO Elon Musk pushed the start of Optimus production to late summer 2026, but Tesla’s target of one million robots a year by the end of 2027 still stands. The long-term ambition is 10 million robots a year out of Texas. The rest of the humanoid field is scaling too: Figure 03 is in the second phase of its BMW Spartanburg pilot, Boston Dynamics/Hyundai Atlas is fully booked for 2026, and China’s Unitree went from roughly 5 500 units in 2025 to between 10 000 and 20 000 this year.

For the Nordics the move lands straight in the home markets of ABB Robotics and Denmark’s Universal Robots and MiR. Norway’s 1X in Moss is preparing the first deliveries of its NEO home robot, while Sintef and NTNU build embodied-AI stacks for maritime and industrial use. With the German Schaeffler agreement for 1 000–2 000 humanoids in car plants as a fresh contract benchmark, Volvo, SSAB, Saab and Yara now have a concrete negotiating base for humanoid pilots – and IF Metall, Almega and Fellesforbundet end up in the same room as employers when shift work, cleaning and materials handling are due to be automated.

Regulation

Pope Leo’s AI encyclical Magnifica Humanitas hits fast pushback – and Nordic reflection

Pope Leo XIV’s first encyclical, Magnifica Humanitas, was published on 25 May. The roughly 40 000-word text warns that AI is concentrating power and explicitly condemns autonomous weapons systems – and has already drawn sharp pushback from critics.

The encyclical was signed on the 135th anniversary of Rerum Novarum – Leo XIII’s manifesto on labour rights from the early industrial era – and was presented personally at the Vatican with Anthropic co-founder Chris Olah by the Pope’s side. The Future of Life Institute calls the text a clear rejection of the superintelligence doctrine, while Fortune and Time published analyses the same week arguing that the encyclical offers no concrete regulatory tools. The Vatican also announced that the Catholic media group EWTN opened a Scandinavian office in Stockholm on 21 May.

For the Nordics the text lands in two of the world’s most secular societies. The Church of Sweden bishops’ conference and the Church of Norway’s ethics council debated AI in pastoral care and communication this spring, and Karolinska, Sahlgrenska and Helse-Norge face sharp choices on AI in clinical diagnostics. Digital Government Minister Karianne Tung is at the same time preparing a Norwegian AI Act for late summer with high-risk provisions for welfare and education at the centre – and Sweden’s IMY has flagged AI in the public sector as its top oversight priority for 2026. The encyclical thus becomes a platform that LO, the Akademikerförbundet SSR union and the Council on Ethics for the Norwegian Government Pension Fund Global can lean on in upcoming bargaining and policy rounds.

Labor Market

Altman and Amodei walk back the AI jobs apocalypse – right ahead of their IPOs

In a single weekend one of the loudest AI forecasts cooled off. OpenAI’s Sam Altman now says he was “pretty wrong” that AI would rapidly erase entry-level jobs, and Anthropic’s Dario Amodei is taking back his earlier warning that the technology could wipe out half of white-collar work.

The backdrop is plainly commercial. Both firms are preparing IPOs at valuations close to USD 1 trillion apiece and need a far softer tone toward politicians, unions and pension funds. Amodei now argues that if 90% of a role is automated, the remaining 10% expands – a productivity thesis echoing Goldman Sachs and McKinsey, but cutting against Anthropic’s own warnings from last year.

For the Nordics the shift in tone changes the terms of an ongoing fight. LO, TCO and Unionen in Sweden, and Finansforbundet and LO Norge, have used the AI labs’ own apocalyptic numbers to demand transition agreements and training mandates. At the same time Standard Chartered, KPMG and Sweden’s SEB have already announced heavy AI savings – so as Altman and Amodei retract, Almega, Svenskt Näringsliv and the Research Council face the question of how fast transition funding really needs to kick in.

Infrastructure

Stargate Norway pulls power from Sweden – Vattenfall locks in the Narvik AI factory

OpenAI and Aker’s planned European AI factory near Narvik will run its first years on Swedish electrons. Nscale, which is building the site, has signed a deal with Vattenfall for renewable power that covers a significant share of the need across 2027–2031.

The facility starts at 230 MW and aims to deliver 100 000 Nvidia GPUs before year-end. The Norwegian state is providing NOK 2.2 billion in loans and private banks add the rest, for a total financing of roughly NOK 7.3 billion. The Vattenfall deal also has a clear Nordic edge: Norway is building its heaviest sovereign-AI bet on top of Swedish hydropower – while Denmark has paused new grid permits after applications hit 60 GW.

For the Nordics the deal exposes how thin renewable supply really is. Statnett, Svenska kraftnät and Fingrid face a real stress test, and Nord Pool prices could wobble once Narvik comes online at full power. For KTH, Chalmers and NTNU, Stargate opens the door to near-Nordic compute – provided Aker, Nscale and OpenAI grant public research the same access EuroHPC offers in Luleå and Kajaani.

Models

Fujitsu allies with both Anthropic and OpenAI – 100 000 employees go on Claude

Japan’s largest IT group is refusing to pick a side. Fujitsu announced on 27 May that it has signed a strategic partnership with Anthropic and at the same time is expanding its work with OpenAI – a sharp move away from the “single-model house” doctrine that has dominated enterprise AI.

About 100 000 Fujitsu employees will use Claude at work, while the company builds a 1 000-person Forward Deployed Engineer team to take the technology to customers. Claude is being woven into Fujitsu’s own AI platform Kozuchi and into the Japanese-developed language model Takane, built jointly with Cohere – one of the few models tailored for non-English public infrastructure. The OpenAI track focuses on Fujitsu’s “Uvance” portfolio for insurance, manufacturing and the public sector.

For the Nordics the Fujitsu blueprint is directly transferable. Tietoevry, Sopra Steria and CGI Nordics run large consulting arms for banking, energy and the public sector facing the same fork in the road – and KPMG has already embedded Claude for around 276 000 employees globally, including its Nordic offices. For Tietoevry’s planned Nordic “AI Factory”, and for AI Sweden’s talks with government on sovereign models, Fujitsu’s dual-track becomes a template: multiple frontier models, one home-grown Nordic model, and hundreds of dedicated engineers per major customer.

Quantum

IonQ posts 755% revenue growth – and sells its first 256-qubit system

The quantum hype is getting its first hard commercial proof. IonQ reports Q1 revenue of USD 64.7 million, up 755% year on year, and raises full-year 2026 guidance to USD 260–270 million.

The company also reports its first sale of a 256-qubit system and is opening a new R&D facility of about 2 000 m² in Boulder, Colorado, dedicated to semiconductor ion-trap chips – the architecture meant to scale toward thousands of logical qubits. The humanoids leave the stage – now they load bags and build cars

It is no longer just trade-show demos. Japan Airlines has begun testing humanoid robots in ground handling at Tokyo’s Haneda Airport – to load baggage and clean cabins. The 132 cm, 35 kg robots are based on models from China’s Unitree, in partnership with GMO AI & Robotics.

Regulation

As the EU brakes, Norway sticks to its own AI law for late summer

While the EU on 7 May agreed to postpone the heaviest high-risk rules of the AI Act until late 2027, Norway is holding its course toward a national AI law of its own. Minister of Digitalisation Karianne Tung is aiming for the law to enter into force in late summer 2026, roughly when the main part of the AI Act starts to apply in the EU.

The bill has been out for consultation and was originally meant to reach the Storting around Easter, but the work has been delayed because negotiations with the EU on the necessary EEA adjustments are taking longer than expected. In parallel, the government is establishing ”KI Norge”, a national arena for responsible AI, and a regulatory sandbox at the Norwegian Digitalisation Agency where companies can test solutions under supervision.

For the Nordics the pace is becoming a point of contention. Norway risks moving ahead of the EU on parts of the framework, while Sweden takes its own path to implement the AI Act with IMY and the government in the driver’s seat. For the Storting, Datatilsynet and Nkom it is about building supervision before the rules apply – and for Nordic companies about avoiding clashing timelines on either side of the Swedish–Norwegian border.

A new Swedish–Canadian collaboration aims to move AI from isolated pilot projects into everyday clinical practice. Unity Health Toronto is joining forces with three Swedish university hospitals – Karolinska, Sahlgrenska and Skåne – in an initiative coordinated by AI Sweden and financed by Vinnova.

When Karolinska, Sahlgrenska and Skåne share methods and failure modes with Canadian hospitals, they build a template that Norway’s Helse-Norge, Sintef and Oslo University Hospital can follow too – while IMY and Datatilsynet watch over the proper handling of patient data.

Hardware

Not the chip but the packaging – TSMC’s CoWoS becomes AI’s new bottleneck

What is slowing the AI build-out is not the silicon itself, but the art of packing it together. TSMC’s advanced packaging technology CoWoS has become the single hardest bottleneck in the 2026 AI supply chain – and Nvidia has already booked around 60% of the capacity.

At its technology forum on 14 May, deputy COO Kevin Zhang highlighted a new keyword, ”COUPE”, bringing optics closer to the compute die to cut latency further. TSMC is building new packaging capacity in Arizona before 2029, but the shortage of both CoWoS and HBM memory is expected to last until at least 2027 – whoever is not first in line waits for their GPUs.

For the Nordics this is the hidden limit on the AI dreams. The billion-euro builds – EuroHPC’s AI factories in Sweden and Finland, Nscale in Narvik, EcoDataCenter’s partnership with Mistral and atNorth – depend entirely on Nvidia’s cards actually being delivered on time. If packaging does not pick up, Nordic sovereign-AI bets, and Ericsson’s plans for AI-native 6G, risk standing with empty server halls.

Source: CNBC, DigiTimes

Infrastructure

Power, not chips, becomes AI's bottleneck – Nordic grids under heavy strain

It is no longer compute that runs short first, but electricity. In the Nordics the pressure has grown so intense that Denmark's state grid operator Energinet has since March paused new connection agreements after the queue of planned projects reached a staggering 60 gigawatts – nearly ten times the country's normal peak demand of around 7 gigawatts.

Data centres account for roughly 14 gigawatts of that queue. The same pattern is visible globally: on May 18, US utility NextEra Energy agreed to buy Dominion for about $67 billion – the largest utility merger in US history – explicitly to meet the thirst of AI data centres. Analysts expect data centres to consume 15–25% of US electricity by 2030. More compute does not help if the grid cannot deliver.

For the Nordics this cuts both ways. The region's cheap hydro and wind power have made it Europe's data-centre hub – Nscale's multi-billion build-out in Narvik, atNorth and EcoDataCenter's partnership with Mistral – but the grids cannot keep pace. Statnett, Svenska kraftnät and Finland's Fingrid must now choose between industry, electrification and AI halls, a politically charged trade-off that has reached the Swedish Energy Agency, Vinnova and Norway's Ministry of Energy.

Source: Fortune, Tom's Hardware, CNBC
Quantum

US pours $2 billion into quantum – and takes equity stakes

The US Commerce Department announced on May 21 that it will distribute more than $2 billion in CHIPS incentives to nine quantum-computing companies – and, as a condition, take a minority stake in each recipient. It is one of the clearest signs yet that quantum technology is being treated as a national priority, on par with semiconductors.

IBM is the anchor with $1 billion to build "Anderson," what the company calls America's first pure-play quantum wafer foundry, matching the federal commitment dollar for dollar. D-Wave, Rigetti, Infleqtion, PsiQuantum, Quantinuum and Atom Computing each receive about $100 million, while Diraq gets up to $38 million and GlobalFoundries up to $375 million for a new quantum-manufacturing division. The news lifted the whole sector: IonQ, which was not even among the recipients, jumped sharply on the news.

For the Nordics it sharpens the competitive picture. Finland's IQM is heading for a Nasdaq listing at around $1.8 billion, Chalmers' quantum centre WACQT and the spin-out Atlantic Quantum are building superconducting qubits, and the Niels Bohr Institute runs one of Europe's heaviest quantum programmes with the Novo Nordisk Foundation. When the US both subsidises and takes equity in its companies, the question becomes acute: how should the Nordics and the EU fund their own quantum capacity without falling behind?

Source: TechTimes, TheStreet

Anthropic's AI finds over 10,000 critical vulnerabilities in the world's software

Under its Project Glasswing initiative, Anthropic has used an as-yet-unreleased frontier model, Claude Mythos Preview, to hunt for vulnerabilities in society's most critical software. Together with around fifty partners, the company has found more than 10,000 high- or critical-severity vulnerabilities – many of them previously unknown "zero-days" in every major operating system and web browser.

The aim is defensive: to find and patch the holes before malicious actors can exploit them. The partners include heavyweight names such as AWS, Apple, Cisco, Google, JPMorgan and Microsoft. Yet the announcement sharpens an uncomfortable insight: the same capability that closes the holes can, in the wrong hands, be used to find ways in – a race between defence and attack that is now accelerating.

For the Nordics this lands squarely in critical infrastructure. Banks such as SEB, DNB and Nordea, telecom operators such as Telenor and Ericsson and a long line of public agencies all run the same global software now under scrutiny. Sweden's MSB and CERT-SE, Norway's NSM and Nkom, and the EU's NIS2 directive and AI Act face a new reality in which AI both raises the defence and lowers the threshold for attack.

Sweden appoints national coordinator for Swedish-language AI models

On May 21, the Swedish government appointed Katrin Westling Palm as national coordinator tasked with driving the development of Swedish-language AI models. The aim is to accelerate high-quality models that reflect the cultural, societal and legal contexts unique to Sweden.

Her mandate covers mapping needs – not least access to data – and proposing concrete measures, as well as analysing how rights holders should be compensated when copyrighted material is used in training. Hardware

AI's appetite drains the memory market – server prices surge

The race for AI capacity has triggered an acute shortage of working memory. Samsung, SK Hynix and Micron – together controlling more than 95 % of the world's DRAM output – have shifted capacity toward high-bandwidth memory (HBM) for AI accelerators, draining the supply of ordinary server and consumer memory.

According to analyst firm TrendForce, contract prices for server DRAM rose by more than 60 % in the first quarter, and TechInsights estimates that memory alone can raise the cost of an AI server by 10–25 %. Each gigabyte of HBM consumes three times as much silicon wafer as ordinary DDR5. TSMC's CEO has said supply will not meet demand until 2027, and Apple has already raised prices on part of the MacBook Pro lineup.

For the Nordics the timing bites. The major data-centre projects – EcoDataCenter and Mistral's billion-euro build in Sweden, atNorth across the Nordics and EuroHPC's AI factories in Sweden and Finland – will raise racks in the middle of the shortage, squeezing budgets. Meanwhile the next IT procurement for Ericsson, Volvo and municipalities grows more expensive, and Telenor's planned swap of tens of thousands of PCs ahead of the Windows 11 phase-out gets pricier.

Labour

AI cited behind record layoffs – while hiring slows

Outplacement firm Challenger, Gray & Christmas counts more than 49,000 AI-linked layoffs in the US so far in 2026 – roughly 16 % of the around 300,000 total cuts. In April, 21,490 layoffs were attributed to AI, the second consecutive month AI topped the reasons employers cited.

Tech-sector cuts ran 40 % higher than in the first quarter of 2025. Several economists stress that the biggest effect is not mass layoffs but the absence of hiring – especially of junior staff. Goldman Sachs estimates AI has shaved monthly employment growth by around 16,000 jobs. At the same time, analysts warn that "AI" is sometimes used as cover for cost cuts that are really about shifting budgets from staff to data centres and AI tools.

For the Nordics the figures land amid an ongoing debate. Norway leads Europe in AI use and Swedish employers are scaling up fast, sharpening the focus on the gap between adoption and reskilling. Unions such as Unionen and TCO in Sweden and LO and NHO in Norway are watching whether the American pattern – fewer entry-level jobs and quieter hiring – takes hold in the Nordics, a question that has also reached the Riksdag and the Storting.

Regulation

EU postpones the AI Act's high-risk rules to late 2027

On May 7, the EU institutions agreed on the "Digital Omnibus," the first package of amendments to the AI Act since 2024. Obligations for high-risk AI under Annex III are pushed from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027, and for AI embedded in regulated products to August 2, 2028.

Transparency rules on labelling synthetic content (Article 50) are also deferred, from August to December 2, 2026. The framework is tightened on one point: a new ban on AI that generates non-consensual intimate imagery and child sexual abuse material. The August 2, 2026 threshold for general-purpose AI models (GPAI) remains, however, when the Commission's enforcement powers – fines of up to 15 million euros or 3 % of turnover – take effect. Formal adoption is expected in June.

For the Nordics the delay brings breathing room. Swedish agencies, hospitals and municipalities that planned high-risk compliance for August gain more than an extra year, while Sweden's PTS – proposed as coordinating supervisory authority in SOU 2025:101 – and Norway's Datatilsynet and Nkom can align the EEA rules at a calmer pace. OpenAI model disproves 80-year-old Erdös conjecture – a first for AI cracking an open math problem

OpenAI said on May 20 that an internal reasoning model independently produced an original proof disproving a famous unsolved conjecture in discrete geometry, first posed by Paul Erdös in 1946. Several mathematicians describe it as the first time an AI has solved a prominent open problem on its own – rather than merely retrieving a known answer.

The problem concerns how many pairs of points can lie at exactly distance 1 when n points are placed in a plane; for nearly 80 years square grids were believed to be near-optimal. The model instead connected the geometry to algebraic number theory and found an infinite family of constructions that beats the grid. The result was set out in a companion paper co-signed by several leading mathematicians – among them Noga Alon, Fields medalist Timothy Gowers and Princeton's Will Sawin, who sharpened the bound further.
