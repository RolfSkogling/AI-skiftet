Models

OpenAI opens GPT-5.6 to everyone – three new models launch today

OpenAI is today making the full GPT-5.6 family generally available: the flagship Sol, the mid-tier Terra and the entry-level Luna. The announcement came on Wednesday, following a preview period in which Sol reached only selected partners.

Sol is the flagship for complex reasoning and agentic tasks, while Terra, according to OpenAI, matches its predecessor GPT-5.5 at roughly half the price – 2.50 dollars per million input tokens and 15 dollars for output. Luna costs 1 and 6 dollars respectively, and Sol's fast mode delivers up to 750 tokens per second. According to American media, the rollout was delayed after pressure from the Trump administration, which wanted a staggered release, but the restrictions have now been lifted. The launch lands in the middle of an intense model summer: Anthropic re-launched Fable 5 on July 1, and Google's Gemini 3.5 Pro is expected later in July.

For the Nordics, the news means that Swedish and Norwegian developers – who have so far been building on GPT-5.5 while users expected GPT-5.6, a gap recently debated in Norway's Digi – now get access to the same models as their American competitors. Terra's halved price lowers the threshold for AI services at major Nordic companies such as Ericsson, Telenor and DNB, while the launch also increases the pressure on European alternatives such as France's Mistral.

Source: OpenAI, Bloomberg, Neowin
Quantum

Microsoft moves its quantum-safe encryption deadline forward to 2029

Microsoft is bringing forward its target for the transition to quantum-safe encryption: the company's most important products and services are to be protected against quantum computer attacks by 2029, four years earlier than the roadmap towards 2033 published last year.

The background is that advances in quantum research have led Microsoft to conclude that cryptographically relevant quantum computers may arrive sooner than expected. Attackers can moreover already harvest encrypted traffic today in order to decrypt it once the machines exist – a tactic known as "harvest now, decrypt later". The effort focuses on three areas: network encryption with broader adoption of TLS 1.3, stored data where organisations need crypto-agility, and cryptographic trust chains. American and French authorities are already pointing towards quantum-safe requirements around 2030 for sensitive systems.

For the Nordics, the announcement puts pressure on both authorities and industry. Norway's security agency NSM and Sweden's MSB have both highlighted the quantum transition in their guidance, and banks such as DNB, SEB and Nordea manage encrypted data with a lifespan of decades. At Chalmers, the Wallenberg centre WACQT drives Swedish quantum research, and commentators in Norway's Digi recently warned that the timeline for the quantum threat is collapsing.

Source: Microsoft, The Quantum Insider, Digi.no
Regulation

Norwegian bestsellers found in pirated dataset used to train AI models

Books by 27 Norwegian authors – including Jo Nesbø, Vigdis Hjorth and Margit Sandemo – have been used to train large language models. That is shown by a survey by researchers at BI Norwegian Business School and OsloMet, Klassekampen reports.

The researchers examined the Scandinavian material in the pirated dataset Books3, which has been used in the training of several language models, including Meta's Llama. Sandemo is the most frequently occurring author from the Scandinavian publishers: 21 of the 47 titles in her much-loved series "The Legend of the Ice People" are included. The content is almost exclusively fiction, according to co-author Terje Colbjørnsen at BI. Publishing director Elizabeth Sellevold at Bladkompaniet, which manages Sandemo's series, was unaware of the matter and calls it a clear breach of copyright.

For the Nordics, the findings provide concrete ammunition in the ongoing copyright battle over AI training. Norway is preparing its AI act to implement the EU's AI regulation, under which developers of general-purpose AI models are already required to disclose their training data, and Norwegian author and publisher organisations now have documented examples to lean on. The survey is also likely to be read closely by the Swedish Writers' Union, which has pursued the same issue against the AI companies.

Source: Digi.no/NTB, Klassekampen

Hardware

DeepSeek is designing its own AI inference chip

Chinese AI lab DeepSeek is developing its own chip for inference – the stage where trained models answer users’ queries – according to a Reuters exclusive on Tuesday based on three sources familiar with the plans. The project is still at an early stage.

DeepSeek has explored the idea for about a year, held talks with companies in chip design, manufacturing and memory, and is trying to recruit experienced chip designers. The goal is to reduce its reliance on both Nvidia and domestic rival Huawei. That puts the company on the same path as OpenAI, which unveiled the Jalapeño inference chip with Broadcom in June, and Anthropic, which according to earlier reports is in talks with Samsung about a chip of its own.

For the Nordics, the news is yet another sign that inference is moving from Nvidia’s GPUs to specialized silicon – a shift that affects the calculus for Nvidia-heavy Nordic data center ventures such as Nscale in Narvik and EcoDataCenter in Falun. DeepSeek’s open models already run at Swedish and Norwegian developers, and if the company brings down its own operating costs, price pressure on AI services may continue – benefiting Nordic companies building on open models, though with the data-handling questions European regulators have previously raised about DeepSeek.

Source: Reuters/US News, Bloomberg, SiliconANGLE
Infrastructure

Amazon borrows at least $25 billion to fund its AI buildout

Amazon tapped the U.S. bond market on Tuesday for at least $25 billion earmarked for the company’s AI infrastructure. The debt is being issued in up to eight tranches with maturities from three to 40 years, with Barclays, Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan and Morgan Stanley managing the sale.

Demand was strong: the order book peaked near $62 billion before terms were tightened, settling around $41 billion – roughly 1.6 times the size of the deal. Amazon expects capital expenditures of about $200 billion this year, up from $131 billion in 2025, with most of the spending going to data centers, chips and other equipment. The company has already raised some $54 billion on bond markets in the U.S. and Europe this year plus $10 billion in Canada in June, but says, according to CNBC, that this will be its last issuance of the year.

For the Nordics, the deal confirms that the AI race is increasingly funded with borrowed money – precisely the trend Norway’s oil fund warned about in its bubble warning in June. Nordic institutions such as the oil fund, Sweden’s AP funds and the insurers are major buyers of U.S. corporate bonds and thus carry a growing share of the risk if AI revenues are slow to materialize. At the same time, Amazon Web Services has operated a cloud region in Sweden since 2018, so part of the investment wave may well land on Nordic soil and in Nordic power grids.

Source: CNBC, Bloomberg, SiliconANGLE

Microsoft cuts 4,800 jobs as AI spending hits record levels

Microsoft announced 4,800 layoffs on Monday, just over 2 percent of its global workforce. Gaming takes the hardest hit: 3,200 of the positions will disappear within Xbox through fiscal year 2027, as the division prepares to spin off game studios and the sales organization is restructured.

The cuts come as the company lifts spending to record levels: about $190 billion in capital expenditures for calendar year 2026, an increase of more than 60 percent and far above the roughly $155 billion analysts had expected. Microsoft thereby joins the list of tech giants partly funding the AI buildout with layoffs – around 150,000 people have lost their jobs in the industry in the first half of 2026 alone, according to tallies compiled by U.S. media.

For the Nordics, the Xbox overhaul raises questions about Stockholm-based Mojang Studios, the Minecraft maker Microsoft has owned since 2014, which sits within that very gaming division. Microsoft is also one of Sweden’s largest cloud players with data centers in Gävle, Sandviken and Staffanstorp – and the pattern of AI investment running in parallel with layoffs gives unions such as Sweden’s Unionen and Norway’s Tekna fresh material for this autumn’s discussions about AI’s impact on jobs.

Nordkraft and Nscale form operations company for the Narvik AI data center

Norwegian power company Nordkraft and AI infrastructure firm Nscale are forming the jointly owned Nordscale Operations AS, which will run operations at Nscale’s AI data center in the Narvik region. Nscale Norway will own 51 percent and Nordkraft 49 percent, according to Monday’s announcement.

The two have worked together since the data center project began in 2025, when Nordkraft delivered operational services during the construction phase. The new company takes over operations as the facility comes online during 2026 and is now recruiting for a range of operations, technical and corporate roles in Narvik. Local businesses will also be engaged for everything from logistics and maintenance to snow removal.

For the Nordics, this is a concrete example of the AI buildout starting to leave a local footprint in the north. Narvik’s power surplus and cold climate have made the town the hub of Nscale’s venture, backed by Aker and linked to OpenAI’s European expansion, and the operations company means jobs and contracts for businesses across northern Norway. For Narvik municipality and Nordland county, the question now is how much of the value chain stays local as the hyperscale facilities grow.

Regulation

UN gathers 193 countries in Geneva for first global AI dialogue

The UN’s first Global Dialogue on AI Governance opened yesterday in Geneva and concludes today. All 193 member states, along with researchers, industry and civil society, are meeting at the Palexpo convention centre to discuss common rules for the technology – the first time this happens in a formal UN forum.

The basis is the preliminary report from the UN’s Independent International Scientific Panel on AI, made up of 40 researchers and published on July 1. The panel notes that the United States accounts for roughly three quarters of the computing power behind the world’s leading AI supercomputers and China for around 15 percent – and warns that the window for effective global governance remains open but may not stay that way for long. Secretary-General António Guterres warned at the opening of catastrophic harm if governance fails to keep pace, calling among other things for a global pledge to protect children from manipulative AI products.

For the Nordics, the forum is an opportunity to advance small states’ interest in common rules rather than a bilateral tug-of-war between the US and China. Sweden arrives with its first comprehensive AI strategy from February and Norway with KI Norge and a new AI act implementing the EU’s AI regulation – and for the supervisory authorities IMY and Datatilsynet, the panel’s recurring reports become an independent scientific basis to lean on.

Source: UN News, UN News (panel report), ITU
Research

Security researchers document first fully AI-driven ransomware attack

The threat research team at security firm Sysdig has documented what it describes as the first ransomware operation carried out entirely by an autonomous AI agent, without human direction. The attacker, named JadePuffer, handled the whole chain itself: intrusion, credential theft, lateral movement through the network, and encryption.

The agent exploited a known vulnerability in the developer tool Langflow to obtain cloud and API keys, moved on to a production server and encrypted 1,342 configuration items. The encryption key was never saved, so the victim cannot recover the data even by paying. The report was published on July 1 – just weeks after the cybersecurity chiefs of the Five Eyes countries jointly warned on June 22 that AI-driven attacks are expected to break through within months rather than years.

For the Nordics, the finding sharpens the urgency of warnings already issued by Norway’s NSM and Sweden’s MSB and CERT-SE about AI-augmented attacks. Companies like Telenor, DNB and Equinor, as well as Swedish public agencies in the middle of AI rollouts, must now expect attackers operating at machine speed – and treat exposed developer tools with LLM connections as one of the first attack surfaces.

Source: BankInfoSecurity, Computing, CBS News
Labour market

Weak US jobs report adds new fuel to the AI debate

The United States added just 57,000 new jobs in June – roughly half the 115,000 analysts expected and one of the weakest monthly figures in years, according to the report from the statistics agency BLS published on Thursday.

Unemployment nevertheless fell to 4.2 percent, but for the wrong reason: labour force participation dropped to 61.5 percent, the lowest level since March 2021. Professional and business services grew by 36,000 jobs and healthcare by 22,000, while leisure and hospitality lost 61,000. The BLS does not cite AI as a cause, but the report adds new fuel to the debate among economists over how much automation contributes to companies holding back on hiring despite healthy profits.

For the Nordics, summer figures from Statistics Sweden (SCB) and Norway’s NAV arrive later in July. Earlier Nordic data has pointed the other way – job ads with AI components are holding up in Sweden and Norway while overall volumes decline, and companies like Ericsson, Telenor and the major banks continue to recruit AI talent. The question is whether the American slowdown is a cyclical signal or an early sign of automation’s effect.

Source: BLS, CNBC, Morningstar

OpenAI’s new frontier model GPT-5.6 Sol will begin serving on Cerebras wafer-scale hardware during July at up to 750 tokens per second, the company says. Models

China’s Meituan releases open frontier-scale model trained entirely without Nvidia chips

Food delivery giant Meituan has released the language model LongCat-2.0 as open source under the permissive MIT license, South China Morning Post and VentureBeat report. The model has 1.6 trillion parameters and a one-million-token context window – and is, according to the company, the industry’s first at that scale trained entirely on Chinese chips.

Both training and inference ran on a cluster of more than 50,000 domestic AI accelerators, where earlier Chinese flagships such as DeepSeek relied on foreign chips for the training itself. LongCat-2.0 is a sparse mixture-of-experts model activating 33–56 billion parameters per token, and ahead of launch it topped the usage charts on the developer platform OpenRouter as an anonymous test model under the codename Owl Alpha. One caveat: at launch, the company had not yet published the model weights on Hugging Face.

For the Nordics, the release cuts both ways. The MIT license makes the model freely usable for Nordic companies and research environments such as NTNU’s NorwAI and AI Sweden, but Chinese origin raises the same sovereignty questions as dependence on American models. Proof that frontier training can now be done without Nvidia also shifts the calculus for Nordic data center ventures such as Nscale in Narvik and the EuroHPC systems in Kajaani and Linköping.

Unsealed emails show why the Pentagon and Anthropic parted ways

Court documents unsealed on July 2 in a federal court in California reveal the email exchange between Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei and the Pentagon’s research and engineering chief Emil Michael, according to reporting by the Wall Street Journal. The core question was whether the Department of Defense could use Claude for fully autonomous weapons systems and domestic surveillance without restrictions – not the model’s capabilities.

When Amodei pointed out that the contract language entirely removed the company’s red lines, that went unchallenged. The documents also show that Michael held shares in competitor Perplexity AI worth between 2 and 10 million dollars. The Congressional Research Service has published its own analysis of the dispute’s consequences.

For the Nordics, the question of vendors’ ethical guardrails is directly relevant as the Swedish Armed Forces, FMV and Norway’s Forsvaret procure AI support, and for Saab and Kongsberg, which themselves develop autonomous systems. Norway has also pushed at the UN for international regulation of autonomous weapons – the dispute shows how the same tug-of-war now plays out between states and AI companies.

The Research Council of Norway has received 112 outline proposals for new research centers on defense capability, security and preparedness, the council announced on June 30. Artificial intelligence and cybersecurity are among the most common themes in the proposals, alongside energy, health, ocean and transport.

Labour market

New report: companies that invest most in AI also hire the most

Companies that invest heavily in AI are growing their workforces rather than shrinking them, according to a new report from Ramp Economics Lab – fintech company Ramp’s in-house research arm – and Revelio Labs, which tracked AI spending and workforce data across nearly 22,000 American companies.

High-intensity AI adopters – those spending an average of 30 dollars per employee per month early on – grew headcount by 10.2 percent, and entry-level positions rose by 12 percent, countering the narrative that AI wipes out junior jobs. The growth shows up broadly across engineering, sales, finance and customer service roles. But companies that stop at pilot projects and subscriptions without sustained investment see no corresponding gains – the gap between those genuinely transforming and those merely experimenting is widening.

For the Nordics, the starting position is strong: according to Statistics Sweden (SCB), 35 percent of Swedish companies use AI – tied for third in the EU with Belgium, behind Denmark at 42 percent and Finland – against an EU average of 20 percent. The report’s pattern also supports the Swedish AI Council’s line: jobs are changing faster than they are disappearing, and it is sustained commitment, not license purchases, that determines who wins with the technology.

Infrastructure

UN report puts numbers on AI’s environmental cost – Norway is already feeling the bill

The UN University's water institute UNU-INWEH has published a report quantifying AI's environmental footprint. By 2030, the world's AI data centers are expected to consume 945 terawatt-hours of electricity per year – nearly three times as much as Pakistan, Bangladesh and Nigeria use combined.

Water consumption could by the end of the decade match the basic annual needs of 1.3 billion people, and the land footprint is expected to exceed 14,500 square kilometers – roughly twice the Jakarta metropolitan area. The report stresses that everyday use, not training, accounts for 80 to 90 percent of energy demand, and that the footprint is determined by where the electricity is produced and from which energy sources.

In the Nordics the question is already concrete: according to NRK, Norway's telecom regulator Nkom had registered 111 data centers across 59 operators as of July 1, together drawing just under 2 percent of Norwegian electricity production. Hydropower gives Norway and Sweden a lower climate footprint per kilowatt-hour than most regions, but for grid operators Statnett and Svenska kraftnät the issue is now grid capacity and prioritization as the server halls in Skien, Luleå and elsewhere keep growing.

Regulation

Anthropic proposes a common scale for how dangerous AI jailbreaks are

On July 2, Anthropic published a first draft of an industry-wide framework for grading the severity of AI jailbreaks – techniques that trick a model into bypassing its safeguards. At the same time, the company details which cybersecurity uses the safety classifiers of its top model Fable 5 block and allow.

The scale, called Cyber Jailbreak Severity, runs from CJS-0 to CJS-4 and combines four factors: how much new capability a jailbreak gives an attacker, how broadly the technique works, how easily it can be turned into a working attack, and how easy it is to discover. The framework is being developed with Glasswing partners Amazon, Microsoft, and Google, and Anthropic has opened a HackerOne program where security researchers can report new jailbreaks. The classifiers divide cybersecurity use into four risk categories: outright malicious uses such as malware are blocked entirely, penetration testing and exploit development are blocked for now, low-risk uses are monitored and sometimes blocked out of caution, while defensive work such as log analysis, incident response, and secure coding is allowed.

For the Nordics, a common scale gives Sweden’s MSB and Norway’s NSM a shared language for assessing AI-related cyber threats, and one that IMY and Nkom can lean on in their new roles as AI supervisory authorities under the EU’s AI Act. For banks such as SEB and DNB, as well as Ericsson and Telenor – which both use AI in their security operations and must defend against AI-driven attacks – it becomes clearer which capabilities the top models are actually allowed to deliver.

Source: Anthropic, Anthropic (Redeploying Fable 5), VentureBeat
Infrastructure

State-run AI workshop opens – a common foundation for AI in Swedish government

On July 1, the AI Workshop (AI-verkstaden) opened its pilot operations – a first step toward shared AI infrastructure for Sweden’s public sector. The workshop is run by the Swedish Tax Agency and the Swedish Social Insurance Agency on behalf of the government, and aims to make it easier for agencies, municipalities, and regions to develop and use AI.

Ten pilot organizations are taking part at launch, including Region Halland and Mörbylånga municipality – deliberately chosen to span different sizes and levels of technical maturity. The pilots are testing AI services for redacting sensitive information, transcription, and digital assistants, with suppliers from both the public and private sectors. Support for onboarding, development, advice, and assistance is being built in parallel. The mandate was issued in January 2026, and the workshop is to be fully built out by 2030.

For the Nordics, this is a concrete Swedish attempt to solve a problem the whole region wrestles with: hundreds of municipalities and agencies that individually lack the muscle to procure and secure AI services on their own. If the model succeeds, it could become a template for Norway, where the Digitalisation Agency Digdir is pursuing similar questions, and it complements the Nordic ministerial declaration on AI cooperation that Sweden, Norway, and Denmark agreed on earlier this summer.

Quantum computer maker IQM completed its combination with Real Asset Acquisition Corp on July 1, and on July 2 the stock began trading on Nasdaq in New York under the ticker IQMX. That makes the Finnish company the first European quantum computing company on the exchange.

Research

Norwegians’ AI use nearly doubles – but trust remains low

The share of Norwegians who have used AI rose from 28 percent in 2023 to 52 percent in 2025, according to a new study from the Institute for Social Research in Oslo. At the same time, AI remains the actor the population trusts the least – even though trust in many traditional institutions has declined over the same period.

Researchers Øyvind Bugge Solheim and Bernard Enjolras interviewed representative samples of Norwegians in both 2023 and 2025 about knowledge, use, trust, hopes, and concerns around the technology. The picture that emerges is a population quickly adopting AI but doing so with clear ambivalence: optimism remains, but concerns about the consequences for democracy, power dynamics, and critical thinking have grown. The researchers conclude that the concerns about democracy and information in particular should weigh heavily in further policy development.

For the Nordics, the findings land in the middle of Norway’s AI debate, with Nkom just appointed the country’s AI watchdog and the Storting preparing the Norwegian AI Act. The pattern mirrors Swedish surveys from the University of Gothenburg, where a majority of the population now uses AI. The trust gap is thus a shared Nordic challenge: usage is outpacing confidence, putting pressure on both Oslo and Stockholm to show the technology can be governed.

Models

Anthropic brings its top model Fable 5 back globally after the US lifts export controls

Anthropic is once again making its most capable model, Claude Fable 5, available globally from July 1. The move follows the US government’s decision on June 30 to lift the export controls imposed on June 12 that had forced the company to suspend both Fable 5 and the more powerful Mythos 5 for all users.

The trigger was a so-called jailbreak: Amazon researchers had found a way to bypass Fable 5’s safeguards and get the model to identify – and in one case demonstrate how to exploit – a software vulnerability. Anthropic has now trained a new safety classifier that blocks the described technique in more than 99 percent of cases and instead routes blocked requests to Opus 4.8. Together with Amazon, Microsoft and Google, the company is also developing a shared industry framework for grading the severity of AI jailbreaks, and is deepening its collaboration with the US government – including pre-release review of frontier models – tied to a June 2 executive order on AI. Through July 7, Fable 5 is included for up to half of weekly usage limits on Pro, Max, Team and select enterprise accounts.

For the Nordics, access to Anthropic’s strongest model is back in Claude.ai, Claude Code and Cowork – a relief for developers, research environments such as WASP and NTNU, and companies that have built workflows around the model. But the nearly three-week suspension, during which Nordic users were locked out because the export order targeted foreign nationals, was a concrete reminder that Washington can cut off access to US frontier models almost overnight. That strengthens the case for the Nordics to build their own compute, an issue now being pushed in both Stockholm and Oslo.

Hardware

Etched exits stealth with its Sohu chip – aiming to challenge Nvidia on AI inference

US chip challenger Etched stepped out of stealth on June 30, revealing that it has raised around 800 million dollars and booked roughly one billion dollars in orders, at a valuation of about 5 billion dollars. Its chip, Sohu, is built for one thing only: running transformer models, the architecture behind today’s language models.

Instead of general-purpose GPUs, Etched etches the transformer architecture directly into the silicon, manufactured on TSMC’s N4P process. The company claims – without independent verification – that a server with eight Sohu chips can handle around 500,000 tokens per second on Meta’s Llama 70B and can replace up to 160 of Nvidia’s H100 GPUs. The first systems are due to ship this summer. Investors include Jane Street and a fund tied to TSMC, and the company was founded in 2022 by Harvard dropouts Gavin Uberti and Robert Wachen. Specialized inference chips are emerging just as Nvidia’s GPUs, memory and packaging are expensive and sold out – TSMC’s CEO warns the shortage will last for years.

For the Nordics, cheaper and more specialized inference could over time change the economics of the region’s AI factories. Stargate Norway in Narvik, Sferical AI in Linköping and Telenor’s AI Factory are all built around Nvidia’s chips today; lower inference costs and less dependence on scarce GPUs would make it cheaper for Ericsson, SEB, DNB and Equinor to run AI at scale. At the same time, early specialized chips carry risk: if model architectures shift, hardware locked to transformer models could age quickly.

Debate

Nordic AI sovereignty in focus as the US tightens access to top models

This summer’s US export reversal – when Anthropic’s Fable 5 and Mythos 5 were for a time shut off to foreign nationals – and a June 2 executive order giving the US government advance oversight of new frontier models have put the Nordics’ dependence on American AI back on the agenda. Compute the region controls itself is moving to the center of the debate.

The Nordics already have building blocks. Sferical AI is a sovereign supercomputer in Linköping backed by Ericsson, Saab, AstraZeneca, SEB and Wallenberg. Stargate Norway in Narvik – a partnership between OpenAI, Nscale and Aker – is aiming for 100,000 of Nvidia’s GPUs by the end of 2026, powered by renewable energy. Telenor’s AI Factory keeps customer data within Norway’s borders. The WASP research program and environments such as NTNU are building Nordic access to both models and talent. But most of it still rests on American chips – and, for the very strongest models, on American providers.

For the Nordics, the events sharpen a strategic question for decision-makers in both the Riksdag and the Storting: how much of the region’s AI future should rest on models Washington can switch off. Regulators IMY and Datatilsynet must at the same time weigh the EU’s AI Act – where the high-risk rules have now been pushed toward late 2027 – against an American approach in which the state reviews releases in advance. For Norway’s oil fund and Sweden’s AP funds, heavily exposed to US AI stocks, sovereignty is also a question of financial risk. Expect more voices calling for European and Nordic models, compute and rules.
