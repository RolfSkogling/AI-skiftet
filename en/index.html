Physical AI

The humanoids leave the stage – now they load bags and build cars

It is no longer just trade-show demos. Japan Airlines has begun testing humanoid robots in ground handling at Tokyo’s Haneda Airport – to load baggage and clean cabins. The 132 cm, 35 kg robots are based on models from China’s Unitree, in partnership with GMO AI & Robotics.

The trial runs for two years and is driven by acute labour shortages in an ageing economy. The same week, German industrial group Schaeffler went all in: a binding agreement with UK firm Humanoid for 1 000–2 000 wheeled humanoids across its plants by 2032, with the first units live in Germany around the turn of the year. In the background, Boston Dynamics’ Atlas is sequencing car parts at Hyundai and Agility Digit has already handled over 100 000 totes in warehouses.

For the Nordics this hits close to home. Norway’s 1X builds its home robot NEO in Moss and is rolling out the first units this year, while ABB Robotics and Denmark’s Universal Robots and MiR supply industrial arms and autonomous trucks across the region. The question now reaches Saab’s and Kongsberg’s autonomous systems, airports such as Swedavia and Avinor, and robotics research at Sintef and NTNU – when does the humanoid become a tool on Nordic worksites?

Source: CNBC, New Atlas, DC Velocity
Regulation

As the EU brakes, Norway sticks to its own AI law for late summer

While the EU on 7 May agreed to postpone the heaviest high-risk rules of the AI Act until late 2027, Norway is holding its course toward a national AI law of its own. Minister of Digitalisation Karianne Tung is aiming for the law to enter into force in late summer 2026, roughly when the main part of the AI Act starts to apply in the EU.

The bill has been out for consultation and was originally meant to reach the Storting around Easter, but the work has been delayed because negotiations with the EU on the necessary EEA adjustments are taking longer than expected. In parallel, the government is establishing ”KI Norge”, a national arena for responsible AI, and a regulatory sandbox at the Norwegian Digitalisation Agency where companies can test solutions under supervision.

For the Nordics the pace is becoming a point of contention. Norway risks moving ahead of the EU on parts of the framework, while Sweden takes its own path to implement the AI Act with IMY and the government in the driver’s seat. For the Storting, Datatilsynet and Nkom it is about building supervision before the rules apply – and for Nordic companies about avoiding clashing timelines on either side of the Swedish–Norwegian border.

Source: regjeringen.no, Altinget, Consilium
Research

Swedish and Canadian hospitals link up AI for patient benefit

A new Swedish–Canadian collaboration aims to move AI from isolated pilot projects into everyday clinical practice. Unity Health Toronto is joining forces with three Swedish university hospitals – Karolinska, Sahlgrenska and Skåne – in an initiative coordinated by AI Sweden and financed by Vinnova.

The point is to share the hardest part: how AI support goes from a promising demo to something actually used in the patient encounter, with the right validation and trust. At the same time, Swedish medical research is taking another step: Karolinska Institutet is part of several of the excellence clusters just selected by the Swedish Research Council and Vinnova, where AI-guided modelling of the human body and generative vaccine development take centre stage.

For the Nordics, healthcare is one of the areas where AI most quickly meets ordinary people. When Karolinska, Sahlgrenska and Skåne share methods and failure modes with Canadian hospitals, they build a template that Norway’s Helse-Norge, Sintef and Oslo University Hospital can follow too – while IMY and Datatilsynet watch over the proper handling of patient data.

Source: Vinnova, Karolinska Institutet
Hardware

Not the chip but the packaging – TSMC’s CoWoS becomes AI’s new bottleneck

What is slowing the AI build-out is not the silicon itself, but the art of packing it together. TSMC’s advanced packaging technology CoWoS has become the single hardest bottleneck in the 2026 AI supply chain – and Nvidia has already booked around 60% of the capacity.

At its technology forum on 14 May, deputy COO Kevin Zhang highlighted a new keyword, ”COUPE”, bringing optics closer to the compute die to cut latency further. TSMC is building new packaging capacity in Arizona before 2029, but the shortage of both CoWoS and HBM memory is expected to last until at least 2027 – whoever is not first in line waits for their GPUs.

For the Nordics this is the hidden limit on the AI dreams. The billion-euro builds – EuroHPC’s AI factories in Sweden and Finland, Nscale in Narvik, EcoDataCenter’s partnership with Mistral and atNorth – depend entirely on Nvidia’s cards actually being delivered on time. If packaging does not pick up, Nordic sovereign-AI bets, and Ericsson’s plans for AI-native 6G, risk standing with empty server halls.

Source: CNBC, DigiTimes

Infrastructure

Power, not chips, becomes AI's bottleneck – Nordic grids under heavy strain

It is no longer compute that runs short first, but electricity. In the Nordics the pressure has grown so intense that Denmark's state grid operator Energinet has since March paused new connection agreements after the queue of planned projects reached a staggering 60 gigawatts – nearly ten times the country's normal peak demand of around 7 gigawatts.

Data centres account for roughly 14 gigawatts of that queue. The same pattern is visible globally: on May 18, US utility NextEra Energy agreed to buy Dominion for about $67 billion – the largest utility merger in US history – explicitly to meet the thirst of AI data centres. Analysts expect data centres to consume 15–25% of US electricity by 2030. More compute does not help if the grid cannot deliver.

For the Nordics this cuts both ways. The region's cheap hydro and wind power have made it Europe's data-centre hub – Nscale's multi-billion build-out in Narvik, atNorth and EcoDataCenter's partnership with Mistral – but the grids cannot keep pace. Statnett, Svenska kraftnät and Finland's Fingrid must now choose between industry, electrification and AI halls, a politically charged trade-off that has reached the Swedish Energy Agency, Vinnova and Norway's Ministry of Energy.

Source: Fortune, Tom's Hardware, CNBC
Quantum

US pours $2 billion into quantum – and takes equity stakes

The US Commerce Department announced on May 21 that it will distribute more than $2 billion in CHIPS incentives to nine quantum-computing companies – and, as a condition, take a minority stake in each recipient. It is one of the clearest signs yet that quantum technology is being treated as a national priority, on par with semiconductors.

IBM is the anchor with $1 billion to build "Anderson," what the company calls America's first pure-play quantum wafer foundry, matching the federal commitment dollar for dollar. D-Wave, Rigetti, Infleqtion, PsiQuantum, Quantinuum and Atom Computing each receive about $100 million, while Diraq gets up to $38 million and GlobalFoundries up to $375 million for a new quantum-manufacturing division. The news lifted the whole sector: IonQ, which was not even among the recipients, jumped sharply on the news.

For the Nordics it sharpens the competitive picture. Finland's IQM is heading for a Nasdaq listing at around $1.8 billion, Chalmers' quantum centre WACQT and the spin-out Atlantic Quantum are building superconducting qubits, and the Niels Bohr Institute runs one of Europe's heaviest quantum programmes with the Novo Nordisk Foundation. When the US both subsidises and takes equity in its companies, the question becomes acute: how should the Nordics and the EU fund their own quantum capacity without falling behind?

Source: TechTimes, TheStreet

Anthropic's AI finds over 10,000 critical vulnerabilities in the world's software

Under its Project Glasswing initiative, Anthropic has used an as-yet-unreleased frontier model, Claude Mythos Preview, to hunt for vulnerabilities in society's most critical software. Together with around fifty partners, the company has found more than 10,000 high- or critical-severity vulnerabilities – many of them previously unknown "zero-days" in every major operating system and web browser.

The aim is defensive: to find and patch the holes before malicious actors can exploit them. The partners include heavyweight names such as AWS, Apple, Cisco, Google, JPMorgan and Microsoft. Yet the announcement sharpens an uncomfortable insight: the same capability that closes the holes can, in the wrong hands, be used to find ways in – a race between defence and attack that is now accelerating.

For the Nordics this lands squarely in critical infrastructure. Banks such as SEB, DNB and Nordea, telecom operators such as Telenor and Ericsson and a long line of public agencies all run the same global software now under scrutiny. Sweden's MSB and CERT-SE, Norway's NSM and Nkom, and the EU's NIS2 directive and AI Act face a new reality in which AI both raises the defence and lowers the threshold for attack.

Sweden appoints national coordinator for Swedish-language AI models

On May 21, the Swedish government appointed Katrin Westling Palm as national coordinator tasked with driving the development of Swedish-language AI models. The aim is to accelerate high-quality models that reflect the cultural, societal and legal contexts unique to Sweden.

Her mandate covers mapping needs – not least access to data – and proposing concrete measures, as well as analysing how rights holders should be compensated when copyrighted material is used in training. Culture Minister Parisa Liljestrand argues that a Swedish model strengthens the language and quality, but also the ability to detect undue information influence from foreign powers. Westling Palm has been director-general at the Government Offices since May 18 and previously led the Swedish Tax Agency and served as a senior expert on the AI Commission.

For the Nordics this is a clear sovereignty marker. Sweden builds on WASP, AI Sweden and the industrial venture Sferical AI in Linköping, and the coordinator is tasked with tracking developments across the rest of the Nordics and the EU. It mirrors Norway's six new AI research centres and the same minority-language question the National Library of Norway is wrestling with for Norwegian models – who should own and train the Nordic languages' models?

Hardware

AI's appetite drains the memory market – server prices surge

The race for AI capacity has triggered an acute shortage of working memory. Samsung, SK Hynix and Micron – together controlling more than 95 % of the world's DRAM output – have shifted capacity toward high-bandwidth memory (HBM) for AI accelerators, draining the supply of ordinary server and consumer memory.

According to analyst firm TrendForce, contract prices for server DRAM rose by more than 60 % in the first quarter, and TechInsights estimates that memory alone can raise the cost of an AI server by 10–25 %. Each gigabyte of HBM consumes three times as much silicon wafer as ordinary DDR5. TSMC's CEO has said supply will not meet demand until 2027, and Apple has already raised prices on part of the MacBook Pro lineup.

For the Nordics the timing bites. The major data-centre projects – EcoDataCenter and Mistral's billion-euro build in Sweden, atNorth across the Nordics and EuroHPC's AI factories in Sweden and Finland – will raise racks in the middle of the shortage, squeezing budgets. Meanwhile the next IT procurement for Ericsson, Volvo and municipalities grows more expensive, and Telenor's planned swap of tens of thousands of PCs ahead of the Windows 11 phase-out gets pricier.

Labour

AI cited behind record layoffs – while hiring slows

Outplacement firm Challenger, Gray & Christmas counts more than 49,000 AI-linked layoffs in the US so far in 2026 – roughly 16 % of the around 300,000 total cuts. In April, 21,490 layoffs were attributed to AI, the second consecutive month AI topped the reasons employers cited.

Tech-sector cuts ran 40 % higher than in the first quarter of 2025. Several economists stress that the biggest effect is not mass layoffs but the absence of hiring – especially of junior staff. Goldman Sachs estimates AI has shaved monthly employment growth by around 16,000 jobs. At the same time, analysts warn that "AI" is sometimes used as cover for cost cuts that are really about shifting budgets from staff to data centres and AI tools.

For the Nordics the figures land amid an ongoing debate. Norway leads Europe in AI use and Swedish employers are scaling up fast, sharpening the focus on the gap between adoption and reskilling. Unions such as Unionen and TCO in Sweden and LO and NHO in Norway are watching whether the American pattern – fewer entry-level jobs and quieter hiring – takes hold in the Nordics, a question that has also reached the Riksdag and the Storting.

Source: Challenger, Gray & Christmas, CBS News
Regulation

EU postpones the AI Act's high-risk rules to late 2027

On May 7, the EU institutions agreed on the "Digital Omnibus," the first package of amendments to the AI Act since 2024. Obligations for high-risk AI under Annex III are pushed from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027, and for AI embedded in regulated products to August 2, 2028.

Transparency rules on labelling synthetic content (Article 50) are also deferred, from August to December 2, 2026. The framework is tightened on one point: a new ban on AI that generates non-consensual intimate imagery and child sexual abuse material. The August 2, 2026 threshold for general-purpose AI models (GPAI) remains, however, when the Commission's enforcement powers – fines of up to 15 million euros or 3 % of turnover – take effect. Formal adoption is expected in June.

For the Nordics the delay brings breathing room. Swedish agencies, hospitals and municipalities that planned high-risk compliance for August gain more than an extra year, while Sweden's PTS – proposed as coordinating supervisory authority in SOU 2025:101 – and Norway's Datatilsynet and Nkom can align the EEA rules at a calmer pace. But the August 2 threshold for GPAI and the labelling rules due in December still affect the Riksdag, the Storting and Nordic AI builders directly.

Physical AI

Sony's robot beats the pros at table tennis – a new milestone for embodied AI

Sony AI has shown that its autonomous robot, Project Ace, can take on and beat both elite and professional table tennis players in real time. Several observers call it a breakthrough for "embodied" AI – systems that don't just reason in text but act physically in the world.

In the demonstrations the robot tracked the ball in 3D using nine high-speed cameras and event-based sensors, reacted up to 200 times a second and returned heavily spun shots. In repeated matches it has beaten both skilled amateurs and professional players. The key point is that the movements are learned through reinforcement learning rather than pre-programmed – the same principle driving today's robot foundation models.

For the Nordics it isn't the table tennis that matters, but the technology underneath: real-time perception and learned fine motor control. That is the same frontier driving industrial robotics at ABB and Denmark's Universal Robots, the NEO home robot at Norway's 1X, and robotics research at Sintef and NTNU. For Swedish and Norwegian manufacturing, healthcare and logistics it points toward robots that can handle fast, unpredictable tasks – not just repetitive ones.

Source: Sony AI, New Atlas, Interesting Engineering, TechRadar

Sweden and the US sign agreement on cooperation in AI, space and quantum technology

At the NATO meeting in Helsingborg on May 22, Sweden's foreign minister Maria Malmer Stenergard and US Secretary of State Marco Rubio signed a memorandum of understanding on bilateral technology cooperation – dubbed the "Technology Prosperity Deal". It is intended to open up expanded collaboration in research and development, including in artificial intelligence, space and quantum technology.

For the Nordics the timing is charged. The agreement comes just as the EU's AI Act approaches its August 2 threshold, and Ericsson welcomed it immediately. OpenAI model disproves 80-year-old Erdös conjecture – a first for AI cracking an open math problem

OpenAI said on May 20 that an internal reasoning model independently produced an original proof disproving a famous unsolved conjecture in discrete geometry, first posed by Paul Erdös in 1946. Several mathematicians describe it as the first time an AI has solved a prominent open problem on its own – rather than merely retrieving a known answer.

The problem concerns how many pairs of points can lie at exactly distance 1 when n points are placed in a plane; for nearly 80 years square grids were believed to be near-optimal. The model instead connected the geometry to algebraic number theory and found an infinite family of constructions that beats the grid. The result was set out in a companion paper co-signed by several leading mathematicians – among them Noga Alon, Fields medalist Timothy Gowers and Princeton's Will Sawin, who sharpened the bound further.

For the Nordics it lands in the middle of the "AI for science" push: Wallenberg's WASP, the data-driven life-science program DDLS and SciLifeLab at Karolinska, and the Berzelius supercomputer in Linköping. The rectors of Chalmers, KTH and Linköping have called for a national mobilization, and for Vinnova, the Research Council, NTNU and UiO the question becomes how autonomous "AI co-scientists" should be used and reviewed.

Source: OpenAI, TechCrunch, Scientific American, MIT Technology Review

Pope Leo XIV signed his first encyclical "Magnifica humanitas" on May 15, on safeguarding the human person in the age of artificial intelligence — it is published on May 25 and presented at the Vatican alongside a co-founder of Anthropic, exactly 135 years after the labour encyclical Rerum Novarum; the message that technology must remain subordinate to the human person and protect work, creativity and moral agency lands squarely in the Nordic debate on AI and working life

The Vatican confirmed on May 18 that Pope Leo XIV had signed his first encyclical, "Magnifica humanitas", already on May 15 — to the day 135 years after Leo XIII signed Rerum Novarum, the encyclical that founded Catholic social teaching on labour and workers' rights. The document, subtitled on protecting the human person in the time of artificial intelligence, is officially published on May 25 and presented in the synod hall by Cardinal Secretary of State Pietro Parolin together with a co-founder of Anthropic before the pope himself speaks. According to early reports, Leo XIV argues that technology must remain subordinate to the human person — not the reverse — and that AI systems should protect work, creativity and moral judgement. For the Nordics the encyclical lends ethical weight to an already ongoing debate. For the Church of Sweden and the Church of Norway — still the largest faith communities in their respective countries — Magnifica humanitas becomes a reference point as bishops' conferences and the Church Synod formulate positions on AI in pastoral care, education and working life. For Sweden's National Council on Medical Ethics (Smer), Norway's Technology Board and union centrals such as LO, TCO, Unionen and Akademikerförbundet SSR, the link to Rerum Novarum reinforces the argument that an "AI impact assessment before reorganisation" is a moral and not merely a contractual question — especially against the backdrop of the Klarna reversal and Microsoft AI chief Suleyman's 18-month forecast earlier this week. And for Anthropic, which just launched Claude for Small Business at Nordic SMEs, the public alliance with the Vatican is an unexpected legitimacy signal in a Nordic procurement environment where ethics and transparency weigh ever more heavily ahead of the AI Act's thresholds.

A federal jury in California threw out Elon Musk's lawsuit against OpenAI and Sam Altman on May 18 — the nine-member advisory jury needed less than two hours, Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers ruled that Musk waited too long (statute of limitations) and cleared Altman, Greg Brockman and OpenAI on all counts; Musk vows to appeal, and the outcome cements the for-profit structure that Nordic enterprise customers and the Norwegian oil fund must now reckon with

A federal court in Oakland decided Elon Musk's long-running lawsuit against OpenAI, Sam Altman and co-founder Greg Brockman on May 18. The nine-member advisory jury needed less than two hours to conclude that Musk had brought his claim too late — beyond the statute of limitations — when he sued in 2024, and Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers of the Northern District of California accordingly dismissed the case and cleared all defendants on every count. Musk's lawyers had argued that Altman treated the non-profit as a "shell" after the for-profit subsidiary was created in 2019, and that after the 10-billion-dollar Microsoft deal in 2023 OpenAI abandoned its commitments to open source and safety in order to "enrich investors and insiders". Musk wrote on X that he would appeal, calling the verdict a "calendar technicality". For the Nordics the consequences are concrete. For Norway's oil fund (Norges Bank Investment Management) and Sweden's AP pension funds — all heavy owners of Microsoft and Nvidia — the ruling reduces uncertainty around OpenAI's corporate governance, even as the question of a profit-driven AI giant's mission remains an ownership-stewardship matter. For Equinor, SEB, Telenor and other Nordic large customers building Codex workflows on their own infrastructure after last week's OpenAI–Dell deal, the ruling gives legal solidity to the vendor choice. And for KI Norge, which launches under Hans Christian Holte in August, and for IMY, the contrast with Anthropic's PBC structure — and the Vatican alliance the same week — is a reminder that corporate form and governance are becoming part of Nordic vendor due diligence under the AI Act.

Labor Market

Standard Chartered CEO Bill Winters said on May 19 that the bank will replace "lower-value human capital" with AI — around 7,800 back-office jobs, roughly 15 percent of its support-function staff, are to go by 2030, concentrated in Chennai, Bengaluru, Kuala Lumpur and Warsaw; the phrasing triggered a backlash and an internal walk-back as early as May 20, and confronts SEB, Swedbank, Handelsbanken, Nordea and DNB with the same question as the Klarna reversal

Standard Chartered CEO Bill Winters said on May 19 that the bank's AI push will erase thousands of roles as "lower-value human capital" is replaced by technology. The plan is to cut support-function staffing by about 15 percent by 2030 — roughly 7,800 of more than 52,000 back-office employees — concentrated at the offices in Chennai and Bengaluru in India, Kuala Lumpur and Warsaw. "It is replacing, in some cases, lower-value human capital with the financial capital and the investment capital we're putting in," Winters said. The remark drew sharp criticism on social media and from at least one former head of state, and as early as May 20 Winters issued an internal memo seeking to reassure staff: "I know this may be unsettling when reduced to simple headlines or a quote out of context." For the Nordics the episode is a mirror. For SEB, Swedbank, Handelsbanken, Nordea, DNB, Sparebank 1 and Storebrand — all of which are automating exactly back office, compliance and customer service at scale — the reaction shows how quickly blunt AI-savings rhetoric backfires, just days after Klarna's Sebastian Siemiatkowski admitted that "AI-first" had gone too far. For the Finansförbundet union in Sweden and Finansforbundet in Norway, "lower-value human capital" becomes a rallying cry in collective bargaining, with demands for transition agreements, reskilling via the TRR job-security council and AI impact assessment under the Swedish co-determination act (MBL). And for Finansinspektionen and Finanstilsynet, the question of operational resilience under DORA comes to the fore when critical banking functions are automated — competence must not be thinned out so far that supervision and incident response are weakened.

Infrastructure

A market report on May 19 puts Finland's data-center market on track for 5.8 billion dollars by 2031 at over 35 percent annual growth — atNorth, Borealis, Elisa, Equinix, Telia, Verne and Google are named as the drivers, while Mistral's 1.2-billion-euro build-out with EcoDataCenter in Sweden and EuroHPC's AI factories in both Sweden and Finland cement the Nordics as Europe's AI power hub; a primary Nordic angle that confronts Vinnova, the Swedish Energy Agency, Svenska kraftnät, Fingrid and Statnett with the grid and sovereignty question

A market analysis published on May 19 values Finland's data-center market at 5.8 billion dollars by 2031, at an annual growth rate of just over 35 percent, and names atNorth, Borealis Data Center, Elisa, Equinix, Telia, Verne and Google as the heavy investors. The report is the latest in a string of signals that the Nordics have become Europe's most sought-after location for AI compute: Mistral confirmed earlier this year a 1.2-billion-euro investment in Swedish infrastructure together with EcoDataCenter (operational 2027), Nebius is building one of Europe's largest AI factories in Finland, and EuroHPC selected seven host sites for European AI factories — among them both Sweden and Finland. Nvidia is also establishing AI technology centres in Sweden and Finland. The drivers are familiar: cheap hydropower, a cold climate and stable grids. For the Nordics the development makes the power question acute. For Svenska kraftnät, Finland's Fingrid and Norway's Statnett, data centres' capacity demand becomes a direct competitor to the electrification of heavy industry — SSAB's and Hybrit's fossil-free steel, battery manufacturing and electrified transport — at a time when connection queues are already long. For Vinnova, AI Sweden, the Swedish Energy Agency and Norway's NVE, the trade-off between attracting hyperscale data centres and securing sovereign compute for domestic players (Sferical AI in Linköping, Berzelius, WASP) is a strategic balancing act. For municipalities such as Luleå, Skellefteå, Boden and Stavanger the projects mean jobs and a tax base but also questions of land use and waste heat. And for Datatilsynet and IMY, where Nordic agencies' and banks' data are actually stored becomes decisive as cloud capacity grows at home but is owned by global players.
