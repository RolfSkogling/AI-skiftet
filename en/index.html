Models

Zuckerberg to staff: AI agents are progressing slower than expected

At an internal town hall on Thursday, Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg told employees that AI agent development has not accelerated the way the company expected over the past four months, according to a recording obtained by the Reuters news agency.

The admission carries weight. Meta has laid off around 8,000 employees this year – roughly one in ten corporate staff – and reassigned another 7,000 to AI groups, including a unit called Agent Transformation. Zuckerberg conceded that the bet has not yet come to fruition and that the cuts were not executed as cleanly as they should have been. Clearer returns on this year’s AI investments, up to 145 billion dollars, are now expected within three to six months.

For the Nordics, the remarks are a calibration point. Norway’s oil fund, a major Meta shareholder, has already warned of an AI bubble, and for companies like Telenor, DNB, Ericsson and SEB building their own agent strategies, the signal is to plan for longer timelines – if even Meta needs more time to turn agent hype into operations, the same applies to smaller players.

Source: TechCrunch, Reuters/US News, PYMNTS
Labour market

New report: companies that invest most in AI also hire the most

Companies that invest heavily in AI are growing their workforces rather than shrinking them, according to a new report from Ramp Economics Lab – fintech company Ramp’s in-house research arm – and Revelio Labs, which tracked AI spending and workforce data across nearly 22,000 American companies.

High-intensity AI adopters – those spending an average of 30 dollars per employee per month early on – grew headcount by 10.2 percent, and entry-level positions rose by 12 percent, countering the narrative that AI wipes out junior jobs. The growth shows up broadly across engineering, sales, finance and customer service roles. But companies that stop at pilot projects and subscriptions without sustained investment see no corresponding gains – the gap between those genuinely transforming and those merely experimenting is widening.

For the Nordics, the starting position is strong: according to Statistics Sweden (SCB), 35 percent of Swedish companies use AI – tied for third in the EU with Belgium, behind Denmark at 42 percent and Finland – against an EU average of 20 percent. The report’s pattern also supports the Swedish AI Council’s line: jobs are changing faster than they are disappearing, and it is sustained commitment, not license purchases, that determines who wins with the technology.

Source: TechCrunch, Ramp/PR Newswire, PYMNTS, SCB
Physical AI

Tesla’s Optimus line in Fremont is running – series production within weeks

Elon Musk this week shared a photo with the production team for the Optimus humanoid robot at the Fremont factory, a sign that the line is up and running, Teslarati reports. Series production of the third-generation robot is expected by observers to start in late July or August.

The line is being built on the floor space where the Model S and X were manufactured until May. Optimus V3 consists of around 10,000 unique parts, and Musk describes this year’s production rate as impossible to predict. The robot is not for sale to consumers – the first units will work in Tesla’s own factories, and analysts expect consumer deliveries in 2027–2028 at the earliest. Competitor Figure is already building one robot per hour at its BotQ factory.

For the Nordics, the race is no longer at a distance: Norway’s 1X Technologies begins delivering its home robot NEO to American customers this year for 20,000 dollars or 499 dollars a month, and as Swedish-Swiss ABB sells its robotics division to SoftBank for 5.4 billion dollars, a Nordic industrial legacy moves straight into the same humanoid race as Tesla and Figure.

During the football World Cup, NRK Finnmark published a humorous article in which Google’s language model Gemini acted as a football pundit, assessing Norway’s chances against France – including what an injury to Marcus Holmgren Pedersen would mean for the team.

The piece, bylined by editorial and news chief Rolf Jakobsen, has sparked strong internal reactions, reports the trade publication Journalisten. For the Nordics, the dispute puts a finger on public service media’s AI boundary in the middle of the World Cup summer and Sweden’s election year. NRK has its own AI guidelines requiring human oversight, and SVT and Sveriges Radio are following the same debate: when an AI gets to speak as an expert without other sources, it is credibility – public service media’s hard currency – that is at stake.

OpenAI proposes giving the US government a 5 percent ownership stake

OpenAI has proposed that the US government take a 5 percent ownership stake in the company, the Financial Times reports. The idea comes from CEO Sam Altman in early talks with the Trump administration, and the stake would be worth roughly $42.6 billion based on the $852 billion valuation from this spring's record funding round.

The concept is a broader arrangement in which Washington, through a state fund, would hold 5 percent of each of the leading American AI companies, modeled on the Alaska Permanent Fund – with returns from AI profits distributed to the public. It is unclear whether Anthropic, Google or Meta would agree to the arrangement, and formal implementation would likely require congressional approval. The proposal comes just days after Washington slowed the broad rollout of GPT-5.6.

For the Nordics, the template is familiar: the model of a state fund distributing resource windfalls is most associated with Norway's oil fund, the world's largest sovereign wealth fund and a major shareholder in both Microsoft and Nvidia – the same fund that recently warned of an AI bubble. For the Storting and Sweden's AP pension funds, the proposal raises a question of principle: how can the state share in AI profits without politics gaining influence over the models?

Anthropic in talks with Samsung to build its own AI chip

Anthropic is in talks with Samsung about developing and manufacturing its own AI chip, The Information and TechCrunch report. The discussions are at an early stage and concern manufacturing on Samsung's most advanced 2-nanometer process.

According to the reports, Anthropic has not yet decided what the chip will be used for or how powerful it will be. The move follows the industry pattern: OpenAI recently unveiled its first in-house inference chip, Jalapeño, together with Broadcom, and the consistent goal is to reduce dependence on Nvidia and push down the cost per computation.

For the Nordics, which are becoming Europe's compute hall for AI, the chip race determines what hardware fills the server halls in Luleå, Boden and Skien. For heavy users such as SEB, DNB, Ericsson and Telenor, more chip suppliers could eventually push down the price per AI call, and the signal is clear: the major AI companies are now taking control of the entire stack, from model to silicon.

Infrastructure

UN report puts numbers on AI’s environmental cost – Norway is already feeling the bill

The UN University's water institute UNU-INWEH has published a report quantifying AI's environmental footprint. By 2030, the world's AI data centers are expected to consume 945 terawatt-hours of electricity per year – nearly three times as much as Pakistan, Bangladesh and Nigeria use combined.

Water consumption could by the end of the decade match the basic annual needs of 1.3 billion people, and the land footprint is expected to exceed 14,500 square kilometers – roughly twice the Jakarta metropolitan area. The report stresses that everyday use, not training, accounts for 80 to 90 percent of energy demand, and that the footprint is determined by where the electricity is produced and from which energy sources.

In the Nordics the question is already concrete: according to NRK, Norway's telecom regulator Nkom had registered 111 data centers across 59 operators as of July 1, together drawing just under 2 percent of Norwegian electricity production. Hydropower gives Norway and Sweden a lower climate footprint per kilowatt-hour than most regions, but for grid operators Statnett and Svenska kraftnät the issue is now grid capacity and prioritization as the server halls in Skien, Luleå and elsewhere keep growing.

Source: UN News, NRK, UNU-INWEH

Regulation

Anthropic proposes a common scale for how dangerous AI jailbreaks are

On July 2, Anthropic published a first draft of an industry-wide framework for grading the severity of AI jailbreaks – techniques that trick a model into bypassing its safeguards. At the same time, the company details which cybersecurity uses the safety classifiers of its top model Fable 5 block and allow.

The scale, called Cyber Jailbreak Severity, runs from CJS-0 to CJS-4 and combines four factors: how much new capability a jailbreak gives an attacker, how broadly the technique works, how easily it can be turned into a working attack, and how easy it is to discover. The framework is being developed with Glasswing partners Amazon, Microsoft, and Google, and Anthropic has opened a HackerOne program where security researchers can report new jailbreaks. The classifiers divide cybersecurity use into four risk categories: outright malicious uses such as malware are blocked entirely, penetration testing and exploit development are blocked for now, low-risk uses are monitored and sometimes blocked out of caution, while defensive work such as log analysis, incident response, and secure coding is allowed.

For the Nordics, a common scale gives Sweden’s MSB and Norway’s NSM a shared language for assessing AI-related cyber threats, and one that IMY and Nkom can lean on in their new roles as AI supervisory authorities under the EU’s AI Act. For banks such as SEB and DNB, as well as Ericsson and Telenor – which both use AI in their security operations and must defend against AI-driven attacks – it becomes clearer which capabilities the top models are actually allowed to deliver.

Source: Anthropic, Anthropic (Redeploying Fable 5), VentureBeat
Infrastructure

State-run AI workshop opens – a common foundation for AI in Swedish government

On July 1, the AI Workshop (AI-verkstaden) opened its pilot operations – a first step toward shared AI infrastructure for Sweden’s public sector. The workshop is run by the Swedish Tax Agency and the Swedish Social Insurance Agency on behalf of the government, and aims to make it easier for agencies, municipalities, and regions to develop and use AI.

Ten pilot organizations are taking part at launch, including Region Halland and Mörbylånga municipality – deliberately chosen to span different sizes and levels of technical maturity. The pilots are testing AI services for redacting sensitive information, transcription, and digital assistants, with suppliers from both the public and private sectors. Support for onboarding, development, advice, and assistance is being built in parallel. The mandate was issued in January 2026, and the workshop is to be fully built out by 2030.

For the Nordics, this is a concrete Swedish attempt to solve a problem the whole region wrestles with: hundreds of municipalities and agencies that individually lack the muscle to procure and secure AI services on their own. If the model succeeds, it could become a template for Norway, where the Digitalisation Agency Digdir is pursuing similar questions, and it complements the Nordic ministerial declaration on AI cooperation that Sweden, Norway, and Denmark agreed on earlier this summer.

Source: Skatteverket, Börskollen
Quantum

Finland’s IQM hits Nasdaq as Europe’s first listed quantum computing company

Quantum computer maker IQM completed its combination with Real Asset Acquisition Corp on July 1, and on July 2 the stock began trading on Nasdaq in New York under the ticker IQMX. That makes the Finnish company the first European quantum computing company on the exchange.

The deal, including a private placement, provides IQM with around 198.7 million euros – roughly 233.5 million dollars – to fund commercialization and global expansion. IQM was spun out of Aalto University and the research institute VTT and builds quantum computers based on superconducting qubits; the company has delivered systems to research environments in several countries. The listing comes just weeks after competitor Quantinuum raised 1.68 billion dollars in its IPO – capital is now pouring into the quantum sector.

For the Nordics, the listing is proof that the region’s quantum ventures can go all the way to the capital markets. IQM is part of the same Nordic ecosystem as Chalmers and the Wallenberg center WACQT, which was recently tasked with coordinating Sweden’s national quantum center, and fresh capital in Espoo strengthens European access to quantum machines – an issue that both Vinnova and the Research Council of Norway have singled out as strategic.

Research

Norwegians’ AI use nearly doubles – but trust remains low

The share of Norwegians who have used AI rose from 28 percent in 2023 to 52 percent in 2025, according to a new study from the Institute for Social Research in Oslo. At the same time, AI remains the actor the population trusts the least – even though trust in many traditional institutions has declined over the same period.

Researchers Øyvind Bugge Solheim and Bernard Enjolras interviewed representative samples of Norwegians in both 2023 and 2025 about knowledge, use, trust, hopes, and concerns around the technology. The picture that emerges is a population quickly adopting AI but doing so with clear ambivalence: optimism remains, but concerns about the consequences for democracy, power dynamics, and critical thinking have grown. The researchers conclude that the concerns about democracy and information in particular should weigh heavily in further policy development.

For the Nordics, the findings land in the middle of Norway’s AI debate, with Nkom just appointed the country’s AI watchdog and the Storting preparing the Norwegian AI Act. The pattern mirrors Swedish surveys from the University of Gothenburg, where a majority of the population now uses AI. The trust gap is thus a shared Nordic challenge: usage is outpacing confidence, putting pressure on both Oslo and Stockholm to show the technology can be governed.

Models

Anthropic brings its top model Fable 5 back globally after the US lifts export controls

Anthropic is once again making its most capable model, Claude Fable 5, available globally from July 1. The move follows the US government’s decision on June 30 to lift the export controls imposed on June 12 that had forced the company to suspend both Fable 5 and the more powerful Mythos 5 for all users.

The trigger was a so-called jailbreak: Amazon researchers had found a way to bypass Fable 5’s safeguards and get the model to identify – and in one case demonstrate how to exploit – a software vulnerability. Anthropic has now trained a new safety classifier that blocks the described technique in more than 99 percent of cases and instead routes blocked requests to Opus 4.8. Together with Amazon, Microsoft and Google, the company is also developing a shared industry framework for grading the severity of AI jailbreaks, and is deepening its collaboration with the US government – including pre-release review of frontier models – tied to a June 2 executive order on AI. Through July 7, Fable 5 is included for up to half of weekly usage limits on Pro, Max, Team and select enterprise accounts.

For the Nordics, access to Anthropic’s strongest model is back in Claude.ai, Claude Code and Cowork – a relief for developers, research environments such as WASP and NTNU, and companies that have built workflows around the model. But the nearly three-week suspension, during which Nordic users were locked out because the export order targeted foreign nationals, was a concrete reminder that Washington can cut off access to US frontier models almost overnight. That strengthens the case for the Nordics to build their own compute, an issue now being pushed in both Stockholm and Oslo.

Hardware

Etched exits stealth with its Sohu chip – aiming to challenge Nvidia on AI inference

US chip challenger Etched stepped out of stealth on June 30, revealing that it has raised around 800 million dollars and booked roughly one billion dollars in orders, at a valuation of about 5 billion dollars. Its chip, Sohu, is built for one thing only: running transformer models, the architecture behind today’s language models.

Instead of general-purpose GPUs, Etched etches the transformer architecture directly into the silicon, manufactured on TSMC’s N4P process. The company claims – without independent verification – that a server with eight Sohu chips can handle around 500,000 tokens per second on Meta’s Llama 70B and can replace up to 160 of Nvidia’s H100 GPUs. The first systems are due to ship this summer. Investors include Jane Street and a fund tied to TSMC, and the company was founded in 2022 by Harvard dropouts Gavin Uberti and Robert Wachen. Specialized inference chips are emerging just as Nvidia’s GPUs, memory and packaging are expensive and sold out – TSMC’s CEO warns the shortage will last for years.

For the Nordics, cheaper and more specialized inference could over time change the economics of the region’s AI factories. Stargate Norway in Narvik, Sferical AI in Linköping and Telenor’s AI Factory are all built around Nvidia’s chips today; lower inference costs and less dependence on scarce GPUs would make it cheaper for Ericsson, SEB, DNB and Equinor to run AI at scale. At the same time, early specialized chips carry risk: if model architectures shift, hardware locked to transformer models could age quickly.

Source: Bloomberg, Tech Times, The Next Web

Nordic AI sovereignty in focus as the US tightens access to top models

This summer’s US export reversal – when Anthropic’s Fable 5 and Mythos 5 were for a time shut off to foreign nationals – and a June 2 executive order giving the US government advance oversight of new frontier models have put the Nordics’ dependence on American AI back on the agenda. Compute the region controls itself is moving to the center of the debate.

But most of it still rests on American chips – and, for the very strongest models, on American providers.

For the Nordics, the events sharpen a strategic question for decision-makers in both the Riksdag and the Storting: how much of the region’s AI future should rest on models Washington can switch off. Regulators IMY and Datatilsynet must at the same time weigh the EU’s AI Act – where the high-risk rules have now been pushed toward late 2027 – against an American approach in which the state reviews releases in advance. Expect more voices calling for European and Nordic models, compute and rules.

Anthropic releases Claude Sonnet 5 – near-flagship power at half the price

Anthropic yesterday, June 30, released Claude Sonnet 5, a new mid-tier model it calls its most agentic yet. It can plan, use tools such as browsers and terminals, and run autonomously for extended stretches.

Sonnet 5 lands close to the flagship Opus 4.8 on reasoning, tool use, coding and knowledge work, yet costs less than half as much. On an agentic coding test it scored 63.2 percent, against 69.2 for Opus 4.8 and 58.1 for its predecessor Sonnet 4.6. Introductory pricing is 2 dollars per million input tokens and 10 dollars per million output tokens – a level that holds until August 31, when it rises to 3 and 15 dollars respectively. The model becomes the default for free and Pro users.

For the Nordics, the lower price matters most. A near-top-tier agentic model at under half the cost makes it cheaper for Swedish and Norwegian developers, startups and large firms such as SEB, DNB and Ericsson to run AI agents at scale – and lowers the barrier for public-sector pilots in municipalities and regions that cost has held back. At the same time, dependence on a handful of American model providers keeps growing, a strategic vulnerability regularly flagged in both Stockholm and Oslo.

Amazon passes one million warehouse robots – new AI model steers the fleet

Amazon has passed one million robots in its warehouses worldwide and is at the same time launching a new AI model, DeepFleet, to steer the entire robot fleet. The millionth robot was delivered to a fulfilment centre in Japan, part of a network of more than 300 sites.

Labour market

Swedish AI council: jobs are changing faster than they disappear

AI is reshaping the Swedish labour market above all through new tasks and skill requirements – not by making jobs vanish. That is the core message of a new assessment from the Labour Market AI Council, a collaboration led by AI Sweden.

The council, which includes Akavia, Almega, DIK, Fremia, Svensk Handel and Unionen, notes that Sweden is among the countries most exposed to AI, but that most occupations are so far complemented rather than replaced. As AI is woven into workflows, the value of domain knowledge, judgement, communication and the ability to decide when human and machine work best together instead rises. A survey by Unionen shows that two-thirds of member companies have already introduced AI – more than a doubling in two years.

For the Nordics the kinship is clear. The council urges the state to strengthen incentives for employers' skills investment, especially where time and resources for learning are scarce – an issue that applies just as much to Norway, where NHO and LO track the Swedish model. For Nordic workers the report points to a labour market where continuing education, not layoffs, becomes the decisive battle of the coming years.

Source: AI Sweden, Almega, Akavia
Research

Swedish-Norwegian project teaches AI to ease healthcare with safer decisions

A Swedish-Norwegian research project has examined how AI-based decision support can make healthcare more accessible, safe and resource-efficient. The Kontiki project is led by the University of Borås and Østfold University College and runs with support from Interreg Sweden–Norway.

In one sub-project, machine learning shows clearly better accuracy than today's routines in assessing deep vein thrombosis – a blood clot in the deep veins – which can reduce the need for hospital visits and resource-intensive examinations. In another, researchers at Chalmers have built a model that assesses how severely injured a trauma patient is already at the scene, with good accuracy tested in both Sweden and Norway.

For the Nordics the point is that the benefit arises where staff shortages are greatest. Rural areas in both Sweden and Norway struggle with long distances to hospitals and stretched staffing, and accurate decision support can relieve emergency departments and ambulances. That the project is run jointly across the border also shows how the Swedish-Norwegian research axis – with Karolinska, Chalmers, NTNU and Sintef as heavy nodes – offers a way for the Nordics to build their own healthcare AI rather than only import finished systems.

Hardware

AI's real bottleneck is memory and packaging – not the GPUs

The shortage of AI compute is less and less about the graphics chips themselves and more about two links further down the chain: high-bandwidth memory (HBM) and the advanced packaging that ties the chips together. Both are effectively sold out for 2026.

The next generation of memory, HBM4, is now ramping up. SK Hynix – expected to supply around two-thirds of Nvidia's HBM4 for the coming Vera Rubin platform – has at the same time chosen to slow its HBM4 build-out in order to capture higher margins on conventional DDR5 memory. Samsung is targeting around 250,000 wafers per month by the end of the year, while Micron is exiting consumer memory entirely to prioritize AI data centers. The tightest choke point sits at TSMC, whose advanced CoWoS packaging is fully booked all the way into 2027 – only then is supply expected to meet demand.

For the Nordics these bottlenecks determine how quickly the region's AI factories can be filled with capacity. Stargate Norway in Narvik, Sferical AI in Linköping and EcoDataCenter's facilities are all built around Nvidia chips – and their real build-out pace is governed by when memory and packaging can be had, not by how many GPUs have been ordered. For large users such as Ericsson, Volvo, SEB and Equinor, the tighter supply means the price of compute could stay high well into 2027, slowing Nordic AI investments that compete with US hyperscalers for the same memory capacity.

Source: TrendForce, Tech Times, Datacenter Dynamics
Quantum

The quantum computer moves into the AI factory as Quantinuum and HPE link the technologies

Quantum computers are beginning to be tied together with the supercomputers and AI systems that already drive research. On June 22, Quantinuum and Hewlett Packard Enterprise (HPE) presented a strategic collaboration to integrate quantum computing with high-performance computing and AI.

The collaboration is part of a broader movement. A week earlier, on June 15, HPE brought on eight quantum partners – including Finland's IQM – to expand quantum capacity in its data centers. In parallel, Sandia National Laboratories and Quantinuum validated a trapped-ion machine with 98 qubits, Helios, on June 18, and Duke University and IonQ demonstrated distributed entanglement across a three-node quantum network on June 20. Together the steps point toward quantum computers working with, rather than replacing, today's AI infrastructure.

For the Nordics, quantum is one of the few areas where the region sits at the absolute frontier. Finland's IQM securing a place in HPE's initiative shows that Nordic quantum hardware counts internationally, and Sweden's new national quantum center, led by Chalmers and WACQT, is building exactly the expertise needed as quantum and AI grow together. For Ericsson (quantum-safe encryption), SEB and Handelsbanken (risk calculations) and Saab (sensors), the question is no longer whether quantum is coming, but whether Nordic players connect in time to the European infrastructure now being built – through LUMI-Q among others.

Infrastructure

The Nordics emerge as Europe’s data center hub for AI – hydropower lures the gigafactories

The Nordic region is emerging as Europe’s most sought-after location for AI data centers. In a short time, several billion-dollar projects have taken shape: Stargate Norway in Narvik, backed by Nscale, Aker and OpenAI, and the newly launched Fossefall, which aims to build clean AI capacity on Nordic hydropower.

The question of sovereignty – who controls the capacity when OpenAI and Microsoft are the anchor customers – lands on the governments in Oslo and Stockholm, while Aker and Equinor position themselves as Nordic AI-infrastructure players.

Research

Norwegian research teaches AI to squeeze more value out of hydropower

The research institute Sintef is leading an international project in which AI will help Norwegian hydropower producers make better day-to-day decisions. Today, producers solve almost the same scheduling puzzle from scratch every day – with no memory of yesterday’s solution.

The project, InterOpt, funded by the Research Council of Norway and running from 2024 to 2028, uses reinforcement learning to find relationships between water value, electricity price and inflow in historical data. The model has to weigh the value of releasing water now against saving it for later, while respecting physical limits and environmental requirements such as minimum flow rates. The producers ANEO, Hydro Energi and Å Energi have a seat at the table, alongside research groups from Spain, Canada and Brazil.

For the Nordics, the timing is hardly a coincidence. As AI data centers now compete for the same hydropower, every extra percent squeezed out of the reservoirs becomes valuable. Better scheduling can give Statkraft, Hafslund and Sweden’s Vattenfall higher revenue without new build-out, and strengthens the Nordic power market’s ability to balance the growing demand of both industry and data centers. Sintef and NTNU at the same time cement their role as a Nordic spearhead in AI for energy systems.

Regulation

EU Council adopts the AI Act’s major simplification – high-risk rules pushed back

The EU Council of Ministers is expected to formally adopt the Digital Omnibus today, June 29 – the package of amendments that simplifies the AI Act. The European Parliament voted in favor back on June 16, with 423 votes for, 57 against and 174 abstentions.

The biggest change is that the requirements for high-risk systems in Annex III – recruitment, credit scoring and biometric identification – are pushed back from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027. At the same time, AI tools that create non-consensual intimate images and abuse material are now explicitly banned, while the deadlines for watermarking AI-generated content are extended. The text will be published in the EU’s Official Journal before it takes effect – until then, the original AI Act applies.

For the Nordics, the postponement offers breathing room. Sweden’s IMY, which the government appointed as market surveillance authority on June 15, and Norway’s Datatilsynet, which introduces the AI Act (KI-loven) in August, gain more time to build up oversight. SEB, Ericsson, Volvo, Telenor, DNB and Equinor – all of which are mapping internal high-risk systems – escape the tight August deadline, but still have to show progress. Critics in both the Riksdag and the Storting warn that the delay weakens protections just as AI systems are spreading fastest.

Two fresh surveys point in the same direction: Nordic workers are rapidly gaining access to AI tools, but fear of mass layoffs is low. PwC’s global AI Jobs Barometer 2026 shows that AI-related roles are holding up in Sweden and Norway even as the total number of job postings falls.

At the same time, only 45 percent of Nordic employers expect major automation of jobs within ten years, compared with 65 percent globally. The explanation lies in strong collective agreements, high union membership and a tradition of social dialogue in which changes are negotiated rather than imposed.

For the Nordics, the figures sketch the contours of a two-track labor market: AI skills command a premium while broad occupational groups risk falling behind. For Unionen, IF Metall, Sveriges Ingenjörer and Norway’s LO and Fellesforbundet, the task becomes ensuring that the rapidly growing access to AI tools is also matched by training, so that the productivity gains are not concentrated in a few high-wage occupations. The question will be central to the 2026 wage round.

On June 11, Chalmers, KTH, Lund University, Stockholm University, Linköping University and Uppsala University signed a memorandum of understanding to establish the Swedish Center for Quantum Technology. The centre is coordinated from Chalmers and brings together research, education and infrastructure across the entire value chain. The goal is to make Sweden European leader in quantum technology by 2035.

For the Nordics the initiative gives the Knut and Alice Wallenberg Foundation, which has funded WACQT with more than one billion Swedish kronor since 2018, a clear continuation platform. For Ericsson (post-quantum cryptography), SEB and Handelsbanken (financial risk computation) and Saab (quantum sensors for defence) it becomes easier to engage a single coordinated Swedish research partner. The centre strengthens the Nordic position in the European quantum race against the German and French national efforts.
