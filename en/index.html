Models

OpenAI ships GPT-5.5 — a step closer to the "super app" vision, less than two months after GPT-5.4

On April 23, OpenAI unveiled GPT-5.5, describing it as its "smartest and most intuitive model yet". The model is rolling out to Plus, Pro, Business, and Enterprise subscribers across ChatGPT and Codex, with GPT-5.5 Pro reserved for the three top tiers. OpenAI says GPT-5.5 is stronger at data analysis, coding, computer use, deep research, and producing documents and spreadsheets — and consistently outperforms both prior OpenAI models and comparable offerings from Google and Anthropic on benchmarks. President Greg Brockman tied the launch explicitly to the company's "super app" strategy, which aims to consolidate ChatGPT, Codex and the forthcoming AI browser into a single enterprise service: "What is really special about this model is how much more it can do with less guidance". Microsoft confirmed that GPT-5.5 is available the same day in Microsoft Foundry. For Swedish and Nordic enterprise customers — who reach OpenAI models through Azure North Europe and through AI Sweden's Google-funded skills programme — the velocity (less than nine weeks from GPT-5.4 to GPT-5.5) pushes procurement to shift from project orders to rolling model subscriptions, and raises pressure on WASP and Sintef to keep Nordic applied AI research relevant in a market where frontier models update faster than tender cycles.

Source: OpenAI, CNBC, TechCrunch, Fortune, Microsoft Azure Blog, Fello AI
Labour market

Freshfields and Anthropic sign multi-year deal — Claude rolled out to 33 offices, adoption up 500 percent in six weeks

On April 23, global law firm Freshfields and Anthropic announced a multi-year collaboration agreement to co-build AI-driven legal workflows. The deal commits Freshfields to a firm-wide deployment of the Claude suite across all 33 of its offices, covering every practice group and business service — a breadth well beyond the pilot-sized projects that have been the norm in legal AI so far. 5,700 Freshfields employees already have access to Claude via the firm's proprietary general AI platform, and usage has risen roughly 500 percent in the first six weeks. The agreement is paired with early access and testing of Thomson Reuters' next-generation CoCounsel Legal, rebuilt on Anthropic technology. RELX shares (the LexisNexis parent and Thomson Reuters competitor) fell on the news. For Swedish and Nordic law firms and corporate legal teams — from Mannheimer Swartling and Vinge to Wiersholm and Schjødt — the deal resets the baseline: when a competitor Freshfields' size standardises on Claude across its entire office network, it becomes harder to justify internal AI-pilot pauses, and pressure grows on IMY and Datatilsynet to issue clear guidance on legal privilege and AI logging.

Debate

Anthropic admits three bugs degraded Claude for months — resets usage limits for all paying subscribers

On April 23, Anthropic published a technical post-mortem acknowledging that three separate changes in Claude's product stack — not the model itself — explain weeks of user complaints about weaker coding and agentic performance. First, on March 4 the company lowered the default "reasoning effort" setting in Claude Code from high to medium to reduce UI latency, visibly weakening the model on complex tasks — a choice reverted on April 7. Second, a caching bug shipped on March 26 cleared the "thinking" history on every turn instead of once after an hour of idleness, leaving the model forgetful and repetitive; the fix landed for Sonnet 4.6 and Opus 4.6 on April 10. Third, on April 16 Anthropic added a system-prompt instruction capping text between tool calls at 25 words and final responses at 100 words to reduce Opus 4.7 verbosity — the change caused a 3 percent drop in coding evaluations and was reverted on April 20. Anthropic has now reset usage limits for all paying subscribers as of April 23, compensating for wasted tokens and friction. For Swedish and Nordic consultancies and tech firms — Skogling Consulting, Varbak, Volvo IT, KTH Innovation, Sintef Digital — that have built delivery pipelines on Claude Code, the post-mortem has two implications: log and cost reviews for March and April should be run to correctly attribute productivity gaps, and vendor transparency on system-prompt changes must now enter procurement and SLA conversations.

Source: VentureBeat, The Register, OfficeChai, GitHub Issue #41930, TechManiacs
Regulation

OpenAI open-sources Privacy Filter — on-device PII redaction, 96 percent F1 on benchmark, Apache 2.0

On April 22, OpenAI released Privacy Filter, an open-weight model with 1.5 billion total parameters (50 million active) for detecting and masking personally identifiable information (PII) in unstructured text. It covers eight PII categories — names, addresses, email addresses and others — and achieves a 96 percent F1 score on the PII-Masking-300k benchmark. The key design choice: the model runs locally on the user's machine without sending data out, and is shipped on Hugging Face and GitHub under Apache 2.0 so organisations can fine-tune or embed it freely. VentureBeat, Help Net Security and Bloomberg Law frame the launch as OpenAI's direct response to the growing problem of employees pasting sensitive data into ChatGPT, DeepSeek and similar services — a behaviour that Nordic employers and regulators are now pushing back on as the EU AI Act becomes applicable on August 2, 2026. For IMY — which received increased budget appropriations from the Swedish government in the spring amendment budget to build AI supervisory capacity — Privacy Filter becomes a concrete reference model that the public sector (regions, municipalities, Försäkringskassan) can run locally on its own systems without data leaking to US clouds. The same applies to Datatilsynet, Nkom and Norway's AI Act, which is scheduled to enter into force in late summer: an Apache 2.0-licensed PII masker lowers the bar for GDPR-compatible AI pipelines across Nordic banks (SEB, DNB, Nordea), healthcare (Karolinska, Sahlgrenska, Sintef Helse) and municipal administration.

Source: OpenAI, VentureBeat, Help Net Security, Bloomberg Law, BetaNews, OpenAI Model Card (PDF)

Hardware

Google makes Ironwood TPU generally available and previews a split 8th generation — Anthropic anchors 1 million chips, Nvidia challenged

At Google Cloud Next in Las Vegas on April 22, Google made its seventh-generation TPU Ironwood generally available, positioning it as "the first Google TPU for the age of inference". The chip delivers 4.6 petaFLOPS per unit and 42.5 exaFLOPS in a 9,216-chip superpod, with a 10x peak performance uplift over TPU v5p and more than 4x per-chip performance for both training and inference compared to Trillium. At the same time, Google previewed its eighth generation in two distinct variants: TPU 8t ("Sunfish") designed by Broadcom for training, and TPU 8i ("Zebrafish") designed by MediaTek for inference — both on TSMC 2nm and targeting late 2027. TPU 8i links 1,152 chips per pod, has 3x more on-chip SRAM and is optimized to run millions of agents concurrently at low latency. Anthropic is anchor customer for both generations: the company gets access to up to one million TPU chips and more than a gigawatt of capacity in 2026, with the first phase covering 400,000 Ironwood units estimated at $10 billion. For Swedish and Nordic AI investments — particularly Google's data center in Skien — the architectural choice signals that the economics of inference are now separated from training, and that the agent bottleneck is shifting from model size to chip-level memory architecture.

Source: Google Blog, CNBC, TechCrunch, The Next Web, VentureBeat, Bloomberg

Regulation

Florida Attorney General opens criminal investigation into OpenAI — to determine whether ChatGPT answers contributed to the 2025 FSU shooting

Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier announced on April 21 that his office is opening a formal criminal investigation into OpenAI in connection with the school shooting at Florida State University on April 17, 2025, in which two people were killed and six injured. According to investigators, the suspect, Phoenix Ikner, consulted ChatGPT before the attack on weapon choice, ammunition and timing for maximizing the number of people on campus. Uthmeier's office has issued subpoenas requiring OpenAI — by May 1, 2026 — to hand over internal guidelines and training materials on how the company handles threats, crime reporting and cooperation with law enforcement, with material going back to March 2024. An OpenAI spokesperson responds that ChatGPT "is not responsible for this terrible crime" and that the model only returned information already publicly available online. The investigation is the first in the U.S. in which a state tests criminal liability for an AI provider linked to a violent crime. Regardless of outcome, it will affect safety design, logging practices and liability theories — also on the European side, where the AI Act debate on product liability and the trilogue on April 28 faces the same principle.

Source: My Florida Legal, NBC News, NPR, Quartz, Channel 4, WEAR TV

Anthropic triples lobbying spend to $1.6 million — 60 percent ahead of OpenAI as Washington fights intensify

Anthropic spent $1.6 million on federal lobbying in the U.S. during the first quarter of 2026, the largest quarterly amount in the company's history and 4.4x more than the same period in 2025. The figures come from Anthropic's mandatory quarterly filing under the Lobbying Disclosure Act and were reported by Axios on April 21. The number exceeds OpenAI's $1 million (up from $560,000 in Q1 2025) by 60 percent and reflects an accelerating political fight over AI safety, compute access, cybersecurity and national security. According to multiple reports Anthropic has been in conflict with the Pentagon over which military and intelligence applications Claude may be used for — a fight that has pushed the "Acceptable Use Policy" discussion to the highest level and is reportedly now in court. Meta still tops the Big Tech list with $7.1 million for the quarter. For Swedish and Nordic decision-makers — who are tracking both the Digital Omnibus on AI trilogue on April 28 and the debate on technical/military AI cooperation — the spending trajectory is a clear signal: AI regulation is no longer a technical issue but a political power struggle, where those without a voice in Washington risk being governed by those who have one.

BMW and Hexagon Robotics scale the AEON humanoid in Leipzig — new "Center of Competence for Physical AI in Production" opens in Munich

During Hannover Messe week, BMW Group and Hexagon Robotics confirmed that the broader pilot of the wheeled AEON humanoid robot is rolling out at BMW's Leipzig plant, with two units targeted for battery and component manufacturing in production by year-end 2026. AEON runs on 22 sensors, self-swapping batteries that hot-swap in 23 seconds, four layers of "physical AI" including imitation learning — where 20 demonstrations are said to be enough to teach the robot a new task — and a walking speed of 2.5 metres per second for around-the-clock operation. BMW is at the same time establishing a "Center of Competence for Physical AI in Production" in Munich to coordinate the European rollout, building on lessons from the Spartanburg pilot with Figure AI (30,000 X3s assembled, 90,000 parts moved). Together with Humanoid's HMND 01 at Siemens in Erlangen and NVIDIA's demo environment at Hannover Messe, the launch marks the moment European car manufacturing crosses from experimental humanoid trials to continuous production pilots — and signals that Nordic suppliers and machine builders must plan for humanoid capacity appearing in customer factories as soon as next year.

Models

OpenAI launches ChatGPT Images 2.0 (gpt-image-2) — thinks before it draws, 2K resolution, multilingual text, and DALL·E retired on May 12

On April 21, OpenAI unveiled ChatGPT Images 2.0 built on the new gpt-image-2 model — the first major image-generation upgrade since GPT-4o's native image feature. The model introduces a "thinking mode" that spends extra compute planning composition, counting objects and checking constraints before rendering, which according to TechCrunch dramatically reduces the number of retries needed to fix element counts or mislabelled diagrams. Text and typography — historically the biggest weakness in image models — now render cleanly even in small UI labels, logos, captions and non-Latin scripts such as Japanese, Korean, Chinese, Hindi and Bengali, up to 2K resolution. VentureBeat and The New Stack describe the model as capable of producing full magazine spreads, infographics, slides, maps and manga in a single pass. Rollout reaches all ChatGPT and Codex users from Tuesday, with advanced outputs reserved for paid tiers, plus a new gpt-image-2 API with pricing tied to quality and resolution. OpenAI confirms in parallel that DALL·E 2 and DALL·E 3 will be retired on May 12. For Swedish and Nordic design, marketing and editorial teams — already pressured by Adobe Firefly and Google Imagen — the release lowers the threshold for replacing parts of the image-supplier chain with a single conversational API.

Hardware

Google in talks with Marvell on two new AI chips — inference TPU and memory processor take aim at Nvidia's dominance

The Information reported on April 20 that Google is in discussions with chipmaker Marvell to develop two new custom AI chips. The first is a memory processing unit (MPU) designed to work alongside Google's existing Tensor Processing Units, shortening the data path between memory and compute — one of the biggest bottlenecks in large-scale model deployment. The second is a next-generation TPU optimised for inference, the lower-latency, higher-volume workload that now accounts for the majority of hyperscaler GPU spend. If the deal lands, Marvell becomes Google's third named custom-silicon partner alongside Broadcom and MediaTek — part of Google's strategy to build a full-stack AI chip ecosystem and reduce Nvidia dependence. Sources say the design is expected to be finalised next year, with plans for approximately 2 million MPUs. Marvell stock rose 5.8 per cent to close at $147.84. For the Nordics, where Google's upcoming data centre in Skien and existing facility in Fredericia will host AI capacity, the story is about which chips will actually run tomorrow's workloads — and how hard Nvidia can be pressed on margin.

Source: CNBC, The Information, The Next Web, Benzinga, Yahoo Finance

OpenAI releases GPT-Rosalind — dedicated life-sciences model with Amgen, Moderna and the Allen Institute as pilot customers

On April 16, OpenAI launched GPT-Rosalind, a specialised model designed for life sciences. The launch signals that frontier labs are entering vertical specialisation — building domain-tuned models for high-value sectors rather than relying on general-purpose models alone. For Nordic biotech and pharma — AstraZeneca, Novo Nordisk, Orion — it is a clear signal that research productivity frontiers are shifting and that domain-specific AI is becoming a competitive requirement.

Infrastructure

Cloudflare launches Agent Memory in private beta — gives AI agents persistent memory without filling the context window

On April 17, as part of Agents Week 2026 (April 13–17), Cloudflare announced Agent Memory, a managed service that gives AI agents persistent long-term memory across sessions without having to cram everything into the context window. The service solves the context-rot problem that has plagued autonomous agents for over a year: as conversations and task histories grow, models lose precision, hallucinate earlier decisions and take longer to reason. Agent Memory stores facts, preferences, tool-use history and task state in an external structured store that the agent queries when relevant — similar to how humans use notes rather than remembering everything at once. The service is launching in private beta and builds on Cloudflare's Workers AI platform. For Nordic companies running AI agents in production — customer service, operations, development — it is a direct answer to one of the biggest obstacles: how to get agents to learn from previous tasks without ballooning both latency and cost.

Source: Cloudflare Blog, The Register, Cloudflare Press, Cloudflare Agents Week
Hardware

NVIDIA releases Ising — the world's first open AI models for quantum computers, 2.5× faster error decoding with Harvard, IQM and U.K. NPL on board

On April 14, with continued coverage through April 19, NVIDIA launched Ising, a family of open AI models designed to accelerate the path to useful quantum computers. Ising includes a 35-billion-parameter vision-language model and a 3D convolutional neural network that together read out quantum error-correction codes 2.5 times faster than prior state-of-the-art methods, a critical bottleneck in building fault-tolerant quantum hardware. The models are open-source and run on NVIDIA's PyTorch and CUDA-Q frameworks. Partners including Harvard's School of Engineering and Applied Sciences, Finnish quantum hardware company IQM and the U.K.'s National Physical Laboratory are already integrating Ising into their quantum systems. The release shows that the AI–quantum convergence is accelerating and that NVIDIA is positioning itself as infrastructure provider for both classical and quantum compute. For the Nordic quantum ecosystem — IQM, Chalmers, DTU, NTNU — open models from NVIDIA lower the barrier significantly for research groups that want to experiment with AI-assisted error correction.

Source: NVIDIA Newsroom, Tom's Hardware, The Quantum Insider, MarkTechPost

Anthropic releases Claude Opus 4.7 broadly — 13 per cent coding lift, but acknowledges Mythos is still held back

On April 16, Anthropic made Claude Opus 4.7 generally available through Claude.ai, the API, Amazon Bedrock, Google Cloud Vertex AI, Microsoft Foundry and GitHub Copilot. About AI-skiftet

AI-skiftet is an independent essay project on how AI is changing work, industry, politics and society.

The perspective is deliberately industry-near. The texts do not primarily come from seminar rooms or general trend-spotting, but from work in sharp production environments, improvement projects and conversations about what the technology is actually changing when quality, economy and responsibility matter.

The ambition is twofold: to raise the level of the Swedish AI debate and to make the arguments more testable. Therefore the texts distinguish as far as possible between observation, inference, scenario and normative proposal, and each essay has its own source notes.

Rolf Skogling — mechanical engineer and consultant in industrial optimisation. Works with AI in sharp operations within Nordic and European manufacturing industry.
