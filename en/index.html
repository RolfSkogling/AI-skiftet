Models

Anthropic launches Claude Opus 4.7, full Microsoft 365 integration and ten ready-to-run finance agents on May 5 — Moody's data via native app, Jamie Dimon on stage in New York

At a closed-door briefing in New York on May 5, Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei and JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon jointly unveiled a three-part push that decisively aims Claude at the financial sector. First, Anthropic debuted its new flagship model Claude Opus 4.7 — described by Anthropic as "the most capable model for financial work yet." Second, the Claude add-ins for Excel, PowerPoint and Word became generally available through Microsoft 365, while Claude for Outlook launched in beta — Claude can now operate as a single agent carrying context across all four applications, and inside Excel it can build financial models from filings, audit formulas across linked workbooks and run sensitivity analyses without leaving the cell. Third, ten ready-to-run agent templates are rolling out for the Claude platform: pitch builder, meeting preparer, earnings reviewer, model builder, market researcher, KYC screener, valuation reviewer, general ledger reconciler, month end closer and statement auditor. Moody's full ratings and risk-data platform covering more than 600 million companies is being embedded in Claude as a native app. For the Nordics, the launch lands in the middle of a banking transformation. Nordea's 1,500-employee restructuring across 2026–2027 (announced March 17) is explicitly tied to AI and Nordic scale advantages — and "month end closer" and "statement auditor" are exactly the roles being cut. SEB, Handelsbanken, Swedbank, Danske Bank and DNB all run Microsoft 365 at tenant scale, where Excel and PowerPoint workflows are daily products for CFO, treasury and M&A teams. Together with Storebrand, Folksam and Länsförsäkringar — which operate under solvency and insurance regimes that the EU AI Act classifies as high-risk under Annex III — the August 2 threshold (still in force after the Digital Omnibus trilogue collapsed on April 28 and the next attempt was scheduled for May 13) becomes a hard deadline for documentation, risk management and human oversight in precisely these agent patterns. For Sweden's IMY, Finansinspektionen and Norway's Datatilsynet, supervision shifts from generic ChatGPT pilots to financial-services agent operations. And for Wallenberg Investments, EQT, AMF and NBIM, the Anthropic–Microsoft axis is yet another signal that the value chain in finance AI is settling at the application layer, not just in the compute stack.

Regulation

US CAISI signs pre-deployment testing agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft and xAI on May 5 — over 40 evaluations already completed, EU's May 13 trilogue heads in the opposite direction

On May 5, the Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) — housed within NIST inside the US Department of Commerce — confirmed new formal agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft and xAI giving US government researchers access to frontier models for evaluation before public release, in classified environments. The agreements build on the voluntary 2024 arrangements with OpenAI and Anthropic and have been renegotiated to reflect CAISI's new directives from the Secretary of Commerce and America's AI Action Plan. CAISI states that more than 40 evaluations have already been carried out on frontier models, many not yet publicly available. The focus is on "demonstrable national security risks": cyberattacks against US infrastructure, chemical and biological weapons, and the mapping of foreign AI systems and possible backdoors. For OpenAI, the work centres on GPT-5.5-Cyber; Anthropic provides CAISI with both public and unreleased models plus documentation of known vulnerabilities. For the Nordics, a sharp contrast materialises within eight days. On May 13 the European Commission, Council and Parliament reconvene in Brussels for the second trilogue attempt to defer the AI Act's high-risk obligations from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027 (Annex III) and August 2, 2028 (Annex I) — the first attempt collapsed after 12 hours on April 28. While the US is building a formal pre-deployment safety stack through CAISI, the EU is moving toward deferred compliance and has no comparable public evaluation body. For Sweden's IMY (which has named AI in the public sector a 2026 supervisory priority), Norway's Datatilsynet (with the KI-loven on consultation), Nkom, Försäkringskassan, Skatteverket and Karolinska, the CAISI model becomes a de facto template against which oversight and conformity assessment will be measured. For Wallenberg AI Factory, Sferical AI, AI Sweden, NTNU's AID centre and Sintef, the US line strengthens the case for a European third path combining sovereign compute with independent red-teaming — before Cyprus hands over the EU Council presidency on June 30 and Lithuania takes over.

Stoltenberg on May 5: "No country has profited more from AI than Norway" — but the oil fund's 21.3 percent in ten holdings has Tangen calling AI concentration the largest in fund history

Norway's Finance Minister Jens Stoltenberg said on May 5 that "hardly anyone has profited more from the AI revolution than Norway" — directly contingent on the oil fund's (Norges Bank Investment Management, NBIM) overweight in US technology companies. At the close of 2025/26 the fund's ten largest holdings represented 21.3 percent of the fund's total value, with seven of them US tech companies. At the same briefing, fund chief Nicolai Tangen called the AI concentration "the largest in the fund's history" and noted that, in the worst case, it could pull equity values down 29 percent according to the fund's most recent stress test. Stoltenberg nonetheless rejected reducing US exposure: "That is a political decision, but I see no major changes." The remarks come in a busy week in which the fund warned via Tangen on April 28 of "total backlash" if companies use AI only to cut jobs, while the Pentagon signed classified AI deals with seven providers on May 1 and Anthropic was valued above USD 900 billion. For Norway the trade-off becomes politically explosive ahead of the Storting's revision of the state budget in May: NHO's December 2025 industry-AI declaration assumes precisely the development that delivers the fund's returns, while LO, Negotia, NITO and Tekna want the same capital used for transition programmes rather than reinforcing already dominant Mag 7 firms. For Sweden, the same question presses: AP-fonderna's tech exposure has risen in parallel, and Wallenberg Investments, EQT, AMF, SEB Investment Management and Folksam Liv face precisely the same concentration risk even at smaller volumes. For the Riksbank and Finansinspektionen — and Norway's Finanstilsynet — the question of AI-related financial stability deepens just as the Annex III threshold approaches August 2.

OpenAI ships GPT-5.5 Instant as ChatGPT's new default on May 5 — 52.5 percent fewer hallucinations in medicine, law and finance, "memory sources" makes context transparent to users

OpenAI shipped GPT-5.5 Instant on May 5 as the new default model in ChatGPT, replacing GPT-5.3 Instant. According to OpenAI's own reporting, 5.5 Instant produces 52.5 percent fewer hallucinated claims than 5.3 Instant on high-stakes prompts in medicine, law and finance, and in internal evaluations shows a 37.3 percent reduction in incorrect claims against conversations users had previously flagged for factual errors. The model scores 81.2 on AIME 2025 (vs 65.4) and 76 on MMMU-Pro (vs 69.2). At the same time, "memory sources" is being introduced — a feature that shows the user exactly which saved chats, files or Gmail entries informed a given response, with the option to correct or delete individual entries. The model rolls out to all ChatGPT users immediately, but advanced personalisation via past chats, files and Gmail goes first to Plus and Pro. For the Nordics the model swap has immediate consequences in four windows. For the Swedish public sector — where IMY in 2026 prioritises AI supervision and Stockholm, Gothenburg, Malmö, Uppsala, Linköping and Umeå are all running ChatGPT, Copilot or Gemini pilots — "memory sources" becomes a real compliance aid ahead of the August 2 threshold by making personal-data traceability in chat services a standard surface rather than a special feature. For Klarna (which reports Q1 2026 on May 14 and whose OpenAI-powered customer-service assistant handles the majority of Swedish chats), 52 percent fewer hallucinations shifts the margin in customer disputes around medical and payment questions. For Lovable (Stockholm), Sana Labs and Swedish AI-builder startups, the new default becomes an immediate A/B-test factor as users' baseline expectations of factual precision rise. And for Norway's Datatilsynet KI-loven consultation and the WASP-HS humanities track, transparent memory sources provide a concrete regulatory design criterion as Annex III conformity is built into Nordic sovereign stacks.

Sources: TechCrunch, Axios, The Decoder, 9to5Mac, SiliconANGLE, The AI Insider, Klarna IR (Q1 May 14)

Invezz documents on May 4 the link between $725 billion in hyperscaler capex and 95,000 tech layoffs in Q1 2026 — Meta's 8,000 take effect May 20

On May 4, Invezz published the analysis "Is Big Tech's $725B AI splurge being funded by mass layoffs?" documenting for the first time how quarterly accounts and headcount rolls move in opposite directions during the same fiscal year. According to Layoffs.fyi and TrueUp, between 81,747 and 95,878 tech employees lost their jobs in Q1 2026 — the highest quarterly figure since 2023, running at roughly 864 people per day. Amazon alone accounted for some 16,000 of the Q1 cuts in corporate functions while AWS grew 24% — its fastest in 13 quarters. Meta withdrew its previously promised 6,000 open roles and is laying off 8,000 (10% of its workforce) effective May 20, with recruiting and HR cut by 35–40%. Microsoft offered voluntary retirement to 8,750 U.S. staff (about 7% of its domestic workforce). Against these numbers Invezz sets the CEOs' 2026 capex guidance — Amazon $200 billion, Microsoft $190 billion, Meta $125–145 billion and Alphabet $180–190 billion, totaling around $725 billion — noting that two thirds goes to GPUs, data centers and real estate, not personnel. For the Nordics, the consequences materialize in three concrete windows in the coming month. For Sweden's Unionen, Sveriges Ingenjörer, IF Metall and Akademikerförbundet SSR, the wage round is approaching its final phase with Klarna, H&M, Volvo Group, Ericsson and SEB all having implemented or announced AI-related role changes — Almega Tjänsteföretagen and Teknikföretagen will need to take a position before the Almedalen week in July. For Norway's LO, Negotia, NITO and Tekna, NBIM chief Tangen's April 28 warning of a "real backlash" from cost-driven layoffs functions as a union reference point as the Storting revises the state budget in May and NHO's December 2025 industrial-AI declaration is operationalized. And for Vinnova, AI Sweden, the Research Council of Norway and Innovation Norway, transition funding faces pressure for faster cycles than the current three-year programs — Karolinska, Sahlgrenska and Akershus University Hospital are all scaling AI-supported care, and skills supply cannot wait three years.

Sweden's IMY names AI in the public sector a 2026 supervisory priority — children and law enforcement round out the picture three months before the AI Act's August 2 threshold

The Swedish Authority for Privacy Protection (IMY) confirmed in April that 2026 supervision and guidance will concentrate on three focus areas: law enforcement, children and young people, and AI in the public sector. Specifically, IMY will follow up how municipalities, regions and government agencies use AI in their operations and ensure that the technology is implemented in a privacy-compliant way. The notice comes as Vinnova on April 28 awarded SEK 160 million to digitalization in Swedish industry with AI, digital infrastructure and communication as three main tracks, and just over a month before the AI Act's high-risk provisions enter into force on August 2 — a date that can no longer be moved after the Digital Omnibus trilogue collapsed in Strasbourg on April 28 and the next session was set for May 13. For Sweden's municipalities and regions, the question sharpens dramatically: Stockholm, Gothenburg, Malmö, Uppsala, Linköping, Umeå and Sundsvall all run internal ChatGPT, Copilot or Gemini pilots, and Region Stockholm's new Chief AI Officer role at Karolinska becomes a working model others will be measured against. For Försäkringskassan, Skatteverket, Pensionsmyndigheten, the Swedish Public Employment Service and CSN — together handling the majority of Sweden's welfare data — risk assessment, documentation and human oversight shift from preparedness to compliance. Internationally, IMY's line synchronizes with a Norwegian regulatory ramp-up: the government's KI-loven is now in consultation and will enter into force in late summer 2026, while the new entity KI Norge is being established during the year as a national arena for responsible innovation. Datatilsynet and Nkom face a short window in which the Storting must determine whether Telenor, DNB, Equinor, Yara and Kongsberg Gruppen — all active partners in Norwegian Open AI Lab alongside NTNU and SINTEF — should follow stricter national rules than their EU competitors for up to 16 months.

Operational technology providers excluded from Anthropic's Mythos rollout — American Water among those meeting the Office of National Cyber Director, Nordic OT industry waits in line

On May 2, Nextgov/FCW reported that operational technology (OT) providers — the hardware- and PLC-driven layer running water, power, oil, gas and manufacturing industry — explicitly express "annoyance" that they have been kept out of the first rollout phase of Anthropic's Mythos Preview, the frontier cybersecurity model launched within Project Glasswing on April 7. Initial recipients have been large IT and finance firms (AWS, Apple, Cisco, Google, JPMorgan Chase, Microsoft) — while the OT segment, which according to CISA is the most exposed part of critical infrastructure, has had to wait for processes still in progress. American Water — one of the largest regulated U.S. water and wastewater utilities — is among the entities that during April met the Office of the National Cyber Director (ONCD) along with ONCD's advisory forum to discuss Mythos and broader AI cybersecurity threats. For the Nordics, the exclusion has immediate significance where OT density is highest. Equinor, Aker BP, Yara, Hydro, Vattenfall, Fortum, Stena Recycling, SSAB, LKAB and Boliden all run SCADA and DCS stacks where an autonomous vulnerability hunter like Mythos could have scanned PLC firmware and industrial software — but they now must queue behind U.S. federal customers exploring the same window. For NVE, the Swedish Energy Agency, MSB and Norway's Nasjonal sikkerhetsmyndighet (NSM), the development forces a de facto prioritization debate: should Nordic OT cybersecurity be procured within the Pentagon's vetted-government track (where Anthropic was blacklisted on May 1 but where Mythos is handled "government-wide"), or via Sferical AI, Wallenberg AI Factory and Telenor AI Factory as a European third path? And for Sweden's Konkurrensverket and Norway's Konkurransetilsynet, the situation reinforces the asymmetric market in which incumbents like Microsoft, Google and AWS can lock European OT customers into sovereign packages before frontier-cybersecurity access is opened up to the region's own industrial actors.

EU AI Omnibus trilogue collapses after 12 hours — August deadline for high-risk rules back in play, next attempt May 13

The decisive political trilogue on the Digital Omnibus on AI in Strasbourg on April 28 ended after 12 hours without the Commission, Council, and Parliament reaching agreement. The first sticking point was the Annex I conformity assessment: Parliament wanted to move sectoral legislation from Annex I Section A to B, a procedural change the Council resisted, while disputes over medical devices and machinery remained unresolved. As of April 30, no formal postponement has been adopted, meaning the AI Act's Annex III rules for stand-alone high-risk systems still take effect on August 2, 2026 unless a deal is reached at the next political trilogue scheduled around May 13. The Cypriot Council Presidency is trying to close the file before its term ends on June 30; otherwise Lithuania takes over from July 1 and negotiations continue. The impact in the Nordics is immediate and concrete. For Sweden, the collapse means that Klarna's AI-based credit assessment, H&M's AI recruitment, and Volvo's autonomous trucks remain at risk of being classified as high-risk this summer — IMY's new AI supervision unit, which received expanded funding in the spring amending budget, must plan staffing and guidance for the August scenario, not the 2027 scenario. For Norway, work on KI-loven is simultaneously thrown out of sync: the government still aims for entry into force in late summer 2026, but Datatilsynet and Nkom face a gap where Kongsberg, Equinor, DNB, Yara, and Telenor could be more strictly regulated than their EU competitors for up to 16 months. White House blocks Anthropic's Mythos expansion the same day OpenAI starts the GPT-5.5-Cyber rollout — two opposing lines on frontier cybersecurity on a single day

For Wallenberg AI Factory, Sferical AI, and Berzelius the split simultaneously opens a market window: Nordic sovereign-compute providers can offer customers a third path that is neither a US frontier firewall nor a broad federal rollout.

On April 29, AFP, France 24, The Local Sweden and the Bangkok Post pushed out one of the strangest AI experiments of the spring: the Andon café in Stockholm's Vasastan district — which opened on April 18 — is run end-to-end by an AI agent named Mona, powered by Google Gemini. About AI-skiftet

AI-skiftet is an independent essay project on how AI is changing work, industry, politics and society.

The perspective is deliberately industry-near. The texts do not primarily come from seminar rooms or general trend-spotting, but from work in sharp production environments, improvement projects and conversations about what the technology is actually changing when quality, economy and responsibility matter.

The ambition is twofold: to raise the level of the Swedish AI debate and to make the arguments more testable. Therefore the texts distinguish as far as possible between observation, inference, scenario and normative proposal, and each essay has its own source notes.
