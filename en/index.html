Physical AI

Sony's robot beats the pros at table tennis – a new milestone for embodied AI

Sony AI has shown that its autonomous robot, Project Ace, can take on and beat both elite and professional table tennis players in real time. Several observers call it a breakthrough for "embodied" AI – systems that don't just reason in text but act physically in the world.

In the demonstrations the robot tracked the ball in 3D using nine high-speed cameras and event-based sensors, reacted up to 200 times a second and returned heavily spun shots. In repeated matches it has beaten both skilled amateurs and professional players. The key point is that the movements are learned through reinforcement learning rather than pre-programmed – the same principle driving today's robot foundation models.

For the Nordics it isn't the table tennis that matters, but the technology underneath: real-time perception and learned fine motor control. That is the same frontier driving industrial robotics at ABB and Denmark's Universal Robots, the NEO home robot at Norway's 1X, and robotics research at Sintef and NTNU. For Swedish and Norwegian manufacturing, healthcare and logistics it points toward robots that can handle fast, unpredictable tasks – not just repetitive ones.

Source: Sony AI, New Atlas, Interesting Engineering, TechRadar
Research

Sweden and the US sign agreement on cooperation in AI, space and quantum technology

At the NATO meeting in Helsingborg on May 22, Sweden's foreign minister Maria Malmer Stenergard and US Secretary of State Marco Rubio signed a memorandum of understanding on bilateral technology cooperation – dubbed the "Technology Prosperity Deal". It is intended to open up expanded collaboration in research and development, including in artificial intelligence, space and quantum technology.

The government describes the agreement as a political declaration of intent with no legal or financial commitments, built on "complementary strengths and mutual interests". It is meant to give Swedish researchers better access to advanced technology and greater exchange with US institutions. Minister for higher education and research Lotta Edholm calls it confirmation of Sweden's standing as a research nation.

For the Nordics the timing is charged. The agreement comes just as the EU's AI Act approaches its August 2 threshold, and Ericsson welcomed it immediately. As Wallenberg's WASP, Chalmers' quantum centre WACQT and the space cluster in Kiruna seek international partners, deeper technological dependence on the US also raises questions of sovereignty and export control – a trade-off that Norway too, with its new AI law and the Research Council's six AI centres, must navigate.

Anthropic's compute bill reveals the real price tag of the AI race

It emerged on May 20 that Anthropic is paying Elon Musk's xAI around $1.25 billion a month for access to the so-called Colossus data centres – a figure that surfaced in SpaceX's IPO prospectus. That works out to roughly $15 billion a year and up to $45 billion over three years, through May 2029.

The deal shows how the fuel in the AI race is increasingly raw compute. Either party can terminate the contract with 90 days' notice, and payments ramp up at the start. In parallel, the tech giants are raising their investments – Meta in late April raised its 2026 AI spending forecast to $125–145 billion –and the question of a possible "AI bubble" is back on the table.

For the Nordics the numbers explain why the battle for compute has moved here: Nscale's multi-billion build in Narvik, Stargate Norway, atNorth in Stockholm and EuroHPC's AI factories in Sweden and Finland. For Norway's oil fund and Sweden's AP funds, heavily invested in Nvidia and the hyperscalers, the returns – and risks – of AI infrastructure are a growing position. And for Nordic firms wanting to build their own models, the bill raises an uncomfortable question: can anyone outside the very largest afford to take part?

The race for AI agents that write and run code on their own is widening. xAI has launched Grok Build and France's Mistral has moved its coding agents to the cloud, both aimed at Anthropic's Claude Code and OpenAI's Codex, which have dominated the segment so far.

For the Nordics this is very concrete. At the same time the skills question becomes acute: what happens to junior developer roles when the agents take over the repetitive work?

Research

OpenAI model disproves 80-year-old Erdös conjecture – a first for AI cracking an open math problem

OpenAI said on May 20 that an internal reasoning model independently produced an original proof disproving a famous unsolved conjecture in discrete geometry, first posed by Paul Erdös in 1946. Several mathematicians describe it as the first time an AI has solved a prominent open problem on its own – rather than merely retrieving a known answer.

The problem concerns how many pairs of points can lie at exactly distance 1 when n points are placed in a plane; for nearly 80 years square grids were believed to be near-optimal. The model instead connected the geometry to algebraic number theory and found an infinite family of constructions that beats the grid. The result was set out in a companion paper co-signed by several leading mathematicians – among them Noga Alon, Fields medalist Timothy Gowers and Princeton's Will Sawin, who sharpened the bound further.

For the Nordics it lands in the middle of the "AI for science" push: Wallenberg's WASP, the data-driven life-science program DDLS and SciLifeLab at Karolinska, and the Berzelius supercomputer in Linköping. The rectors of Chalmers, KTH and Linköping have called for a national mobilization, and for Vinnova, the Research Council, NTNU and UiO the question becomes how autonomous "AI co-scientists" should be used and reviewed.

Source: OpenAI, TechCrunch, Scientific American, MIT Technology Review

Stockholm-based Spotify and Universal Music Group announced landmark licensing agreements on May 21 – covering both recorded music and music publishing – that let users employ generative AI to create covers and remixes of their favorite songs. Spotify shares rose about 13 percent on the news.

For the collecting societies STIM and Norway's TONO, for the Musicians' Union and Nordic artists, it raises questions of compensation, consent and credit – while tying into the EU copyright debate and the AI Act's labeling of synthetic content from August 2.

Models

Google DeepMind hires Contextual AI for up to $100 million – a RAG pioneer switches sides

Google DeepMind has agreed, according to Bloomberg (May 19), to hire more than 20 researchers from enterprise-AI company Contextual AI and license its technology for a reported $80–100 million. Among those moving over is co-founder and CEO Douwe Kiela, one of the pioneers behind RAG (retrieval-augmented generation).

Contextual builds systems that ground AI answers in a company's own documents, databases and knowledge. The arrangement gives DeepMind a research team plus a business-ready product layer without a full acquisition, echoing its Hume deal from January – an "acquihire" model reportedly used to avoid drawn-out competition reviews.

For the Nordics, grounded AI is exactly what Schibsted, NRK, Klarna, SEB, DNB and the public sector build on to keep answers tied to verified internal data. At the same time the "hire-and-license" trend shapes exit prospects for Nordic AI companies such as Lovable, Legora and Sana – and raises the question for Sweden's Konkurrensverket and Norway's Konkurransetilsynet of whether such deals sidestep ordinary merger review.

Quantum

Honeywell-owned Quantinuum files to go public – could be quantum computing's biggest IPO

Quantinuum, majority-owned by Honeywell, filed an S-1 registration with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission on May 8 for a planned Nasdaq listing under the ticker QNT. The offering is expected in June and is reported to value the company at up to $20 billion – which would make it the quantum-computing industry's largest IPO so far.

The filing shows both growth and rising losses: for 2025, net revenue of $30.9 million and a net loss of $192.6 million (versus $23 million and $144.1 million in 2024). J.P. Morgan and Morgan Stanley are leading the offering, while its size and price range have not yet been set.

For the Nordics the contrast with home-grown quantum efforts is direct: Finland's IQM (which in February flagged a New York listing at around $1.8 billion), Chalmers' WACQT and the Niels Bohr Institute in Copenhagen. For SEB, DNB and Equinor, a maturing quantum market brings "harvest now, decrypt later" closer – putting quantum-safe encryption on the agenda for IMY and Datatilsynet – while the AP funds and Norway's oil fund gain a new, volatile asset class to manage.

Quantum

Chalmers' WACQT hosts a Nordic quantum week – a new design points to fault-tolerant computers

At Chalmers in Gothenburg, Swedish quantum research is gathering this week: the Wallenberg centre WACQT held its annual review on May 19–21 and hosts the QuantumCavities workshop on May 24–27 at Hindåsgården. WACQT is a twelve-year national effort of around one billion kronor aimed at building a high-end Swedish quantum computer.

The backdrop is a concrete breakthrough: in April, Chalmers researchers presented a new theoretical design using "giant superatoms" that offers a new way to protect, control and share quantum information. The Nordics are not alone – Finland's IQM, which in February announced a New York SPAC listing at a valuation of around $1.8 billion, is delivering a 20-qubit machine to Aalto and building 150- and 300-qubit systems for VTT with error correction as the goal.

For the Nordics, the Wallenberg sphere ties AI and quantum together through WASP and the Berzelius supercomputer in Linköping. For Ericsson, Saab and AstraZeneca (via Sferical AI) and for Norway's Equinor and DNB, quantum-safe encryption is a matter to plan for now. And for IMY and Datatilsynet, every advance sharpens "harvest now, decrypt later" – that encrypted government and bank data is collected today to be decrypted later.

For the Nordics the encyclical lends ethical weight to an already ongoing debate. Regulation

A federal jury in California threw out Elon Musk's lawsuit against OpenAI and Sam Altman on May 18 — the nine-member advisory jury needed less than two hours, Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers ruled that Musk waited too long (statute of limitations) and cleared Altman, Greg Brockman and OpenAI on all counts; Musk vows to appeal, and the outcome cements the for-profit structure that Nordic enterprise customers and the Norwegian oil fund must now reckon with

A federal court in Oakland decided Elon Musk's long-running lawsuit against OpenAI, Sam Altman and co-founder Greg Brockman on May 18. The nine-member advisory jury needed less than two hours to conclude that Musk had brought his claim too late — beyond the statute of limitations — when he sued in 2024, and Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers of the Northern District of California accordingly dismissed the case and cleared all defendants on every count. Musk's lawyers had argued that Altman treated the non-profit as a "shell" after the for-profit subsidiary was created in 2019, and that after the 10-billion-dollar Microsoft deal in 2023 OpenAI abandoned its commitments to open source and safety in order to "enrich investors and insiders". Musk wrote on X that he would appeal, calling the verdict a "calendar technicality". For the Nordics the consequences are concrete. For Norway's oil fund (Norges Bank Investment Management) and Sweden's AP pension funds — all heavy owners of Microsoft and Nvidia — the ruling reduces uncertainty around OpenAI's corporate governance, even as the question of a profit-driven AI giant's mission remains an ownership-stewardship matter. For Equinor, SEB, Telenor and other Nordic large customers building Codex workflows on their own infrastructure after last week's OpenAI–Dell deal, the ruling gives legal solidity to the vendor choice. And for KI Norge, which launches under Hans Christian Holte in August, and for IMY, the contrast with Anthropic's PBC structure — and the Vatican alliance the same week — is a reminder that corporate form and governance are becoming part of Nordic vendor due diligence under the AI Act.

Source: NPR, NBC News, Fox Business

Standard Chartered CEO Bill Winters said on May 19 that the bank's AI push will erase thousands of roles as "lower-value human capital" is replaced by technology. For the Nordics the episode is a mirror. Regulation

Norway–India Digital Development Partnership signed on May 18 during PM Modi's state visit — open-source DPI, digital identity via MOSIP (130 million users worldwide), triangular cooperation with the Global South, and a clear Nordic positioning where Norway brands itself as a DPI bridge between the EU and the South; a primary Nordic angle that forces the Storting, Datatilsynet, Nkom and Digdir to coordinate quickly ahead of the EU AI Act 2027 threshold

During Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi's state visit to Oslo on May 18, Norway and India signed twelve bilateral agreements, the most forward-looking of which is the India–Norway Digital Development Partnership. The deal establishes cooperation on digital public infrastructure (DPI), digital public goods and open digital ecosystems, with an explicit focus on reusable solutions for the Global South. The Norwegian government specifically highlights MOSIP — the Modular Open Source Identity Platform — which has already provided digital ID to more than 130 million people in the Philippines, Ethiopia, Sri Lanka and Morocco. The triangular cooperation means Norway and India will jointly build and fund digital identity, digital payments, health records and administrative platforms for countries in Africa and Southeast Asia that otherwise lack the technical foundations. For the Nordics the agreement opens the primary angle on several fronts at once. For Datatilsynet, Nkom and Digdir, the deal becomes a concrete testbed for how Norway's implementation of the EU AI Act (KI-loven, out for consultation since June 2025, entering into force in late summer 2026) will handle AI-supported identity verification and biometrics when data is shared via the triangular cooperation channel with India — where a GDPR adequacy decision is absent. For the Storting's Foreign Affairs and Defence Committee and the Ministry of Digitalisation and Public Governance, the DPI track becomes a strategic differentiator versus Sweden, which via Tink, BankID and Open Payments built a more closed Nordic standard; Norway is now branding itself as the open-source bridge between the EU's eIDAS 2.0/EUDI Wallet and the Global South. For Sweden's Digg, the Riksdag Transport Committee and the Swedish Institute of International Affairs, the Norwegian deal is a signal that Sweden's own AI strategy (adopted in February 2026) needs to be complemented with an international DPI pillar, especially as Vinnova has just been tasked to fund EU projects in AI and advanced semiconductors. For Sintef Digital, NORCE, NTNU and UiO, the agreement opens funding tracks toward Indian IIIT Bangalore and iSPIRT (the technical owner of MOSIP); and for Swedish RISE, KTH and Karolinska, the triangular-cooperation model could become a template for how Nordic life sciences and climate AI reach global scale. For Forbrukerrådet, Konsumentverket and Nordic civil-society organisations (Tekna, IT-Pol, Internetstiftelsen, Bits of Freedom), the central scrutiny question will be whether the MOSIP stack can be reverse-engineered for biometric surveillance before commercial rollout in the EU.
