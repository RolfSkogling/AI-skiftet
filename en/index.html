Models

OpenAI opens ChatGPT Health to all adult users in the US

On July 23, OpenAI made its health feature ChatGPT Health available to all users over 18 in the United States, across all subscription tiers.

Users can connect medical records from systems such as Epic and Oracle Health as well as Apple Health data, and get help interpreting test results, diet and exercise. According to OpenAI, roughly 300 million health questions are now asked in ChatGPT every week, up from 230 million at the pilot launch. The rollout comes as the company faces two lawsuits over allegedly dangerous medical advice, and one day after one of them sought to block the feature.

For the Nordics the service is not yet available, but the groundwork is already in place: Norway’s Helseplattformen in Mid-Norway and several Danish regions run the Epic records system that ChatGPT Health connects to. A European launch would be tested hard against the GDPR’s rules on health data, with IMY and Datatilsynet as key players – and it raises the question of how 1177 and Helsenorge will hold up once patients start bringing AI-interpreted test results to their GP.

Source: TechCrunch, Engadget, SiliconANGLE
Debate

White House accuses Moonshot of distilling Anthropic’s Fable

On July 22, White House technology adviser Michael Kratsios accused China’s Moonshot AI of building its giant Kimi K3 model through large-scale distillation of Anthropic’s Fable model.

According to Kratsios, Moonshot built an internal platform that switched between different access routes to avoid detection, and is also said to have reached export-restricted Nvidia servers via Thailand. The Treasury is threatening sanctions and blacklisting. It is the first time a senior US official has singled out a named Chinese lab for copying a named American model. Independent researchers, however, are sceptical that distillation alone explains K3’s top results, and Moonshot has not commented.

For the Nordics the accusation sharpens the dilemma around open models: Kimi K3 is the world’s largest open-weight model and is already used by European developers. If sanctions are imposed, Nordic companies that have built on Chinese open models may be forced to reconsider, and security communities such as Sweden’s FOI and Norway’s NUPI get a stark example of how model weights have become trade policy.

Source: TechCrunch, CyberScoop, TechCrunch (analysis)
Hardware

Intel’s AI comeback: fastest growth in almost 15 years

On July 23, Intel reported second-quarter revenue of $16.1 billion, up 25 percent year on year and well above the expected $14.4 billion.

The engine is the data center and AI business, which grew 59 percent to $6.3 billion. It is the company’s fastest growth since 2011, and CFO David Zinsner says Intel is supply constrained – data center customers are demanding more than the company can deliver. Intel also raised its third-quarter guidance to around $16.3 billion, and the stock rose more than 13 percent in after-hours trading.

For the Nordics the figures confirm that the AI build-out is now lifting chip suppliers beyond Nvidia – relevant for Nordic data centers from Luleå to Skien that need alternative supply chains. At the same time, the capacity shortage reinforces the cost pressure on memory and components that Ericsson has already flagged in its network build-out.

Source: CNBC, Quartz

Norwegian ruling sets precedent for AI-generated abuse imagery

A man from Hammerfest has been sentenced to one year and three months in prison for, among other things, using AI to produce abuse images of girls he knew.

The verdict was handed down by the Indre og Østre Finnmark district court after a confession. In a police raid in March, more than 6,000 files of abuse material were found, including 258 unique AI-manipulated images of girls in the man’s vicinity. The case is one of the first in Norway where AI-generated material has been fully tried in court, and it shows that existing law also covers synthetic images.

For the Nordics the ruling offers guidance on a question both Swedish and Norwegian authorities are wrestling with: Swedish police and Ecpat have warned of a rapid rise in AI-generated abuse material, and the Finnmark verdict becomes a near-precedent example that the tool does not absolve the perpetrator – a line Swedish courts are expected to follow as well.

Source: NRK, Digi.no

Physical AI

Tesla promises Optimus production soon – but the robots stay in the factory

In its quarterly report on July 22, Tesla said it is now installing the first production lines for the Optimus humanoid robot and will «start production soon».

The first robots will be used internally for data collection and further development, not sold to customers. Elon Musk called Optimus «the hardest product we have ever tried to mass-manufacture» because almost every component is newly designed. The quarter showed 480,000 vehicles delivered but an operating margin squeezed to 1.4 percent, down from 4.1 percent a year earlier.

For the Nordics, Tesla’s robot promises are measured against a region that already has physical AI in operation: Norway’s 1X Technologies is developing the NEO home robot, ABB Robotics in Västerås and Denmark’s Universal Robots and MiR ship industrial and collaborative robots at volume. For them, Optimus is both a competitor and a benchmark when customers compare what can actually be bought today.

Source: CNBC, Yahoo Finance
Quantum

Finland’s IQM runs a quantum algorithm on Deutsche Bahn’s real train data

Finnish quantum company IQM and Germany’s Deutsche Bahn have, for the first time, solved a real railway scheduling problem using a hybrid quantum–classical algorithm.

The test used a live dataset of 190 departures across five German cities – roughly 98,500 possible cycles – with the quantum part solving smaller sub-problems inside a classical framework. Three results stand out: the method works on today’s hardware, it improves automatically as quantum processors scale, and the full pipeline ran end to end on an IQM machine.

For the Nordics this is close to home turf: IQM is a Nordic company headquartered in Finland and the first European quantum company on the Nasdaq. The same class of optimization problem faces rail operators SJ and Vy and grid operators Svenska kraftnät and Statnett, while Chalmers’ quantum centre WACQT and NTNU are building the Nordic skills base needed to use the technology.

Source: HPCwire, IQM Quantum Computers
Debate

Anthropic wants to fund large-scale trials against AI-driven unemployment

On July 22, Anthropic published the research agenda for its $200 million fund, which aims to generate evidence on how societies can prepare for AI’s economic upheavals.

The fund names five priorities: shaping AI’s impact on workplaces, equipping people for transitions, modernizing income support, giving workers a stake in AI-driven growth, and generating new evidence on public investment. Concrete ideas include wage insurance, pre-distributive capital accounts and longer trials of unconditional income. Anthropic generally intends to fund projects between $5 and $30 million and will not accept applications below $1 million.

For the Nordics, several proposals sit close to the Nordic model – unemployment insurance, transition support and security agreements negotiated by parties such as LO and NHO. The fund is explicitly global, so research bodies like IFAU in Uppsala, SNS and Norway’s Frisch Centre could apply to test Nordic security solutions against an AI-transformed working life.

Source: Anthropic, ABC News/AP
Labor Market

OpenAI launches Presence – agents already handling three in four support calls

On July 22, OpenAI unveiled Presence, an enterprise platform for building, governing and monitoring AI agents for voice and chat in production.

The platform bundles policies, guardrails, approved actions, simulations and evaluation in one place. Presence already runs on OpenAI’s own English-language support line, where the company says it resolves 75 percent of calls without human intervention. For now the service is offered only to selected enterprise customers via OpenAI’s own engineers, not as self-service.

For the Nordics this touches a large occupational group: customer service and support are staff-heavy functions at Telenor, Telia, DNB and SEB, all of which are already embedding AI agents. When an agent can close three in four calls on its own, it raises concrete questions for unions such as Unionen and Norway’s LO about staffing, reskilling and what happens to entry-level jobs.

Source: OpenAI, VentureBeat

Models

Google ships Gemini 3.6 Flash – but the Pro flagship is still missing

On Tuesday, Google DeepMind released three new models: Gemini 3.6 Flash, 3.5 Flash-Lite and 3.5 Flash Cyber. The flagship Gemini 3.5 Pro, however, is still nowhere to be seen.

Gemini 3.6 Flash is described as the workhorse of the family: better at coding, knowledge work and multimodal tasks, while cutting token usage by up to 17 percent and lowering the output price from 9 to 7.50 dollars per million tokens. Flash Cyber is fine-tuned to find and fix security vulnerabilities and is offered only to governments and selected partners in a pilot programme. Product lead Logan Kilpatrick says 3.5 Pro is now being tested with partners, and that Google has started its most ambitious pre-training run yet – for Gemini 4.

For the Nordics, cheaper workhorse models lower the running costs of the AI agents that Ericsson, SEB and Telenor, among others, are building into their systems. The Flash Cyber pilot is also relevant for Sweden’s signals intelligence agency FRA and its new cyber centre, and for Norway’s NSM – both have singled out AI-driven attacks as a growing threat.

Source: TechCrunch, 9to5Google
Regulation

Norwegian data centre fight: minister rejects licensing requirements

Norway’s digitalisation minister Karianne Tung (Labour) rejects demands for a licensing system for data centres, despite pressure from Høyre, SV and Rødt in the Storting.

Both Høyre and SV have tabled proposals for licensing requirements, and SV’s Lars Haltbrekken wants the vetting handled by the energy regulator NVE or grid operator Statnett. Tung argues that power and capacity questions should be solved through energy policy, warns that a licensing system could breach the EEA agreement, and points out that a registration duty and an updated data centre regulation are already in place.

For the Nordics, the fight mirrors a wider tug-of-war over the AI boom’s physical footprint: in Sweden, broadcaster SVT is scrutinising the promises of 500 Google jobs in Torsboda, and New York’s halt to new AI data centres shows the issue is as much about power, water and local benefit as about digitalisation. The outcome in the Storting could guide Swedish siting policy too.

Source: Digi.no, Digi.no (background)
Infrastructure

Hugging Face breached by autonomous AI agent in first known attack of its kind

AI platform Hugging Face confirms that an autonomous AI agent broke in through the company’s data pipeline and executed more than 17,000 logged actions over a weekend – one of the first documented fully AI-driven intrusions against a major platform.

According to the company, the attacker was an agent framework built on top of an unknown language model. The agent escalated privileges, harvested credentials and moved laterally through the systems, but no signs of tampering were found in public models or datasets. Hugging Face itself used an AI model to reconstruct the intrusion, and now advises other security teams to keep a vetted AI model on standby for future machine-speed attacks.

For the Nordics, the breach confirms the warnings from Telenor Norway CEO Birgitte Engebretsen and from Sweden’s FRA about AI-driven attacks: for MSB, NSM and the Nordic CERT functions, this is a concrete textbook case in which response time, not detection, becomes the critical bottleneck.

Source: Hugging Face, SecurityWeek, Axios
Research

Anthropic opens AI grants for rare disease research

Anthropic is launching a grant programme giving research teams up to 50,000 dollars in Claude credits over six months for rare disease research. Applications are open until August 2.

The programme has two tracks – one for basic research and one for early-stage biotechs in clinical development. The credits can be used for literature reviews, hypothesis generation and analysis of small datasets. Early partners include the Monarch Initiative, whose disease ontologies and knowledge graphs help researchers find connections between diagnoses. Rare diseases are estimated to affect around 400 million people globally, and most still lack treatment.

For the Nordics, the call is directly open to applicants: groups such as Karolinska Institutet’s centre for rare diagnoses, Sahlgrenska and Oslo University Hospital work with precisely the small patient groups where AI-assisted hypothesis generation can make the biggest difference – but the application window closes as soon as August 2.

Source: Anthropic, Clinical Research News

Models

Alibaba previews giant model Qwen3.8 Max, claiming second place in the AI race

At the AI conference in Shanghai, Alibaba previewed its largest model to date: Qwen3.8 Max, with 2.4 trillion parameters. The company claims that only Anthropic’s Fable 5 performs better.

The model is the first Qwen model above the trillion mark that is also multimodal – it handles text, images, video, and documents. The preview is available on Alibaba’s coding platforms at ten percent of the regular price, and open weights are promised soon. No model card or independent benchmark results have been published, however, so the second-place claim rests for now on Alibaba’s own word. The release comes just days after Moonshot’s Kimi K3 topped the coding leaderboards and was forced to pause new sign-ups when capacity ran out.

For the Nordics, Chinese open weights keep changing the calculus: when near-frontier models can be downloaded freely, both prices and the case for homegrown Nordic base models from AI Sweden and Norway’s NorwAI come under pressure. At the same time, Norwegian commentators, most recently in digi.no, warn that the dependency merely shifts from American to Chinese suppliers.

Regulation

EU forces Google to open Android to competing AI assistants

The European Commission has issued two binding decisions under the Digital Markets Act: Google must give competing AI assistants the same system-level access to Android as Google’s own Gemini assistant, and share anonymized search data with challengers on regulated terms.

In concrete terms, rivals will be able to register their own wake words, be activated via long-press, and perform actions inside apps – functions so far reserved for Gemini. Eleven Android features must be open to qualified competitors by August 1, 2027 in Android 18, and the pricing terms for the search data must be finalized by January 2027. American industry voices call the decisions a harmful expansion of the DMA, while the Commission sees them as a cornerstone of competition in the AI market.

For the Nordics, the decisions mean that Swedish and Norwegian Android users will eventually be able to choose, say, France’s Mistral or a Nordic assistant as the system default on their phones. The search data sharing also opens a door for smaller European search challengers – and for Nordic companies building AI agents on top of search.

Source: European Commission, Digi.no
Infrastructure

Telenor chief warns of the next wave of AI-driven cyberattacks

Ever more powerful AI models are making it easier to find and exploit vulnerabilities in digital systems, warns Telenor Norway CEO Birgitte Engebretsen in an interview with digi.no. Both companies and private individuals must sharpen their digital preparedness, she says.

The warning comes in the shadow of a heavy quarterly report: the group’s pre-tax profit fell to around 4 billion Norwegian kroner, from nearly 4.9 billion in the same quarter last year, and the share price dropped by around ten percent after the presentation. Group CEO Benedicte Schilbred Fasmer also lowered the full-year outlook but pointed to efficiency measures – including AI – meant to lift profitability.

For the Nordics, the warning echoes the Swedish government’s recent assignment to the FRA cyber center to counter AI-driven threats: AI is lowering the bar for attackers faster than many defenders can rearm. That one of the Nordics’ largest telecom operators now says so openly will likely increase pressure on Norway’s NSM and Sweden’s MSB to step up support for companies and municipalities – freshly underscored by the cyberattack on Lørenskog municipality.

Source: Digi.no, Nettavisen/NTB
Hardware

China’s memory giant CXMT raises $8.6 billion in record listing

Memory maker ChangXin Memory Technologies (CXMT) is raising 8.6 billion dollars in Shanghai in Asia’s largest stock listing so far this year. Institutional orders exceeded the offering by around 570 times before allocations were adjusted.

CXMT manufactures DRAM memory – the component that has become the great scarcity of the AI boom – and is China’s main hope for reducing dependence on Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron. After the adjustment, the oversubscription ended at just over 210 times, with more than 9.4 million accounts in the bidding, and the valuation lands at around 85 billion dollars ahead of the market debut on July 27. At the same time, analysts point out that the memory market is historically cyclical and that new Chinese capacity could eventually push prices down.

For the Nordics, memory prices are already being felt: Ericsson singled out the memory shortage in its quarterly report as a cost driver in network buildout, and Nordic data center operators such as EcoDataCenter face the same cost pressure. More DRAM capacity – even Chinese – could ease the squeeze over time, but in the short term the listing cements memory as the strategic bottleneck of the AI era.

Source: South China Morning Post, Reuters/Yahoo Finance
Research

Bristol Myers first in pharma with Nvidia’s new supercomputer

Drugmaker Bristol Myers Squibb becomes the first in the life sciences sector to buy a DGX SuperPOD built on Nvidia’s next architecture, Vera Rubin. The system will be used across the entire chain from molecular design to clinical programs.

According to the company, AI tools are already cutting the time it takes to get compounds ready for clinical testing by 20 to 30 percent, and AI is used in all of its small-molecule programs. The new system is said to deliver up to ten times more computing power per megawatt than its predecessor – an increasingly important metric as energy consumption has become a bottleneck. The deal builds on nearly three years of collaboration with Nvidia.

For the Nordics, the deal raises the bar in an industry where British-Swedish AstraZeneca and Denmark’s Novo Nordisk are investing heavily in the same technology – the Novo Nordisk Foundation is already behind the Gefion AI supercomputer in Denmark. The question for Nordic labs is no longer whether the AI infrastructure is needed, but how quickly it can be put in place.

Labour market

SVT scrutinizes the basis for 500 promised Google jobs in Torsboda

Google wants to buy the entire Torsboda industrial site in Timrå, northern Sweden, for an AI data center, and the seller expects at least 500 direct jobs. A review by public broadcaster SVT shows the figure comes from the municipally owned selling company – while Google itself says nothing at all about staffing needs.

The estimate rests on a consultancy analysis by Sweco comparing five earlier Google sites in Finland, the Netherlands, Belgium, Denmark and Ireland. Google says there are neither approved construction plans nor a confirmed timeline, and industry observers note that modern AI data centers may need far fewer employees than older facilities.

The deal, announced on June 18, has become a contested issue in Västernorrland ahead of this fall's Swedish elections: the Green Party is campaigning against the sale, local politicians in Timrå and Sundsvall are split, and the energy company warns that the power contract is becoming urgent. Torsboda is shaping up as a Swedish test of what the AI boom's data centers actually deliver in jobs and tax revenue.

Quantum

Google’s quantum computer learns from its own errors while it runs

A Google-led research team shows in the journal Nature that a quantum computer can keep itself calibrated during an ongoing computation. An AI system reads the stream of error-correction data and adjusts the processor in real time.

On the Willow processor, reinforcement learning cut logical error rates by roughly 20 percent beyond what conventional calibration and expert tuning achieve, and made performance up to 3.5 times more stable when researchers deliberately disturbed the hardware. The system tunes more than 1,000 control parameters simultaneously, without halting the computation for recalibration. Simulations suggest the method scales to machines with tens of thousands of parameters.

For the Nordics, self-calibrating operation is directly relevant: at Chalmers, the Wallenberg Centre for Quantum Technology (WACQT) is building Sweden’s own quantum computer, and Finland’s IQM connects its systems to the LUMI supercomputer. With AI taking over the tuning, small Nordic labs could run larger machines without Google’s army of engineers.

Source: Nature, The Quantum Insider
Debate

Wikimedia warns: AI search is pulling the rug from under Wikipedia

As Wikipedia marks 25 years, Wikimedia Norway warns that AI-generated summaries in search engines threaten the encyclopedia’s entire model. Director Elisabet Carrera tells Klassekampen that AI search is pulling the rug from under the organization.

The AI overviews draw their content from Wikipedia and other sites but mean fewer users click through to the sources, and several sites report traffic drops since Google launched AI summaries in search last year. The Norwegian Bokmål edition, launched in November 2001, today holds nearly 700,000 articles written by unpaid volunteers. NTNU researcher Kristine Ask still calls Wikipedia one of the great human achievements.

For the Nordics the question cuts both ways: small language editions such as Norwegian and Swedish Wikipedia depend heavily on traffic to recruit new volunteers, while the language models from OpenAI, Google and Anthropic are trained on Wikipedia’s very texts. If traffic fades, the resource that both Nordic schoolchildren and the AI models themselves stand on is eroded.

Source: Klassekampen, Digi.no/NTB

Physical AI

Hyundai workers stage history’s first strike over humanoid robots

Around 35,000 workers at Hyundai in South Korea downed tools for four hours a day on July 13–15 in protest against plans to put the humanoid robot Atlas on the factory floor. According to the Wall Street Journal, it is the car industry’s first factory stoppage over humanoid robots.

The union demands that no robot be deployed without a prior labor-management agreement, guarantees against job losses, and a fixed monthly salary that protects incomes if automation cuts working hours; bonus issues are also part of the dispute. The backdrop is that Hyundai, which recently took full ownership of robot maker Boston Dynamics, wants to deploy more than 25,000 robots across Hyundai and Kia plants starting in Georgia in 2028 – just as Atlas was recently shown off as a ball-kicking crowd-pleaser at the FIFA World Cup.

For the Nordics, the conflict is a harbinger. Volvo Cars, Scania, and Hydro are pursuing advanced automation plans, and so far Sweden’s IF Metall and Norway’s Fellesforbundet have met robotization with reskilling and co-determination rather than conflict. The Hyundai strike thus becomes a test case that Nordic industrial unions are likely to study closely ahead of coming bargaining rounds.

Source: Wall Street Journal, Forbes, eWeek
Regulation

OpenAI rolls out age assessment and parental controls in ChatGPT

On Friday, OpenAI presented a comprehensive set of teen protections in ChatGPT: an AI model that assesses the user’s age, expanded parental controls, and more frequent reminders to take breaks.

The age model is now rolling out broadly on consumer plans. If the service is unsure of a user’s age, the under-18 experience is applied by default, and adults can verify their age to unlock the full mode. Parents can set quiet hours, turn off voice mode and image generation, and receive alerts at signs of self-harm. The model rules, the Model Spec, have also been updated with specific teen protections. According to OpenAI, nearly nine in ten teens on the service use it for learning, information seeking, or productivity in a typical week.

For the Nordics, the announcement lands in the middle of an ongoing oversight debate. On July 15, Sweden’s Public Health Agency was tasked by the government with investigating generative AI’s risks to children’s and young people’s mental health, Norway is pushing for age limits on social media, and the EU’s updated AI rules ban AI-generated child sexual abuse material from December. OpenAI’s self-regulation becomes an entry in the debate over whether voluntary safeguards suffice – or whether Sweden’s IMY and Norway’s Medietilsynet and Datatilsynet need sharper tools.

Source: OpenAI, Fox News, Digital Trends
Infrastructure

Inference company Fireworks valued at $17.5 billion

US-based Fireworks AI, which runs and tunes open AI models for companies, has raised $1.5 billion in a Series D round valuing the company at $17.5 billion – one of the largest rounds yet in the fast-growing inference layer.

The round was led by Atreides Management, Index Ventures, and TCV, with Nvidia among the co-investors. The company, founded in 2022 by former Meta engineers with CEO Lin Qiao at the helm, reports an annualized revenue run rate of over one billion dollars – a fivefold increase in a year – and serves over 40 trillion tokens a day to customers such as Uber, Shopify, GitLab, and MongoDB. The business case rests on more and more companies wanting to run specialized, cheaper models instead of paying a premium for the largest closed systems.

For the Nordics, this is precisely the market segment the region’s AI data centers are positioning for: Nscale’s facility in Narvik, Brookfield’s campus in Strängnäs, and EcoDataCenter in Falun are being built to lease out compute with Nordic hydropower as the selling point. And for fast-growing Swedish companies like Lovable, heavy consumers of inference, the price competition is pointing in the right direction.

Source: CNBC, Fireworks, SiliconANGLE
Research

Europe’s frontier-lab contest kicks off – Nordic coalition wants a lab of its own

July marks the start of the build phase of the Next Frontier AI Challenge, the European competition that, with 125 million euros, aims to produce at least three European frontier labs with foundation models of their own. Up to ten teams are funded for 24 months, after the jury made its selection at the end of June.

The program is led by SPRIND, Germany’s federal agency for breakthrough innovation, and was launched by Germany, France, and the European Commission at the EurIPS research conference in Copenhagen in December 2025. Funding is paid out in three stages – up to 3, 8, and 15.5 million euros per team – and in the autumn of 2028 up to three winning labs are to be positioned for funding rounds of up to one billion euros each. No broad public list of the selected teams has yet been published.

For the Nordics, the stakes are concrete. AI Sweden, together with Denmark’s DIREC, the Danish Data Science Academy, and the Nordic AI Partnership network, has mobilized to identify and support Nordic candidate teams, with the goal that at least one of Europe’s coming frontier labs ends up in the Nordics – building on existing infrastructure such as the LUMI supercomputer in Finland and competence hubs like WASP in Sweden and NTNU in Trondheim.

Models

Moonshot’s Kimi K3 becomes the largest open AI model ever

China’s Moonshot AI released Kimi K3 late on Thursday, a model with roughly 2.7–2.8 trillion parameters and a one-million-token context window. That makes it the largest open-track model to date, and Bloomberg describes it as a major step toward narrowing the gap with America’s top models.

K3 is built on a sparse mixture-of-experts architecture with the new Kimi Delta Attention mechanism, has native vision and costs 3 and 15 dollars per million tokens in and out. In blind tests at the evaluator Arena, developers ranked K3 highest on front-end coding – though this is preference voting rather than verified benchmarks, and on broader measures the model is judged to sit just below the best closed systems. The weights are promised as a free download on July 27 – at which point anyone can inspect and run the model locally.

For the Nordics, the July 27 weights release opens a new possibility: research environments like WASP in Sweden and NorwAI at NTNU, as well as public agencies with strict data-sovereignty requirements, can run a frontier-class model on their own infrastructure without data leaving the country. At the same time, it sharpens the question for Nordic companies of what the premium for closed American models actually buys.

Source: Bloomberg, Fortune, Axios
Debate

Xi opens Shanghai AI conference and launches global cooperation body

China’s President Xi Jinping delivered the opening keynote at the World AI Conference in Shanghai on Friday – the first time a Chinese head of state has taken the conference stage since it began in 2018. At the same time, according to several international media outlets, China formally launched the World AI Cooperation Organization – a new intergovernmental AI body headquartered in Shanghai.

Xi called for broad global AI efforts at a moment when US export controls are choking China’s access to advanced chips. The conference runs July 17–20 with more than 140 forums and over 1,100 exhibitors, and the offer is aimed particularly at countries in the Global South: a complete Chinese stack of chips, models and rules. No equivalent American proposal for global AI governance is on the table.

For the Nordics, the move poses an uncomfortable question. Sweden and Norway have anchored their AI policy in the EU’s track through the AI Act, and as rule-making power begins to be shared between Washington, Brussels and Shanghai, the Riksdag, the Storting and the Nordic foreign ministries are forced to consider how small export-dependent countries navigate between the blocs.

Source: NPR, Bloomberg, TechTimes
Research

Sweden’s Neko Health raises 700 million dollars for AI health scanning

Stockholm-based Neko Health, co-founded by Spotify’s Daniel Ek and Hjalmar Nilsonne, has raised 700 million dollars in a Series C round valuing the company at nearly seven billion dollars – a fourfold increase since its previous round in January 2025.

The round was led by Lightspeed Venture Partners and O.G. Venture Partners, with names like Mark Zuckerberg and Priscilla Chan among the investors. The money will fund a US expansion, with the first clinic opening in New York later this year. The company combines proprietary sensor technology with AI analysis to detect signs of disease early, operates eight clinics in Sweden and the UK, has completed over 100,000 full-body scans and already has 300,000 people on its American waiting list.

For the Nordics, this is one of the largest AI rounds ever for a Swedish company and proof that Nordic health tech holds its own in the global race for capital. At the same time, the value of health screening for healthy people is contested in medical research – the risk of false positives and overdiagnosis is a well-known problem – a debate that is alive in Swedish and Norwegian healthcare too.

Source: TechCrunch, Neko Health
Hardware

Huawei shows giant cluster challenging Nvidia – with no American parts

Huawei this week showed off its Atlas 950 SuperPoD system at the AI conference in Shanghai. The system links 8,192 Ascend processors into what in practice works as a single giant computing unit.

According to Huawei, the cluster delivers 8 exaflops of FP8 compute and 6.7 times the power of Nvidia’s NVL144 system – figures that are self-reported and not yet verified by any independent party. The UnifiedBus 2.0 architecture lets each chip address the others’ memory directly, functioning as a memory fabric rather than a network. The message is strategic: Chinese AI infrastructure can now be built entirely without American components, and the demonstration came the same week as the Kimi K3 launch and Xi’s speech.

For the Nordics, the dependence on American chips is total today: the LUMI supercomputer in Finland is built on AMD, while Sweden’s EcoDataCenter and Google’s Norwegian data center in Skien are filled with Nvidia hardware. A credible Chinese stack fragments the supply chain over time and turns chip choice into a geopolitical question for Nordic procurement departments as well.

Source: Seoul Economic Daily, Digitimes
