Regulation

EU softens the AI Act – final sign-off expected in late June

EU countries are approaching the final step of a major overhaul of the AI Act. After the European Parliament voted in favour of the so-called Digital Omnibus package on 16 June, the Council of Ministers is expected to give its formal approval on 29 June, after which the changes will be published in the EU's Official Journal.

The package postpones the heaviest requirements on high-risk AI: stand-alone systems in Annex III of the regulation – covering recruitment, credit scoring and law enforcement – now have until 2 December 2027, and AI embedded in regulated products until August 2028. At the same time, an explicit ban is introduced on AI that generates child sexual abuse material or non-consensual intimate images, taking effect from December 2026. The near-term transparency requirements in Article 50 – labelling AI-generated content – remain in place, however, and start to apply as early as 2 August 2026.

For the Nordics, the message is that the clock is ticking. In Sweden, the Privacy Protection Agency (IMY) was tasked on 15 June with becoming the market surveillance authority for AI, and in Norway a dedicated AI Act is being prepared via the EEA, with Nkom as coordinating supervisor and Datatilsynet among the competent authorities. Swedish and Norwegian companies building AI services therefore need to know, already this summer, what must be labelled and documented – even if the sharpest high-risk requirements are now delayed.

Source: European Council, iubenda, IMY
Labour market

Swedish employers: AI skills have become harder to find than IT skills

For the first time, Swedish employers report that it is harder to recruit AI skills than traditional IT skills. That is the finding of a new survey from the staffing company ManpowerGroup.

In the survey, 18 percent of employers say they struggle to find staff with the right AI knowledge, and 16 percent lack the skills to develop their own AI models. Internationally, the development of AI models and AI applications (20 percent) climbs to the top of the list of the hardest skills to recruit for the first time – ahead of both engineering and classic IT skills. At the same time, there is a broad shortage of qualified labour in Swedish working life, while parts of the traditional IT sector are cutting jobs; Ericsson announced around 1,600 positions in Sweden earlier this year.

The picture is familiar across the Nordics: demand for AI know-how is growing faster than the education systems can keep up, while routine white-collar jobs come under pressure. Sweden's AI Sweden, unions such as Unionen and TCO, and Norway's LO and NHO are all pushing the issue of upskilling. The conclusion is that, for now, the transition is less about mass layoffs and more about quickly building new knowledge among those who already have a job.

Source: ManpowerGroup / TT, TechSverige
Research

AI schedules operating rooms at Sahlgrenska – more procedures in early tests

At Sahlgrenska University Hospital in Gothenburg, AI is being tested to schedule operating rooms more intelligently. Early results point to markedly better use of the rooms.

The system weighs together operating times, staffing, sterilisation times and how urgent each procedure is. The preliminary figures show around 12 percent higher utilisation of the operating rooms, which according to the hospital corresponds to hundreds of additional operations per year without building more rooms. The work is run through the hospital's competence centre for AI, and according to AI Sweden's national care map there are more than 170 AI initiatives under way in Swedish healthcare – even though the five most active regions account for more than 60 percent.

For the Nordics, the capacity shortage in healthcare is a shared challenge, and operating-room planning in particular is an area where AI can deliver concrete benefit without taking over medical decisions. Similar work is under way in Region Stockholm, where Karolinska recently appointed a chief AI officer, and in the Swedish-Norwegian Interreg project Kontiki, which has tested AI-based decision support in healthcare on both sides of the border for three years. At the same time, the wide spread between regions is a reminder of the risk that the benefits are distributed unevenly.

Source: Sahlgrenska, AIkompassen, AI Sweden
Models

The race tightens: GPT-5.6 expected while Anthropic's top models stay blocked

The fight over the sharpest AI model is entering an intense phase. OpenAI is expected to launch GPT-5.6 shortly, while rival Anthropic's most capable models are switched off on the order of US authorities.

GPT-5.6 is reported to be coming in several variants and with an expanded context window of up to 1.5 million tokens, but it has no official launch date yet – several observers expect days rather than weeks. The situation is sharpened by the fact that on 12 June Anthropic was forced to switch off its top models Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 after the US issued an export-control directive. The suspension has not yet been lifted, even though researchers and members of Congress are demanding answers, and OpenAI is reportedly pushing prices down at the same time – something observers see as the start of a price war between the labs.

For the Nordics, the dependence on US models and US politics becomes clear. Nordic companies and developers that had built services on Claude's strongest models lost access practically overnight, without having done anything wrong themselves. It strengthens the arguments for European and Nordic AI sovereignty – from Swedish investments in homegrown compute to the EU's new frontier-model alliance – and makes the question of where the models run, and who can switch them off, a strategic concern for both business and government.

Physical AI

ABB unveils industrial physical AI as humanoids move into production

On Monday, Automate 2026 opened in Chicago, North America’s largest robotics show. This year’s theme is clear: humanoid robots are moving from demonstration to real production. Swedish-Swiss ABB Robotics used the occasion to launch its new “Physical AI Toolchain.”

ABB’s toolchain spans the entire chain from data generation and training to validation and deployment, and builds on the company’s NVIDIA partnership from March 2026, which reaches around 99 percent accuracy from simulation to real robot (sim-to-real). In parallel, Figure is showing its BotQ factory, now building one robot per hour; Boston Dynamics is starting to deliver its electric Atlas to Hyundai and Google DeepMind; and Agility Robotics’ Digit robot is working shifts at a Toyota plant in Canada.

For the Nordics, much is at stake. ABB has large operations in Västerås, Denmark’s Universal Robots and MiR in Odense lead in collaborative robots, and Norway’s 1X builds humanoids. As the robots enter series production, both Nordic manufacturing – Volvo, Scania, Hydro – and the labor market are affected, where LO and NHO are beginning to prepare for what physical AI means for jobs.

Hardware

AI hunger drains the memory market – computers and phones expected to cost more

Explosive demand for AI has triggered a historic shortage of computer memory. Manufacturers are shifting capacity from ordinary consumer electronics to AI data centers, and fast HBM memory is expected to claim around 23 percent of all DRAM production by the end of the year.

According to analyst firm IDC and news agency Bloomberg, prices for DRAM and SSD storage could rise by up to 130 percent by the end of 2026, while computer prices themselves are expected to climb around 17 percent. The cheapest computers, under 500 dollars, risk disappearing in practice. TSMC’s advanced packaging capacity (CoWoS) is sold out for 2026, and a single Nvidia B300 GPU requires eight stacks of HBM3e memory.

For the Nordics it cuts both ways. Swedish retailers and observers such as SweClockers and Inet are already flagging pricier computers, phones and game consoles for Nordic consumers, with the budget segment hit hardest. At the same time, the Nordic AI factories – in Narvik and Telenor’s sovereign AI factory – compete for the same scarce memory: the bottleneck lies mainly in memory, not in the GPUs themselves.

Source: Bloomberg, IDC, SweClockers
Research

AI becomes Norwegian GPs’ new favorite tool – but is it safe?

AI tools that record the doctor’s visit and automatically write the medical note have quickly become Norwegian general practitioners’ (fastleger) favorite. From April 1, 2026, all Norwegian GPs must also be able to offer encrypted video consultations, which is speeding up adoption.

The Journal of the Norwegian Medical Association and DigitalNorway weigh the gains – less time on documentation, more time for the patient – against the risks. Datatilsynet requires that the patient be informed in advance that the conversation is being recorded and what the recording will be used for, and vendors stress that no health data is stored on their servers. The EU AI Act also begins to apply from August 2026 via the EEA Agreement.

For the Nordics, Norway is a living test. Sweden faces the same wave – Nacka municipality is trialing transcription to free up time for social workers, and Region Stockholm is building AI into care. The Norwegian experience gives Swedish regions, Inera and IMY a concrete template for how clinical AI can be introduced without sacrificing patient confidentiality.

Source: DigitalNorway, Tidsskriftet, Datatilsynet

EU picks Italian-led alliance to build its own frontier model in all 24 EU languages

The European Commission has named the EUROPA consortium, led by the Italian company Domyn, as the winner of its Frontier AI Grand Challenge. The task: build an open frontier model with more than 400 billion parameters that masters all 24 official EU languages.

The challenge was launched in February 2026 to strengthen Europe's capacity to develop advanced AI on its own infrastructure. The winner gets a year of access to the EU's supercomputers (EuroHPC) and a dedicated Nvidia cluster to train the model, which will be released as open source.

For the Nordics, Swedish, Danish and Finnish are part of the model's language scope, and one of EuroHPC's flagship machines – LUMI – sits in Kajaani, Finland. The effort runs in parallel with AI Sweden's and WASP's work toward a Swedish language model and Telenor's sovereign AI factory in Norway, all with the same goal: not to be wholly dependent on American models.

Quantum

The Nordics shift up a gear in the quantum race – Finnish 150-qubit machine and a Swedish testbed open

Finnish quantum-computer maker IQM will deliver a superconducting 150-qubit quantum computer to the VTT research institute in Finland during 2026 – a step toward a 300-qubit machine in 2027. The computer will be built into Finland's supercomputing environment and is backed by a state grant of 70 million euros.

In parallel, Chalmers in Gothenburg opens its quantum computer for algorithm testing this year. The Swedish effort – the Wallenberg Centre for Quantum Technology, a twelve-year program worth just over a billion kronor – reached 25 qubits in 2024 and aims for 100 well-functioning qubits by 2029, with a testbed where Swedish industry can try the technology.

For the Nordics this redraws the map: Finland and Sweden are building national quantum resources at the same time, and together with quantum groups at the Niels Bohr Institute in Copenhagen and NTNU in Norway, a Nordic cluster is taking shape. For companies in pharmaceuticals and materials research, the ability to compute problems ordinary computers cannot handle is drawing closer.

Labor Market

AI Sweden: skills development matters more than abrupt job displacement

A new report from AI Sweden, the national center for applied AI, concludes that AI's main imprint on the Swedish labor market will be changed work tasks and new skill requirements – not mass layoffs. Most occupations will be complemented by the technology rather than replaced.

Demand is not only for technical specialists, but for people who can use AI effectively in their existing professions. At the same time, AI Sweden warns that the lack of current statistics makes it hard to target measures, and calls for a joint analytical structure linking research, the social partners and public agencies.

For the Nordics this is a counterweight to the layoff headlines: unions, employers and transition organizations have joined forces in the Labor Market AI Council to strengthen Sweden's capacity to adapt. The message – invest in skills, not panic – also resonates in Norway, where LO and NHO are tracking the same question.

Source: AI Sweden, TechSverige
Debate

Ahead of the autumn election, Swedish authorities brace against AI disinformation and deepfakes

As the September general election approaches, Swedish authorities are stepping up defenses against AI-driven disinformation. The Civil Contingencies Agency (MSB) describes a more serious security situation than in previous elections, with threats of cyberattacks and influence campaigns – primarily linked to Russia.

Several agencies are now running scenario exercises together. The Psychological Defence Agency is tasked with identifying and countering foreign information influence, while the Swedish Agency for the Media has been assigned to strengthen media and information literacy, AI included, across the country. Concerns include deepfakes – manipulated videos that can make a candidate appear to say or do something fabricated.

For the Nordics this is a sharp test of whether democracies can withstand synthetic manipulation. From December 2026 the EU's Digital Omnibus bans AI-generated nude images and tightens labeling of AI content, and Norway is preparing its own election safeguards. How Sweden handles the autumn vote will be a yardstick for its neighbors.

Swedish public-sector AI workshop launches July 1

On July 1, 2026, a national “AI workshop” launches on a limited scale – a shared, secure test environment where Swedish agencies, municipalities and regions can build and try AI services. The Social Insurance Agency (Försäkringskassan) and the Tax Agency (Skatteverket) lead the assignment on the government’s orders.

The initiative comprises around SEK 200 million over 2026–2030, with an extra SEK 100 million for each agency in this year’s budget. The goal is secure, fast and equitable AI: shared infrastructure with logging, traceability and data protection, plus legal guidance so that small municipalities need not build their own from scratch.

For the Nordics, this is one of the most concrete public-sector AI initiatives in the region. It is meant to let all 290 municipalities and 21 regions test AI under the same safeguards and could become a benchmark for neighbours such as Norway and Denmark, which are building their own public AI platforms. At the same time it must work with the EU AI Act, where the Swedish Post and Telecom Authority (PTS) is being built up as the national AI supervisor during 2026.

Debate

US lets the government test AI models before launch – more labs join

The US Department of Commerce, through the Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI), has struck agreements that let the government evaluate AI models before they are released publicly. Google DeepMind, Microsoft and Elon Musk’s xAI are now joining, alongside OpenAI and Anthropic, which already take part.

The agreements build on earlier partnerships from 2024, renegotiated under Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick’s directives and the US “AI Action Plan.” CAISI carries out pre-release evaluations of frontier models’ capabilities and security. There is still no legal requirement – the tests are voluntary – but they mark a clear shift toward government insight into the most powerful models.

For the Nordics, it raises the question of how Europe should respond. The EU has chosen a different path with the AI Act and a coming AI Office, while Sweden builds up the Post and Telecom Authority (PTS) and Norway leans on the Data Protection Authority (Datatilsynet). No Nordic or European government pre-release testing of frontier models exists – sharpening the debate over sovereignty when the models are American.

Quantum

PsiQuantum breaks ground on the world’s first large-scale quantum computer

On 17 June the quantum company PsiQuantum broke ground on what it calls the world’s first large-scale, fault-tolerant quantum computer – a facility in Queensland, Australia. The company has also opened a test and validation lab at Griffith University.

The machine is based on photonics – light particles in silicon chips of the Omega type – rather than superconducting qubits. The goal is enough error-corrected “logical” qubits to run useful computations. The Griffith lab will quality-check the chips with a custom cooling system called Poseidon before they go to the factory, and the company is building an identical facility in the United States.

For the Nordics, the project raises the stakes in the region’s own quantum push. Chalmers WACQT – the Wallenberg Centre for Quantum Technology – is aiming for 100 superconducting qubits by 2029 and is opening its quantum computer for algorithm testing by Swedish industry this year. Finland’s IQM and the Niels Bohr Institute in Copenhagen are pursuing their own tracks. Two technological paths are now competing in earnest, and Nordic researchers and companies must choose where to place their bets.

Regulation

IMY becomes Sweden’s AI watchdog as the EU rules draw near

On 15 June the Swedish government decided that the Authority for Privacy Protection (IMY) will be the market surveillance authority for the EU’s AI Act in Sweden. IMY is to oversee that AI systems are used lawfully and safely, with special responsibility for law enforcement, credit scoring and real-time facial recognition.

The task is shared with the Swedish Post and Telecom Authority and the Financial Supervisory Authority, which become supervisory bodies in their own fields. IMY itself warns that the role requires more funding and expertise, and is now starting to build up its oversight. The decision comes days after the European Parliament pushed back the heaviest high-risk requirements, giving the authority a little more time to prepare.

For the Nordics, the decision maps out who will hold the AI reins. In Norway, the Norwegian Communications Authority (Nkom) becomes the national coordinating supervisory authority when the regulation is adopted via the EEA later this summer, alongside the Data Protection Authority. For Swedish and Norwegian companies, municipalities and hospitals buying AI, it means they will soon have a named authority to answer to – and that demands for documentation and risk management are drawing closer.

Norway becomes a hub for Europe’s AI factories as new capacity rolls out

Norway is emerging as one of Europe’s hottest locations for AI data centers. The Nordic company atNorth has bought land in Haugaland for a build that will start at 120 MW and grow to 350 MW, while the startup Fossefall wants to build “AI factories” on surplus power at around a dozen Norwegian sites.

For the Nordics it is both an opportunity and a conflict of goals. The investments bring jobs and export income to regions such as Narvik, Haugaland and northern Sweden, but they compete with households and industry for electricity. Both the Storting and Swedish politicians are now wrestling with how much grid capacity should go to AI when electrifying industry also demands power.

AI is linked to rising layoffs – but white-collar workers are hit hardest

AI is starting to show up more clearly in U.S. layoff statistics. The consulting firm Challenger, Gray & Christmas counted around 55,000 U.S. job cuts in 2025 where the employer itself cited AI as the reason, and more have been added this year – even as many employers are also hiring more.

The pattern is uneven. It is above all white-collar office jobs – programmers, customer service, data entry, content production and marketing – that are singled out as most exposed, while roles in AI infrastructure, healthcare and skilled trades are in demand. Researchers stress that layoffs are only part of the picture, since some of the jobs are rehired later.

For the Nordics, the warning signal is clear. Swedish and Norwegian white-collar workers – from SEB and DNB to municipal administrations – sit in exactly the roles being singled out. Unions such as Sweden’s TCO and Norway’s LO are already pressing for transition support and reskilling, and the issue will become central as AI use spreads across Nordic working life.

Regulation

European Parliament passes delayed AI rules – and bans AI nude images

On 16 June the European Parliament gave final approval to the “Digital Omnibus on AI”, the package that simplifies and postpones parts of the EU’s AI Act. The vote ended 423 in favour, 57 against and 174 abstentions.

The heaviest obligations on high-risk systems are pushed back – to 2 December 2027 for stand-alone systems and 2 August 2028 for AI built into regulated products. The requirement for machine-readable labelling of AI-generated content is delayed to 2 December 2026. At the same time, a new ban is introduced on non-consensual “nudify” imagery and on AI-generated child sexual abuse material. Digital-rights organisations such as Liberties warn that the simplification erodes protection for fundamental rights.

For the Nordics, the postponement means more time for Swedish and Norwegian companies before the high-risk rules bite – but also continued uncertainty for Sweden’s IMY, Norway’s Nkom and the Datatilsynet, which must build up supervision. The labelling requirement from December 2026 directly affects Nordic media such as SVT and NRK and the public sector’s use of AI. And because Norway adopts the regulation via the EEA, both the Riksdag and the Storting must adjust to a timeline that has now slipped.

Source: European Parliament, European Council, howtheyvote.eu, Sofia Globe

Hardware

Samsung clears the tests for new AI memory HBM4 – but the shortage is expected to last until 2027

Samsung has passed the final quality tests for the next generation of AI memory, HBM4, at both Nvidia and AMD, and large-scale supply is expected to begin in June. At the same time, Samsung and rival SK Hynix warn that the AI-memory shortage could last until 2027 and beyond.

HBM (high bandwidth memory) is the specialised memory stacked close to the AI accelerators that is needed to train and run large models – among them Nvidia’s new Rubin platform and AMD’s MI400. SK Hynix says its HBM, DRAM and NAND capacity is essentially sold out for all of 2026. As manufacturers shift silicon wafers from ordinary DDR5 memory to the more profitable HBM, the price of regular RAM rises, hitting everything from server farms to consumer PCs.

For the Nordics this is the hard physical limit on AI ambitions. Nordic data centres such as Nscale’s facility in Narvik and Sweden’s Sferical AI supercomputer in Linköping – built on Nvidia’s GB300 – depend entirely on HBM-equipped graphics cards, and national research computers at KTH queue behind the hyperscalers. When the memory runs out and prices rise, Nordic “sovereign” computing power becomes both more expensive and harder to get in place in time.

Source: Tom’s Hardware, Data Center Dynamics, Network World

Research

OpenAI tests model behaviour against 1.3 million real conversations – before launch

On June 16, OpenAI presented a method called Deployment Simulation that aims to predict how a new AI model will behave in live use – before it is released to users.

The method reuses de-identified conversations from earlier deployments: it keeps the start of a real conversation and lets the new candidate model write the next response, to see what changes. In its own research paper the company describes running the analysis on roughly 1.3 million conversations from the models GPT-5 Thinking through GPT-5.4, spanning August 2025 to March 2026. It measured both whether the pre-release review caught new, unexpected behaviours and whether it correctly predicted whether problematic behaviours would rise or fall after release.

For the Nordics, the method lands in the middle of the regulation debate. When the EU’s AI Act begins to be enforced against frontier models on August 2, 2026, and Norway’s Nkom and Sweden’s IMY are to be able to scrutinise how models are tested, documented pre-release evaluation becomes a central requirement. The method is also relevant to Nordic AI-safety research at WASP, KTH and NTNU, which work on how model behaviour can be predicted and independently audited.

Three in five Norwegians worry about privacy when the state uses AI

A recent citizen survey from Norway’s public-administration agency DFØ shows that three in five Norwegians – 59 percent – have little confidence that privacy is protected when public authorities use AI.

The result comes as Norway prepares for the EU’s AI Act, which takes effect in the country in late summer 2026. The government has sent a proposal for a new AI law out for consultation, designated Nkom as the national coordinating AI authority, and set up KI Norge within the Norwegian Digitalisation Agency as an arena for responsible AI. Datatilsynet, meanwhile, runs a regulatory sandbox where companies and authorities can test AI under supervision.

For the Nordics, the figures show that the technical rollout has outrun trust. The same pattern is visible in Sweden, where IMY and municipalities wrestle with how AI should be used in healthcare, schools and government decisions without eroding trust. As the EU’s rules approach enforcement on August 2, both Nkom and IMY face the same task: showing citizens that AI in the public sector can be trusted.
