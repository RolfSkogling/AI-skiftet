Infrastructure

Software giants unite behind agent standard challenging Anthropic's MCP

Google, Microsoft, Salesforce, Snowflake, and ServiceNow have agreed to back a shared protocol for how AI agents connect to enterprise systems and data, The Information reports. The move is openly framed as an attempt to push back against Anthropic and OpenAI in the enterprise market.

The battle is over the plumbing of enterprise AI: the standards that decide how agents reach tools, data, and each other. Anthropic's MCP protocol has become the de facto standard for tool connections over 18 months, and the new alliance gathers companies that together own most of the world's business data and workflows. At the same time, the same companies sit alongside OpenAI and Anthropic in the Linux Foundation's Agentic AI Foundation, which is meant to produce open agent standards – cooperation in the foundation, fierce competition in the market.

For the Nordics, the standards war touches every organization currently building AI agents on top of Microsoft 365, Salesforce, and Snowflake – from SEB and Telia to Equinor and Norwegian municipalities. Which protocol wins determines where the integrations live and how hard the lock-in becomes – a question Sweden's Digg and Norway's Digdir will have to weigh as the public sector procures agent platforms.

Source: The Information, Tom's Hardware
Regulation

EU's new cloud and AI act out for Swedish consultation – replies due October 2

The Swedish government has sent the European Commission's proposed cloud and AI regulation, the Cloud and AI Development Act, out for national consultation and has submitted its explanatory memorandum to the Riksdag. Consultation responses are due by October 2.

The proposal, presented by the Commission on June 3, aims to strengthen Europe's cloud and AI ecosystem on three fronts: research and development of next-generation technology, faster build-out of data centers with a focus on essential public functions, and a single EU-wide framework for assessing sovereignty in cloud services. The goal is to roughly triple the EU's data center capacity within five to seven years. In its memorandum, the government takes a positive view of the ambition to strengthen the EU's capacity, competitiveness, and digital sovereignty.

For the Nordics, the proposal is business-critical: Sweden and Norway compete for AI data centers with abundant power and cooling, from Luleå and Boden to Narvik, and faster permitting across the EU could both boost the build-out and sharpen the competition. Swedish authorities and industry players have until October 2 to respond, and the consultation round lays the groundwork for Sweden's position when negotiations begin in the Council.

Source: Government of Sweden, European Commission
Models

Goldman Sachs names its favorite Chinese AI models for clients

Goldman Sachs is now explicitly recommending Chinese AI models to its clients, CNBC reports. The bank's favorites are DeepSeek and ByteDance, along with publicly listed Zhipu, whose GLM models are praised for performing close to the frontier.

On July 9, the bank's thematic investing team added China's AI value chain to its core recommendations. Zhipu and DeepSeek are judged to lead in text models, while ByteDance has pulled ahead in multimodal and video. The backdrop is intense price pressure: Chinese open-weight models deliver a large share of frontier capability at a fraction of the price, and Goldman expects Chinese model revenue to grow from roughly 35 billion yuan this year to nearly 880 billion by 2030. Meanwhile, OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google are jointly trying to block Chinese attempts to distill their top models.

For the Nordics, the development puts IT leaders at companies like H&M, Telia, and DNB in front of an uncomfortable calculation: the price gap makes Chinese models hard to ignore for simpler high-volume tasks, while security requirements and NIS2 rules make the choice sensitive. When Wall Street's most established bank normalizes Chinese models, the pressure also grows on European alternatives such as France's Mistral.

Hardware

SK Hynix completes the largest foreign listing in US history

South Korean memory maker SK Hynix listed its depositary receipts on the Nasdaq on Friday, raising $26.5 billion – the largest US listing by a foreign company ever. As of today, Monday, the stock trades under its permanent ticker SKHY.

The offering comprised 177.9 million depositary receipts at $149 apiece and surpasses Alibaba's 2014 record of $25 billion. The stock opened around $170, up roughly 14 percent. SK Hynix controls about 60 percent of the global market for HBM – the extremely high-bandwidth memory built into Nvidia's AI processors that has become the bottleneck of the entire AI buildout.

For the Nordics: HBM chips sit inside the Nvidia processors now filling Nordic AI data centers, from Stargate Norway in Narvik to the facilities in Luleå. Norway's sovereign wealth fund has also long been a shareholder in SK Hynix and thus gets a direct share of the rally.

Source: Yahoo Finance, Quartz
Quantum

Finland's IQM to build the quantum computer wired into the LUMI supercomputer

The LUMI AI Factory and Finland's CSC have selected IQM to deliver a new quantum computer, named LUMI-IQ. The system, an IQM Halocene H4 with 150 qubits, is to be installed in Kajaani, Finland, by 2027.

The quantum computer will be integrated directly with the European supercomputer LUMI so that classical and quantum processors can work in the same computational flow – including for developing hybrid AI models. The deal was announced on July 8 and is one of the largest European orders for a quantum computer intended for hybrid AI operation. IQM received clearance for a Nasdaq listing as recently as June.

For the Nordics: the LUMI consortium includes Sweden through the Swedish Research Council and Norway through Sigma2, so Swedish and Norwegian researchers will get access to the machine. The order also raises the tempo for Chalmers and WACQT, which are building Sweden's own quantum computer in Gothenburg.

Source: The Quantum Insider, HPCwire
Labour market

The Fed examines AI's impact on jobs – Andreessen leads the task force

The US Federal Reserve is setting up a task force to assess how AI affects productivity, jobs and monetary policy. Venture capitalist Marc Andreessen, Stanford economist Charles I. Jones and Microsoft's Asha Sharma will lead the group.

The "Productivity and Jobs" task force is one of five that Fed Chair Kevin Warsh presented on July 9 as part of a broad review of how the central bank conducts monetary policy. The mandate is to assess the economic impact of new general-purpose technologies such as AI: if the technology lifts productivity, the economy can grow faster without inflation – but if it displaces jobs at scale, the central bank faces far harder trade-offs. The choice of Andreessen, one of the AI industry's biggest investors, has already raised questions about conflicts of interest. Jones is currently on leave for a stint at Anthropic.

For the Nordics: the Riksbank and Norges Bank are wrestling with the same question – how AI should factor into interest-rate and inflation assessments. The Fed's conclusions tend to set the tone for smaller central banks, and the analyses will feed the debate on AI and employment already under way at Statistics Sweden, NAV and the social partners.

Source: Federal Reserve, HousingWire
Models

OpenAI's new voice models listen and speak at the same time

OpenAI has launched GPT-Live, a new generation of voice models that can listen and speak simultaneously. The technique, known as full duplex, makes conversations more human-like: the model can interject an "mhm" or stay quiet while the user thinks.

GPT-Live-1 mini replaces the previous advanced voice mode in ChatGPT by default, while paying users get access to the larger GPT-Live-1. For harder questions, the voice model hands the task to a top-tier model in the background – GPT-5.5 at launch – and reads out the answer when it is ready. The models have been rolling out globally since July 8.

For the Nordics: more natural AI voices affect everything from Telenor's and Telia's customer service to voice-driven services at banks such as SEB and DNB. How well the duplex technique handles Swedish and Norwegian remains untested – a recurring dilemma when voice AI is optimized for English first.

Source: OpenAI, TechCrunch

Google's Gemini 3.5 Pro targets a wide launch on July 17 – six weeks late

Google DeepMind plans to release its flagship model Gemini 3.5 Pro widely on July 17, according to reports in several tech outlets. The company is said to have scrapped the old architecture and rebuilt the model from the ground up.

Pro is promised with a 2-million-token context window – twice the size of Gemini 3.5 Flash and among the largest at the frontier – and a reasoning mode called Deep Think behind a $250-a-month subscription. The launch is six weeks late and lands just days after OpenAI's GPT-5.6 and Musk's Grok 4.5. Google has not yet officially confirmed the date; the API currently lists only Gemini 3.5 Flash and 3.1 Pro.

For the Nordics, a two-million-token window means Swedish and Norwegian organizations can feed entire codebases or multi-year medical records into a single call – relevant for companies such as Ericsson and DNB and for healthcare in Region Stockholm. At the same time, competition among Google, OpenAI, Anthropic and xAI is pushing down the prices Nordic developers pay.

Debate

Apple sues OpenAI over theft of trade secrets on AI hardware

On July 10, Apple filed a lawsuit against OpenAI in federal court in Northern California, accusing the AI company of systematically stealing confidential hardware technology. The suit also names Jony Ive's company io Products and two former Apple employees.

According to the complaint, OpenAI's hardware chief Tang Tan, a former Apple vice president of product design, urged Apple employees to share secrets during job interviews, and engineer Chang Liu allegedly kept an Apple laptop and accessed the company's cloud storage after leaving. Apple says more than 400 former Apple employees now work at OpenAI. It is a sharp turn for two companies that struck a partnership in 2024.

For the Nordics, the dispute puts a spotlight on how fiercely the US giants are fighting over hardware talent – an issue that touches Swedish and Norwegian engineers at Ericsson, Nordic Semiconductor and Axis. The outcome may also matter for when AI hardware built on disputed technology reaches Nordic homes.

Source: CNBC, Bloomberg, TechCrunch
Research

Chalmers AI predicts molecular behavior 10,000 times faster

Researchers at Chalmers and the University of Gothenburg have trained an AI model that simulates how molecules behave about 10,000 times faster than traditional molecular dynamics. The work was carried out with AstraZeneca and published in the journal Science Advances.

Instead of calculating every tiny time step, the model learns the underlying rules for how molecules change and skips the intermediate steps. It was tested on more than 12,500 organic molecules and just over 1,000 peptides and can predict binding, stability and side-effect risk. A calculation that once took weeks can shrink to minutes – but the model does not replace clinical testing.

For the Nordics, this is a cutting edge in Nordic pharmaceutical research: AstraZeneca has large operations in Gothenburg, where much of the money in early development is spent. Faster screening of candidates can shorten the path to new medicines and strengthens the case for the national AI push that Chalmers, KTH and Linköping drive through WASP.

Source: Chalmers, Millenniemålen
Physical AI

Norway's 1X shows a robot hand that can zip jackets and sort screws

The Norwegian-American robotics company 1X Technologies on July 9 unveiled a new robot hand with 25 degrees of freedom for its NEO humanoid. The hand is tendon-driven and senses contact forces in the fingertips.

In demonstrations, the hands assembled Lego models, handled screws and coins, plugged in USB-C cables, closed zippers and caught soft balls. 1X says it has a production line with capacity for 10,000 hands a year and plans to start delivering NEO to US homes for about $20,000 before the end of the year.

For the Nordics, 1X – with Norwegian roots – is one of the clearest examples that European physical AI can compete at the top with Tesla and Figure. The progress also puts pressure on Nordic robotics players such as ABB Robotics, Universal Robots and Kongsberg to keep pace as foundation models move into the hardware itself.

Musk’s Grok 4.5 launches as an Opus challenger – Europe must wait

SpaceXAI – formerly xAI – has launched Grok 4.5, which the company calls its most intelligent model to date, built for coding and agentic tasks. Elon Musk describes it as an Opus-class model, but faster, more token-efficient and cheaper.

The model was trained on tens of thousands of Nvidia GB300 processors and on data from the coding tool Cursor, whose owner Anysphere SpaceX is acquiring for 60 billion dollars. Pricing is set at 2 dollars per million input tokens and 6 dollars for output – well below Anthropic’s Opus 4.8 at 5 and 25 dollars respectively. Grok 4.5 is available through the coding agent Grok Build, Cursor and the SpaceXAI developer console, and the release came just a day before OpenAI’s broad GPT-5.6 launch.

For the Nordics, access is delayed: the EU launch is expected only in mid-July, so Swedish and Norwegian developers must wait while American competitors start building. Once the model lands, the price level puts pressure on the cost of agent systems at Nordic companies such as Ericsson, DNB and SEB – in a model summer where GPT-5.6 and Fable 5 are already in place.

Ant Group leads major round in Chinese home-robot maker Zeroth

Suzhou-based Zeroth Robotics is raising around 74 million dollars in a pre-Series A round led by Alibaba-affiliated Ant Group. The money will be used to expand the company’s push into AI robots for the home.

Debate

Rushdie dismisses AI in literature: no capacity for originality

Author Salman Rushdie dismisses AI’s role in literature, film and storytelling. Asked what place the technology should have in creative work, the Booker Prize winner answers: none, zero.

AI can absorb enormous amounts of information and produce variants of it, but it cannot create something no one has done before – and that is exactly what art is about, Rushdie tells Variety in connection with receiving a cultural award in London. Art should also challenge, he adds: when you challenge people they do not always like it, but that is all the more reason to do it.

For the Nordics, the remarks land in the middle of an ongoing copyright battle: this week Norway’s digi.no revealed that books by Norwegian bestselling authors had been used in pirated datasets for AI training, and the Rushdie interview quickly spread through Norwegian media via the news agency NTB. For authors and publishers in Sweden and Norway, the comments add weight to demands for licensing and compensation when protected works are used in AI training.

Source: Variety, Digi.no/NTB
Infrastructure

Økokrim warns: Norwegian data centres could be used for money laundering

Norway’s economic crime authority Økokrim warns in its 2026 risk assessment that data centres could be exploited for money laundering. The industry is immature and lacks established norms, which makes it vulnerable to criminal actors, says agency head Pål Lønseth.

The telecoms regulator Nkom has 111 registered data centres, spread across 59 commercial operators. Several are foreign-owned, in some cases from countries without security-policy cooperation with Norway, Lønseth notes – while stressing that the warning is not based on any concrete suspicion. The industry body Norwegian Data Center Industry rejects the notion of an industry-wide problem, but defends keeping customer lists confidential for security reasons.

For the Nordics, the warning comes in the middle of a historic, AI-driven data centre boom: in Narvik, Nordkraft and Nscale are building one of Europe’s largest AI data centres, and similar facilities are emerging in Luleå and Boden in Sweden. Tougher scrutiny of ownership and money flows may become the new normal for the industry on both sides of the border.

Source: E24, Digi.no/NTB

OpenAI opens GPT-5.6 to everyone – three new models launch today

OpenAI is today making the full GPT-5.6 family generally available: the flagship Sol, the mid-tier Terra and the entry-level Luna. The announcement came on Wednesday, following a preview period in which Sol reached only selected partners.

Sol is the flagship for complex reasoning and agentic tasks, while Terra, according to OpenAI, matches its predecessor GPT-5.5 at roughly half the price – 2.50 dollars per million input tokens and 15 dollars for output. Luna costs 1 and 6 dollars respectively, and Sol's fast mode delivers up to 750 tokens per second. According to American media, the rollout was delayed after pressure from the Trump administration, which wanted a staggered release, but the restrictions have now been lifted. The launch lands in the middle of an intense model summer: Anthropic re-launched Fable 5 on July 1, and Google's Gemini 3.5 Pro is expected later in July.

For the Nordics, the news means that Swedish and Norwegian developers – who have so far been building on GPT-5.5 while users expected GPT-5.6, a gap recently debated in Norway's Digi – now get access to the same models as their American competitors. Terra's halved price lowers the threshold for AI services at major Nordic companies such as Ericsson, Telenor and DNB, while the launch also increases the pressure on European alternatives such as France's Mistral.

Quantum

Microsoft moves its quantum-safe encryption deadline forward to 2029

Microsoft is bringing forward its target for the transition to quantum-safe encryption: the company's most important products and services are to be protected against quantum computer attacks by 2029, four years earlier than the roadmap towards 2033 published last year.

The background is that advances in quantum research have led Microsoft to conclude that cryptographically relevant quantum computers may arrive sooner than expected. Attackers can moreover already harvest encrypted traffic today in order to decrypt it once the machines exist – a tactic known as "harvest now, decrypt later". The effort focuses on three areas: network encryption with broader adoption of TLS 1.3, stored data where organisations need crypto-agility, and cryptographic trust chains. American and French authorities are already pointing towards quantum-safe requirements around 2030 for sensitive systems.

For the Nordics, the announcement puts pressure on both authorities and industry. Norway's security agency NSM and Sweden's MSB have both highlighted the quantum transition in their guidance, and banks such as DNB, SEB and Nordea manage encrypted data with a lifespan of decades. At Chalmers, the Wallenberg centre WACQT drives Swedish quantum research, and commentators in Norway's Digi recently warned that the timeline for the quantum threat is collapsing.

Source: Microsoft, The Quantum Insider, Digi.no

Norwegian bestsellers found in pirated dataset used to train AI models

Books by 27 Norwegian authors – including Jo Nesbø, Vigdis Hjorth and Margit Sandemo – have been used to train large language models. That is shown by a survey by researchers at BI Norwegian Business School and OsloMet, Klassekampen reports.

The researchers examined the Scandinavian material in the pirated dataset Books3, which has been used in the training of several language models, including Meta's Llama. Sandemo is the most frequently occurring author from the Scandinavian publishers: 21 of the 47 titles in her much-loved series "The Legend of the Ice People" are included. The content is almost exclusively fiction, according to co-author Terje Colbjørnsen at BI. Publishing director Elizabeth Sellevold at Bladkompaniet, which manages Sandemo's series, was unaware of the matter and calls it a clear breach of copyright.

For the Nordics, the findings provide concrete ammunition in the ongoing copyright battle over AI training. Norway is preparing its AI act to implement the EU's AI regulation, under which developers of general-purpose AI models are already required to disclose their training data, and Norwegian author and publisher organisations now have documented examples to lean on. The survey is also likely to be read closely by the Swedish Writers' Union, which has pursued the same issue against the AI companies.

Source: Digi.no/NTB, Klassekampen

DeepSeek is designing its own AI inference chip

Chinese AI lab DeepSeek is developing its own chip for inference – the stage where trained models answer users’ queries – according to a Reuters exclusive on Tuesday based on three sources familiar with the plans. The project is still at an early stage.

DeepSeek has explored the idea for about a year, held talks with companies in chip design, manufacturing and memory, and is trying to recruit experienced chip designers. The goal is to reduce its reliance on both Nvidia and domestic rival Huawei. That puts the company on the same path as OpenAI, which unveiled the Jalapeño inference chip with Broadcom in June, and Anthropic, which according to earlier reports is in talks with Samsung about a chip of its own.

For the Nordics, the news is yet another sign that inference is moving from Nvidia’s GPUs to specialized silicon – a shift that affects the calculus for Nvidia-heavy Nordic data center ventures such as Nscale in Narvik and EcoDataCenter in Falun. DeepSeek’s open models already run at Swedish and Norwegian developers, and if the company brings down its own operating costs, price pressure on AI services may continue – benefiting Nordic companies building on open models, though with the data-handling questions European regulators have previously raised about DeepSeek.

Source: Reuters/US News, Bloomberg, SiliconANGLE
Infrastructure

Amazon borrows at least $25 billion to fund its AI buildout

Amazon tapped the U.S. bond market on Tuesday for at least $25 billion earmarked for the company’s AI infrastructure. The debt is being issued in up to eight tranches with maturities from three to 40 years, with Barclays, Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan and Morgan Stanley managing the sale.

Demand was strong: the order book peaked near $62 billion before terms were tightened, settling around $41 billion – roughly 1.6 times the size of the deal. Amazon expects capital expenditures of about $200 billion this year, up from $131 billion in 2025, with most of the spending going to data centers, chips and other equipment. The company has already raised some $54 billion on bond markets in the U.S. and Europe this year plus $10 billion in Canada in June, but says, according to CNBC, that this will be its last issuance of the year.

For the Nordics, the deal confirms that the AI race is increasingly funded with borrowed money – precisely the trend Norway’s oil fund warned about in its bubble warning in June. Nordic institutions such as the oil fund, Sweden’s AP funds and the insurers are major buyers of U.S. corporate bonds and thus carry a growing share of the risk if AI revenues are slow to materialize. At the same time, Amazon Web Services has operated a cloud region in Sweden since 2018, so part of the investment wave may well land on Nordic soil and in Nordic power grids.

Microsoft cuts 4,800 jobs as AI spending hits record levels

Microsoft announced 4,800 layoffs on Monday, just over 2 percent of its global workforce. Gaming takes the hardest hit: 3,200 of the positions will disappear within Xbox through fiscal year 2027, as the division prepares to spin off game studios and the sales organization is restructured.

The cuts come as the company lifts spending to record levels: about $190 billion in capital expenditures for calendar year 2026, an increase of more than 60 percent and far above the roughly $155 billion analysts had expected. Nordkraft and Nscale form operations company for the Narvik AI data center

Norwegian power company Nordkraft and AI infrastructure firm Nscale are forming the jointly owned Nordscale Operations AS, which will run operations at Nscale’s AI data center in the Narvik region. Nscale Norway will own 51 percent and Nordkraft 49 percent, according to Monday’s announcement.

Regulation

UN gathers 193 countries in Geneva for first global AI dialogue

The UN’s first Global Dialogue on AI Governance opened yesterday in Geneva and concludes today. All 193 member states, along with researchers, industry and civil society, are meeting at the Palexpo convention centre to discuss common rules for the technology – the first time this happens in a formal UN forum.

The basis is the preliminary report from the UN’s Independent International Scientific Panel on AI, made up of 40 researchers and published on July 1. The panel notes that the United States accounts for roughly three quarters of the computing power behind the world’s leading AI supercomputers and China for around 15 percent – and warns that the window for effective global governance remains open but may not stay that way for long. Secretary-General António Guterres warned at the opening of catastrophic harm if governance fails to keep pace, calling among other things for a global pledge to protect children from manipulative AI products.

For the Nordics, the forum is an opportunity to advance small states’ interest in common rules rather than a bilateral tug-of-war between the US and China. Sweden arrives with its first comprehensive AI strategy from February and Norway with KI Norge and a new AI act implementing the EU’s AI regulation – and for the supervisory authorities IMY and Datatilsynet, the panel’s recurring reports become an independent scientific basis to lean on.

Source: UN News, UN News (panel report), ITU
Research

Security researchers document first fully AI-driven ransomware attack

The threat research team at security firm Sysdig has documented what it describes as the first ransomware operation carried out entirely by an autonomous AI agent, without human direction. The attacker, named JadePuffer, handled the whole chain itself: intrusion, credential theft, lateral movement through the network, and encryption.

The agent exploited a known vulnerability in the developer tool Langflow to obtain cloud and API keys, moved on to a production server and encrypted 1,342 configuration items. The encryption key was never saved, so the victim cannot recover the data even by paying. The report was published on July 1 – just weeks after the cybersecurity chiefs of the Five Eyes countries jointly warned on June 22 that AI-driven attacks are expected to break through within months rather than years.

For the Nordics, the finding sharpens the urgency of warnings already issued by Norway’s NSM and Sweden’s MSB and CERT-SE about AI-augmented attacks. Companies like Telenor, DNB and Equinor, as well as Swedish public agencies in the middle of AI rollouts, must now expect attackers operating at machine speed – and treat exposed developer tools with LLM connections as one of the first attack surfaces.

Source: BankInfoSecurity, Computing, CBS News
Labour market

Weak US jobs report adds new fuel to the AI debate

The United States added just 57,000 new jobs in June – roughly half the 115,000 analysts expected and one of the weakest monthly figures in years, according to the report from the statistics agency BLS published on Thursday.

Unemployment nevertheless fell to 4.2 percent, but for the wrong reason: labour force participation dropped to 61.5 percent, the lowest level since March 2021. Professional and business services grew by 36,000 jobs and healthcare by 22,000, while leisure and hospitality lost 61,000. The BLS does not cite AI as a cause, but the report adds new fuel to the debate among economists over how much automation contributes to companies holding back on hiring despite healthy profits.

For the Nordics, summer figures from Statistics Sweden (SCB) and Norway’s NAV arrive later in July. Earlier Nordic data has pointed the other way – job ads with AI components are holding up in Sweden and Norway while overall volumes decline, and companies like Ericsson, Telenor and the major banks continue to recruit AI talent. The question is whether the American slowdown is a cyclical signal or an early sign of automation’s effect.

Source: BLS, CNBC, Morningstar
Hardware

OpenAI runs top model Sol on Cerebras giant chip at record speed

OpenAI’s new frontier model GPT-5.6 Sol will begin serving on Cerebras wafer-scale hardware during July at up to 750 tokens per second, the company says. That is roughly ten times faster than what typical GPU clusters deliver for a frontier-class model. Access is initially limited to select customers while capacity is built out.

Sol was previewed on June 26 under the US government’s voluntary pre-deployment review, and according to Axios the White House offices for cybersecurity and science policy have asked OpenAI to roll the model out in stages to approved partners – the first time a frontier model launch has been formally staged by the US government. Cerebras has previously disclosed a multi-year OpenAI contract worth over 20 billion dollars, and the track is separate from the Jalapeño inference chip from Broadcom that OpenAI presented on June 29.

For the Nordics, inference at that speed changes the calculus for companies building on OpenAI’s API – from Norwegian industrial AI company Cognite to Swedish developer platform Lovable – where response time often determines what can be built. It is also another sign that inference is moving to specialised silicon, which affects Nordic data centre ventures like Nscale in Narvik as Nvidia’s dominance loosens.

Source: OpenAI, Axios, Tech Times
