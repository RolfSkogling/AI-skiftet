Models

Anthropic releases Claude Opus 4.7 broadly — 13 per cent coding lift, but acknowledges Mythos is still held back

On April 16, Anthropic made Claude Opus 4.7 generally available through Claude.ai, the API, Amazon Bedrock, Google Cloud Vertex AI, Microsoft Foundry and GitHub Copilot. The company states that the model delivers a 13 per cent lift on coding benchmarks, resolves roughly three times more production-grade tasks than Opus 4.6, supports high-resolution vision up to 3.75 megapixels and uses a new tokenizer. Pricing is unchanged from Opus 4.6. At the same time, Anthropic openly confirms — to CNBC and Axios — that 4.7 is "less broadly capable" than the internal Claude Mythos Preview, which the company continues to withhold on safety grounds after it triggered the ASL-4 protocol. The release is a direct response to OpenAI's GPT-5.4-Cyber last week: the market gets a productised, broader model while the most capable variants stay inside closed programs. For Swedish and Nordic organisations this means Claude-based coding and agent workflows get an immediate measurable lift — but also that the gap between publicly available models and what the labs can actually build is widening further.

Source: Anthropic, CNBC, Axios, 9to5Mac, GitHub Changelog
Infrastructure

Microsoft takes over Stargate Norway — OpenAI pulls back from 230 MW Narvik data centre, Nscale stays as operator

Microsoft and neocloud provider Nscale confirmed on April 14–15 that Microsoft will take over the capacity OpenAI had originally booked at the Stargate Norway facility in Narvik. Announced in July 2025, the site is planned at 230 megawatts and will host over 100,000 Nvidia GPUs by year end. Microsoft is now taking on the contract for 30,000 upcoming Nvidia Vera Rubin chips from Nscale, inside the Arctic Circle. OpenAI is stepping back from the direct agreement and is instead in talks with Microsoft about renting the capacity indirectly — part of a broader move in which the company has cut its infrastructure outlook to around $600 billion through 2030, down from the $1.4 trillion previously signalled. For Norway, the deal means continued large-scale hyperscale investment in the north, but strategic control shifts from OpenAI to Microsoft. For the Nordics it is a reminder that the region's large AI data centres are being built by neocloud operators on contract to U.S. frontier labs — and that those contracts can move essentially overnight.

Source: CNBC, TechRepublic, Quartz, Sherwood News, WinBuzzer
Labour

AI Sweden to lead Google-funded AI upskilling — 13,000 workers across five Northern European countries

AI Sweden announced this week that it will lead a new cross-border training initiative to give 13,000 workers in Sweden, Finland, Denmark, Belgium and the Netherlands critical AI skills. The effort is part of the Google.org AI Opportunity Fund and forms the Northern European component of the larger "AI Works for Europe" programme, whose total $30 million will reach 50,000 workers across the UK, Northern and Central Europe. Training is aimed primarily at professions with high AI exposure — administration, legal, communications and customer service — and will be delivered in local languages. AI Sweden is responsible for content development and coordinating across the five participating countries. The initiative arrives just as the Stanford AI Index, Deloitte's Nordic report and BCG's "value bubble" warning all point in the same direction: broad tool adoption is not enough — strategic upskilling is what Nordic companies need to translate AI into real productivity.

Source: AI Sweden, Google, Google AI Opportunity Fund, TechBuzz AI

Research

Stanford AI Index 2026: frontier models surpass human experts — but AI incidents up 55 per cent

Stanford's annual AI Index Report, published on April 14, provides the most detailed snapshot yet of AI's acceleration. Frontier models now meet or exceed human baselines at PhD level in science, multimodal reasoning and competition mathematics. On the coding benchmark SWE-bench Verified, performance rose from 60 to nearly 100 per cent in a single year. Organisational adoption reached 88 per cent and generative AI achieved 53 per cent population adoption in just three years — faster than the personal computer and the internet. At the same time the report shows that employment among software developers aged 22–25 has fallen nearly 20 per cent since 2024, that China is closing in on the U.S. lead (Claude Opus 4.6 leads by just 2.7 percentage points over ByteDance's Dola-Seed Preview), and that documented AI incidents rose from 233 to 362 during 2025 — a 55 per cent increase. U.S. private AI investment reached $285.9 billion, 23 times China's $12.4 billion. The report's clearest message: responsible AI is not keeping pace with capability development.

Source: Stanford HAI, IEEE Spectrum, MIT Technology Review, The Register
Labour

Deloitte: 56 per cent of Nordic organisations have broader AI access — but fear of mass job losses remains low

Deloitte's "State of AI in the Nordics 2026" report, based on a survey of 170 senior executives from Denmark, Finland, Norway and Sweden benchmarked against 3,235 respondents globally, shows that the share of Nordic organisations where at least 40 per cent of employees have access to approved AI tools rose from 37 to 56 per cent in one year. Despite the rapid spread, fear of mass layoffs remains low — Nordic executives express significantly less concern about large-scale redundancies than their global counterparts. The report suggests that the Nordic labour-market model with strong unions and transition agreements acts as a buffer, but warns that tool adoption without strategic workflow redesign risks recreating the ROI gap BCG identified in March. The figures reinforce the picture of a region giving employees broad AI access without yet translating it into transformative results.

Source: Finnish AI Region, Solita, BCG
Regulation

Regulation wave for AI companion apps — Washington and New York require crisis detection and AI labelling

Several U.S. states are now tightening rules for AI companion apps — chatbots that simulate friendship or romantic relationships. Washington signed HB 2225 on March 24, requiring AI chatbots to clearly inform users they are interacting with AI and not a human, and mandating that platforms connect users to crisis services when distress signals are detected; the law takes effect January 1, 2027. New York's S-3008C has been in effect since November 2025, requiring active protocols to identify suicidal behaviour and refer users to crisis hotlines. Maine has banned therapy services via AI without a licensed professional, and in Tennessee platform developers can face criminal liability. In total five states — Washington, New York, Maine, Tennessee and Utah — are now early regulators, and another five to ten are expected to follow before the end of the year. For Swedish and Norwegian lawmakers watching the EU AI Act debate, the U.S. state-level explosion shows what happens when federal regulation stalls.

Source: RoboRhythms, JDSupra/BakerHostetler, Manatt, Governor Hochul

Models

OpenAI launches GPT-5.4-Cyber — a cybersecurity model for vetted security teams in its Trusted Access program

On April 14 OpenAI unveiled GPT-5.4-Cyber, a fine-tuned variant of GPT-5.4 for defensive cybersecurity. The model is "cyber-permissive" and handles tasks such as binary reverse engineering, vulnerability analysis and malware analysis without source code — capabilities the standard version constrains. At the same time OpenAI is expanding its Trusted Access for Cyber program to thousands of verified security professionals and hundreds of teams, with multiple trust tiers in which the highest unlocks GPT-5.4-Cyber. The launch comes a week after Anthropic's Mythos reveal and confirms that the U.S. frontier labs are now competing over the offense-defense utility asymmetry that Anthropic has flagged as critical. Unlike Anthropic — which keeps Mythos fully closed — OpenAI has chosen a verification-based approach: access is broader, but identity checks and logging are meant to keep misuse out. For Swedish and Norwegian SOC teams this means a new frontline of AI tools that requires both technical competence and governance routines to be used responsibly.

Source: SiliconANGLE, Bloomberg, Axios, 9to5Mac
Hardware

ASML raises 2026 full-year guidance — AI chip demand drives EUV orders to record levels

Dutch lithography giant ASML reported Q1 results above expectations on April 15 and raised its full-year 2026 guidance to €34–39 billion, roughly 12 per cent growth at the midpoint with gross margins held at 51–53 per cent. The company — the world's only maker of EUV lithography machines — points to continued strong demand from AI chip customers, with TSMC leading EUV purchases and Samsung closing in on a larger order of around twenty EUV systems for its P5 fab. SK Hynix is reported to be spending about $8 billion on EUV equipment in 2026 alone. ASML also raised its long-term target to €44–60 billion in annual revenue by 2030. The report — together with TSMC's record $35.6 billion revenue last week — confirms that the AI hardware cycle is accelerating and that the bottleneck in global semiconductor production sits with a handful of suppliers. For Europe's technological sovereignty debate, ASML's strength is both an advantage and a reminder of how concentrated the supply chain is.

Source: CNBC, ASML IR, 24/7 Wall St.
Regulation

EU trilogue on AI Act delays heads to April 28 decision — high-risk systems could slip to 2027 or 2028

The EU's trilogue negotiations on the Digital Omnibus on AI are approaching a decision point: the next political trilogue meeting is scheduled for April 28, with technical talks running in parallel. The Council adopted its position on March 13, Parliament's committees on March 18 and plenary confirmed it on March 26. If the proposal goes through, the obligations for standalone high-risk AI systems would move from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027, while some other mandatory rules could land as late as August 2028. The Commission also wants to extend SME exemptions to small mid-caps, strengthen the AI Office's powers and reduce governance fragmentation. Law firms warn, however, that until the text is formally adopted companies should plan against the original August 2, 2026 deadline. For Swedish and Norwegian firms this means running two parallel tracks — what IAPP and OneTrust describe as one of the year's toughest compliance calculations.

Source: European Parliament, IAPP, OneTrust, A&O Shearman

Debate

PwC: 74 per cent of AI's economic value is captured by just 20 per cent of companies

On April 13, PwC released a new AI Performance study showing that a small group of companies is pulling sharply ahead in the race to turn AI into financial results. Nearly three-quarters — 74 per cent — of AI's economic value is captured by 20 per cent of companies. Leaders generate 7.2 times more value than their competitors, enjoy operating margins 4 percentage points higher and invest 2.5 times more in AI — but above all they aim their efforts at growth and strategic priorities rather than cost cutting alone. The study, based on 1,217 senior executives across 25 sectors, also finds that the most successful companies are twice as likely to redesign workflows around AI rather than layering AI tools onto existing processes. For Swedish and Nordic firms — where BCG has previously reported that just 4 per cent achieve a fivefold AI return — the numbers sharpen the picture of an escalating gap between AI leaders and laggards.

Source: PwC, IT Pro, Digit.fyi
Hardware

TSMC reports record $35.6 billion in revenue — ASML and TSMC publish Q1 results this week

Taiwan's TSMC reported preliminary Q1 revenue of 1.13 trillion New Taiwan dollars (around $35.6 billion) on April 10, a 35 per cent year-over-year increase and above analyst expectations. The company also raised prices on its most advanced processes, driven by demand from Apple and Nvidia. On April 15 and 16 ASML and TSMC publish their full Q1 results — two numbers the market reads as the clearest thermometer of the AI hardware cycle. TSMC maintains its 2026 capex guidance at $52–56 billion, with 70–80 per cent allocated to advanced process technology, and forecasts that AI data-center chip revenue will grow at a 50–60 per cent CAGR through 2029. ASML is the only manufacturer of the EUV lithography machines required for these chips. Together with Amazon's $200 billion plan and the CoreWeave-Meta deal, the figures confirm that AI hardware remains the dominant global investment category — and how concentrated and fragile the supply chain is.

Source: CNBC, European Business Review, IndexBox

Debate

"Vulnpocalypse" — security experts warn AI is already tipping the scales in favour of hackers

In the wake of Anthropic's Mythos Preview revelation, the debate over AI-driven cyber offence is escalating. Fortune reported on April 10 that experts see Mythos as a wake-up call but stress that the era of AI-powered hacking has already begun. NBC News coined the term "Vulnpocalypse" — a future where AI finds and chains vulnerabilities faster than defenders can patch. Anthropic's own offensive cyber research lead Logan Graham expects competitors, including Chinese actors, to deliver comparable capability within 6–12 months. Christian Science Monitor described on April 11 how Mythos signals "a new era for cyber risks" and noted that US Treasury Secretary Bessent has already convened major banks to discuss the implications. Taken together, the reactions show that defensive AI arms racing is now a geopolitical reality — not a theoretical risk.

Source: Fortune, NBC News, CS Monitor, Platformer
Regulation

Over 600 AI bills in US states — Nebraska, Maryland and Maine pass new laws

According to an update from Troutman Pepper on April 13, state legislators in the US have introduced over 600 AI-related bills so far in 2026. In the past week, Nebraska passed a chatbot law, Maryland regulated AI-driven pricing and Maine banned offering therapy services via AI without a licensed professional. Meanwhile, Indiana, Utah and Washington have introduced rules preventing health insurers from using AI as the sole basis for denying claims. Colorado is preparing to replace its 2024 AI Act with tighter rules for automated decision-making systems. The explosion of state-level regulation is creating a patchwork that forces AI companies to navigate hundreds of jurisdictions — while simultaneously pressuring Congress to deliver a unified federal framework.

Source: Troutman Pepper, NBC News, Drata
Debate

BCG: Only 4 per cent of Nordic companies see returns on AI — risk of a local "value bubble"

Boston Consulting Group's report "The Nordic AI Inflection Point", published in March 2026, shows that just 4 per cent of Nordic companies report a return of at least five times their AI investment — on par with global and EU competitors despite the Nordics treating AI as a top priority. The problem: Nordic companies allocate 40–50 per cent of their AI budget to off-the-shelf productivity tools compared with 8–11 per cent among global competitors, who instead invest in transformative end-to-end initiatives. Despite the low results, Nordic executives expect AI to drive 30 per cent revenue growth and 25 per cent cost reduction by 2029 — expectations 2–3 times higher than global benchmarks. BCG warns that if the ROI gap persists, Nordic economies risk a local AI value bubble and could lose significant ground to international competitors.

Source: BCG, BCG (PDF)

Models

Anthropic launches Claude Managed Agents — fully hosted infrastructure for autonomous AI agents via API

On April 8, Anthropic opened a public beta of Claude Managed Agents, an API suite that lets developers build and run autonomous AI agents in the cloud without managing their own infrastructure for sandboxing, state management or tool execution. The service costs $0.08 per session hour on top of standard token prices and supports two architectures: Agent Teams, where multiple Claude instances with independent contexts communicate directly and share task lists, and Subagents that operate within the main agent's session and only report results. Notion, Rakuten and Sentry are already in production — Notion for workspace delegation, Rakuten for enterprise agents in Slack and Sentry for automated debugging. The launch marks a shift from "model as a service" to "agent as a service" and puts pressure on competitors to offer similar managed agent platforms.

Source: SiliconANGLE, The Decoder, 9to5Mac, Help Net Security
Infrastructure

CoreWeave and Meta expand AI cloud deal to $21 billion — $35 billion total through 2032

CoreWeave announced on April 9 that it has expanded its long-term agreement with Meta Platforms to $21 billion in new AI cloud capacity to be delivered between 2027 and 2032. Combined with the previous $14.2 billion deal, Meta's total commitment reaches $35 billion, making Meta one of CoreWeave's largest customers. The new capacity will be deployed from multiple data centres and include early installations of the NVIDIA Vera Rubin platform, designed to optimise performance and scalability for Meta's AI operations. CoreWeave shares rose 3.5 per cent on the news. The deal underscores that AI infrastructure investments are now measured in decades rather than years — and that frontier laboratories are signing multi-year chip and energy contracts at a scale without historical precedent.

Source: Bloomberg, CNBC, CoreWeave IR, The Next Web
Infrastructure

Amazon commits $200 billion to AI infrastructure in 2026 — AWS AI revenue hits $15 billion run rate

In his annual shareholder letter published on April 9, Amazon CEO Andy Jassy revealed that AWS AI services now generate a revenue run rate of $15 billion — roughly 10 per cent of AWS's total revenue and a figure the company has not previously reported separately. Jassy defended Amazon's planned capital expenditure of approximately $200 billion for 2026, up from $50.7 billion the previous year, which pushed free cash flow from $38 billion to $11 billion. He also disclosed that Amazon's internal chip business generates more than $20 billion annually and that the company may sell AI chips externally — a potential threat to Nvidia and AMD. Amazon shares rose 5.6 per cent following the letter. Together with the CoreWeave-Meta deal, the figures show that AI infrastructure is now the single largest investment category in global technology.

Source: GeekWire, Sherwood News, Quartz, 24/7 Wall St.

About AI-skiftet

AI-skiftet is an independent essay project on how AI is changing work, industry, politics and society.

The perspective is deliberately industry-near. The texts do not primarily come from seminar rooms or general trend-spotting, but from work in sharp production environments, improvement projects and conversations about what the technology is actually changing when quality, economy and responsibility matter.

The ambition is twofold: to raise the level of the Swedish AI debate and to make the arguments more testable. Therefore the texts distinguish as far as possible between observation, inference, scenario and normative proposal, and each essay has its own source notes.

Rolf Skogling — mechanical engineer and consultant in industrial optimisation. Works with AI in sharp operations within Nordic and European manufacturing industry.
