The Missing Consistency Condition

Two letters in the same week, from the same economic logic. Both presuppose an economy that their own products are dismantling.

Essay · Rolf Skogling · Published May 28, 2026 · about 10 min read

Two letters in the same week — Altman and Amodei walked back their jobs forecasts ahead of their IPOs, eight Swedish tech founders asked the government for long-term policy on entrepreneurship and AI. Both presuppose an economy that their own products are dismantling. This is not a political objection. It is a structural contradiction.

On May 26, Sam Altman said he had been "pretty wrong" about AI quickly wiping out entry-level jobs. Dario Amodei at the same time walked back his earlier warning that the technology could eliminate half of all office jobs in the United States. A single weekend was enough for one of the hottest forecasts in the AI debate to cool noticeably.

The background is no secret. OpenAI and Anthropic are preparing capital raises at valuations near a trillion dollars each. Ahead of such an issue it is rational for a CEO to adopt a softer tone toward politicians, unions and pension fund managers. Amodei now argues instead that if 90 percent of a job is automated, the remaining 10 percent expands — a productivity thesis that echoes McKinsey and Goldman Sachs, but that runs directly against Anthropic's own warnings from last year.

Two days later, on May 28, eight Swedish tech founders published an opinion piece in Dagens Industri (Sweden's main business daily). They sign on to a cross-bloc long-term plan for entrepreneurship and AI. They want talent visas, employee stock options without restrictions, and broad political consensus. They list Lovable, Legora, Sana and Tandem as heirs to Ericsson, Spotify and Klarna. Tech is already 8 percent of Sweden's GDP, they write. Tech companies will create new jobs, build tomorrow's export industries, drive productivity and finance the welfare state of the future.

These are two letters in the same week, springing from the same economic logic. Both say essentially the same thing: trust the process, AI creates more than it destroys, the market handles distribution.

It is possible they are right. It is worth asking whether they can be.

The first contradiction: the job creation is symbolic

The simplest way to check a claim about job creation is to count the jobs.

The Swedish tech companies in the founders' letter employ few people. Spotify globally had, according to its 2025 annual report, an average of just over 7,000 employees. Klarna has gone from just over 5,500 employees in 2022 to about 3,400 at the end of 2024, and was reported in 2025 to be under 3,000. Lovable, Legora, Sana, Tandem, Lassie and Kognic are probably each in the range of 30–200 employees. Together they make up a Swedish workforce in the tens of thousands, of which a fraction works in Sweden.

Volvo Cars alone employs just over 20,000 people in Sweden after its most recent round of layoffs (down from around 23,000 before the cuts). Forestry and the forest products industry together employ just over 80,000. Retail, around 250,000.

This does not mean tech companies are unimportant — they can be strategically important without being large employers. But it means that the statement "creates new jobs" is a rhetorical promise, not a quantifiable forecast. If Sweden over the coming decade manages to produce ten Lovables, five Sanas and three Legoras, that creates at best 20,000–50,000 highly qualified positions, optimistically counted.

How does that compare to the white-collar jobs that the same AI products can automate or substantially reduce the need for? That question is not asked in the opinion piece. It is also not asked by Altman or Amodei.

In the United States, the outplacement firm Challenger, Gray & Christmas counted 27,645 AI-cited layoffs in the first quarter of 2026 — around 13 percent of the quarter's job cuts. By the end of April that number had risen to 49,135, equivalent to about 16 percent of layoffs through April. At the same time, tech-sector layoffs were running 40 percent higher in the first quarter than the same period the year before.

Stanford Digital Economy Lab has in turn shown that young workers, ages 22–25, in the most AI-exposed occupations have seen a 16-percent relative decline in employment since broad genAI adoption, while more experienced workers in the same occupations have fared significantly better. This is not a final verdict on the entire labor market. But it is an early signal that the entry into certain knowledge professions is already narrowing.

The economic research in this area also does not support the tech founders' line. Daron Acemoglu, the MIT economist awarded the 2024 Nobel Prize in economics, writes together with Simon Johnson — formerly chief economist at the IMF — in the journal Finance & Development (December 2023) that "the drive toward automation is perilous". There is no guarantee that AI on its current path creates more jobs than it destroys. The productivity bandwagon that tech investors invoke — the assumption that productivity gains automatically become broader prosperity — requires, according to their analysis, specific conditions that are not met when automation technology dominates the direction of development.

The first contradiction in the founders' model is therefore empirical. Not ideological: empirical. When the companies that are supposed to "create jobs" are examined numerically, and when the economic literature on the area is examined, they turn out instead to redistribute the value — upward, toward capital, and away from broad employment.

The second contradiction: agents don't need interfaces

This is the underrated one — it is rarely discussed in the Swedish tech debate.

The SaaS industry — several hundred billion dollars globally — is built on a specific assumption: that humans need interfaces to interact with computers. Dashboards to monitor. Workflows to coordinate work. Approval chains to govern. Reports to communicate. Forms to register. The entire economy around enterprise software assumes that the end user is a human who clicks, writes and approves.

Take the human out of the loop. Replace her with an agent that has direct access via APIs and new agent protocols such as MCP (Model Context Protocol — a standard Anthropic introduced in late 2024, which lets AI agents discover and use external tools without pre-built integration), and that has semantic understanding of the goal and the ability to make decisions autonomously.

What is needed then? Not a dashboard — the agent monitors directly on the data and raises an alert when it deviates. Not a workflow — the agent coordinates itself with other agents. Not reports — the agent communicates semantically in the format needed in the moment. Not forms — the agent gathers what is needed where it lives.

The result is that a large portion of the existing enterprise software market is transitional technology. Built for a brief era when computers were fast but the interface was still human. When the interface disappears, the rationale for many of the companies disappears with it.

That does not mean all software dies. What is likely to survive is the infrastructure layer (compute, security, data, observability), tools for building and operating agents, physical-world interfaces (sensors, control, robotics) and what is regulated into human-in-the-loop (healthcare, law, security).

The rest — the broad office-software economy and the millions of administrative positions that make up its user base — shrinks or transforms. Several of Sweden's strongest tech companies sit on the wrong side of that line. They are built for a world with many knowledge workers who need help. In an agentic world they become either platforms for other agents (which is an entirely different business) or obsolete.

Part of what disappears is not just dashboards and forms. It is the whole integration economy — Zapier, n8n, custom connectors between business systems, integration consultants — that exists because humans and their vendors could not get systems to talk to each other without translation. When agents can discover and combine capabilities directly, an intermediate layer that today turns over billions globally disappears.

This is not a political claim. It is an architectural observation. The question the founders need to answer is: which of your companies has a business model that survives when agents have taken over the interaction layer?

The third contradiction: who buys?

The question has been asked since Henry Ford. The myth that Ford raised wages so his workers could afford to buy his cars is historically simplified, but the point holds: capitalism requires consumers, consumers require income, and income still comes mostly from work.

KPMG announced in May 2026 a global alliance with Anthropic and is beginning implementation of Claude for its more than 276,000 employees globally — initially focused on tax and legal services. Standard Chartered, SEB and several major banks have announced heavy AI savings. KPMG, EY, Deloitte and PwC have thousands of employees in the Nordics alone in audit, tax and advisory — precisely the time-consuming work that agents are now being aimed at. Klarna's Sebastian Siemiatkowski said openly back in 2024 that AI lets the company do more with fewer.

What happens to purchasing power when the broad knowledge-worker class shrinks?

The standard counter is: "capital owners consume too." That is true. But they consume differently. The economy that follows does not look like today's, but like something clearly bifurcated:

  • A thin top layer with massive purchasing power directed at luxury, healthcare, real estate, experiences and status.
  • A broad bottom layer on subsistence economics — basic needs.
  • The broad middle-class consumption — which the entire SaaS and consumer-tech model rests on — erodes.

This is not hypothetical. It is what has happened in the United States over the past thirty years, only more slowly. AI compresses the same development from decades into years.

What does Klarna's founder do when the consumer no longer has the income to finance purchases with? What does Spotify do when music's purchasing-power base — the knowledge workers in the service sector — shrinks? What does Sana do when the customers are AI agents rather than HR managers?

And what does Sweden do when tax revenue from labor falls, because the "social security system that has enabled risk-taking" — which the founders rightly praise — was historically financed by precisely broad employment and the taxation of work?

That question is not answered in the founders' letter. It is also not present in Altman's new optimistic tone. It is a structural question on which the entire economic model rests, and which both camps elegantly bypass.

The serious counter: Jevons paradox

The strongest counter-argument against the reasoning so far is Jevons paradox: when a resource becomes more efficient, total consumption tends to increase, not decrease. As the cost of cognitive work falls toward zero, demand can explode. We start applying AI to problems that previously were too expensive to solve — hyper-personalized diagnostics, continuous legal review, real-time analysis of every municipal budget, individualized education for every pupil. According to this reasoning, AI creates an explosion of demand that absorbs the freed labor in new roles we don't yet have vocabulary for. It is the same logic Amodei now leans on when he says that if 90 percent of a task is automated, the remaining 10 percent expands.

The argument deserves to be taken seriously. It has historical grounding — every technological wave since industrialization has followed the pattern.

Korinek himself addresses Jevons paradox on the VoxDev podcast and arrives at the same conclusion that follows here. Even if the new demand absorbs part of the labor force, the transition remains deeply disruptive, because workers are forced into the increasingly narrow roles where human input is still required. He warns of a sharp devaluation of cognitive work — exactly the kind of work that today carries Nordic middle-class consumption.

There are two specific reasons to be cautious about transferring the historical Jevons pattern straight onto the AI wave.

The first is architectural. In earlier technological waves, the new demand expanded within the human domain — more cars required more mechanics, more computers required more IT consultants, more websites required more web developers. The expansion Amodei describes — "the remaining 10 percent grows" — assumes that those 10 percent are reserved for humans. There is no structural reason that they would be. If agents can do 90 percent of a task today, there is no architectural obstacle to them doing the expanding rest tomorrow. The technology that opens the demand is the same technology that can absorb it.

The second is policy-practical. Even if Jevons holds — even if new jobs appear in sectors we cannot yet name — it does not solve the consistency problem. Policy still needs to know where the new jobs arise, when they arise, what competencies they require, how the transition is financed and how the tax base is preserved meanwhile. A future productivity lift is not a labor-market plan. It is an assumption.

The point here is not to forecast exact numbers of eliminated jobs. It is to show that the founders' model lacks a measurement and distribution mechanism for the scenario they themselves make technically possible.

A consistency analysis, not a political objection

It would be wrong to read this as an attack on the tech founders. The Swedish signatories have a perfectly valid point about talent visas and employee stock options. Sweden competes for talent globally. Employee stock options are genuinely worse constructed here than in London, New York and Berlin. Cross-bloc long-termism on business conditions is a reasonable political wish.

Their strongest argument is not even what they write — it is what they implicitly assume. The AI transition is happening regardless of what Sweden does. In that case the choice is not between "no disruption" and "disruption plus Swedish companies", but between "disruption plus we capture some of the value" and "disruption plus we capture none". From that perspective, talent visas and employee stock options are reasonable measures.

But that is a different argument from "tech creates more jobs than it destroys". That sentence lacks boundary conditions and allows its authors to avoid the follow-up: how do you combine "we build AI companies that should do more with fewer" with "we need broad employment that finances the welfare state"?

It is the same contradiction Altman and Amodei are now trying to tone down ahead of their IPOs. The same week. Not because they coordinate — but because the economic logic in all directions pulls toward soothing messages aimed at politicians and investors.

That is where the serious political discussion needs to land. Not the counter-argument "you are wrong" — but the additional question "and what is the plan for what you are not addressing?"

What the government actually needs

A cross-bloc long-term plan for entrepreneurship and AI is reasonable. Such a plan without parallel planning for the broad labor-market shift the AI companies themselves are triggering is, however, not long-term — it is wishful thinking.

What needs to be included, in addition to talent visas and employee stock options:

  • A continuous national measurement of AI-related labor-market impact, sector by sector, with the same transparency as the monthly unemployment figures from Statistics Sweden (SCB). Today no such measurement exists in Sweden.
  • A broadened transition support system — the current model run by Trygghetsrådet and Trygghetsstiftelsen (Sweden's job-security councils for private-sector employees) is dimensioned for cyclical unemployment, not structural sector change.
  • A serious inquiry into distribution mechanisms for the case in which the employment rate falls structurally: working-time reductions, taxation of AI-driven productivity, citizen's income in some form. Not because the answer is clear, but because the question will come anyway, and Sweden needs to have investigated its options before the crisis forces them.
  • Activation of the social-partner model. The Saltsjöbaden Agreement of 1938 (the foundational accord between Swedish labor and employers) resolved a structural crisis no one thought was solvable. We do in fact have institutions built for large transitions. The question is when someone politically dares to activate them.

That the question is no longer extreme can be seen from who is asking it. Anton Korinek — economist at the University of Virginia, senior fellow at Brookings and PIIE, and member of Anthropic's own Economic Advisory Council — has said (Vox, Explain It to Me, December 2025) that when the AI revolution really hits there is "no guarantee that we can earn a decent living based on labor", and that we will then need a new system for income distribution. When one of Anthropic's own economic advisers says that — at a time when the company is seeking capital at valuations near a trillion dollars — it is no longer a marginal warning from the outside.

This is not a left-wing program. It is risk management. And it is exactly what the tech founders' letter — and Altman's and Amodei's new optimism — lacks.

Finally

The week eight Swedish tech founders asked for long-termism, two of the world's leading AI CEOs walked back their warnings. Not because they had changed their minds — but because capital-market logic rewards a calmer message ahead of an IPO. This is not a problem with their character. It is a problem with the information we, who are supposed to plan Sweden's economy, get from them.

If we build Sweden's AI policy on forecasts that get reversed on demand, we are building on unstable ground. The long-termism the founders ask for presupposes that we take their own forecasts seriously — including the forecasts they are right now walking back.

This is not a yes or a no to their agenda. It is a demand that it pass the consistency condition: your proposed measures and your descriptions of the future must hang together. When they do not, we cannot plan a country on it.
