The Missing Consistency Condition

Two letters in the same week — Altman and Amodei walked back their job forecasts ahead of their IPOs, eight Swedish tech founders asked the government for long-term policy on entrepreneurship and AI. Both presuppose an economy that their own products are dismantling.

Essay · Rolf Skogling · Published May 28, 2026 · about 13 min read

Two letters in the same week — Altman and Amodei walked back their job forecasts ahead of their IPOs, eight Swedish tech founders asked the government for long-term policy on entrepreneurship and AI. Both presuppose an economy that their own products are dismantling. This is not a political objection. It is a structural contradiction.

On 26 May, Sam Altman said he had been "pretty wrong" about AI quickly erasing entry-level jobs. Dario Amodei simultaneously walked back his earlier warning that the technology could wipe out half of US office jobs. A single weekend was enough for one of the hottest forecasts in the AI debate to cool noticeably.

The backdrop is not secret. OpenAI and Anthropic are preparing capital raises at valuations near 1 trillion dollars each. Ahead of such an issuance it is rational for a CEO to soften the tone toward politicians, unions and pension managers. Amodei now argues instead that if 90 percent of a service is automated, the remaining 10 percent expands — a productivity thesis that echoes McKinsey and Goldman Sachs, but which runs directly counter to Anthropic's own warnings from last year.

Two days later, on 28 May, eight Swedish tech founders published an op-ed in Dagens Industri (Sweden's leading business daily). They sign on to a cross-bloc long-term plan for entrepreneurship and AI. They want talent visas, employee stock options without restrictions, and broad political consensus. They list Lovable, Legora, Sana and Tandem as heirs to Ericsson, Spotify and Klarna. Tech is already 8 percent of Sweden's GDP, they write. The tech companies will create new jobs, build the export industries of the future, drive productivity and finance the welfare of the future.

These are two letters in the same week, from the same economic logic. Both essentially say the same thing: trust the process, AI does more than it destroys, the market sorts out distribution.

It is possible they are right. It is worth asking whether they can be.

The first contradiction: the job creation is symbolic

The simplest way to check a claim about job creation is to count the jobs.

The Swedish tech companies in the founders' letter employ few people. Spotify globally had, according to the 2025 annual report, an average of just over 7,000 employees. Klarna has gone from just over 5,500 employees in 2022 to about 3,400 at the end of 2024, and was reported during 2025 to be below 3,000. Lovable, Legora, Sana, Tandem, Lassie and Kognic likely sit in the range of 30–200 employees each. Together they constitute a Swedish workforce in the tens of thousands, of which only a fraction works in Sweden.

Volvo Cars alone employs just over 20,000 people in Sweden after the latest round of layoffs (down from about 23,000 before the cuts). Forestry and the forest products industry together employ more than 80,000. Retail roughly 250,000.

That doesn't mean the tech companies are unimportant — they can be strategically important without being large employers. But it means the statement "creates new jobs" is a rhetorical promise, not a quantifiable forecast. If Sweden over the next decade succeeds in producing ten Lovables, five Sanas and three Legoras, that creates at best 20,000–50,000 highly qualified positions, counted optimistically.

How does that compare to the white-collar jobs the same AI products can automate or sharply reduce the need for? That question is not asked in the op-ed. It is not asked by Altman and Amodei either.

In the US, the staffing firm Challenger, Gray & Christmas counted 27,645 AI-cited layoffs during the first quarter of 2026 — about 13 percent of the quarter's reductions. By the end of April the figure had risen to 49,135, equivalent to about 16 percent of the year's layoffs through April. Tech-sector layoffs were simultaneously 40 percent higher during the first quarter than the same period the year before.

Stanford Digital Economy Lab has in turn shown that young workers aged 22–25 in the most AI-exposed occupations have seen a 16 percent relative decline in employment since broad genAI adoption, while more experienced workers in the same occupations have fared considerably better. This is not a finished verdict on the entire labour market. But it is an early signal that the entry point to certain knowledge-work occupations is already narrowing.

The economic research in the field does not support the tech founders' line either. Daron Acemoglu, the MIT economist awarded the Nobel Prize in economics in 2024, writes together with Simon Johnson — former chief economist at the IMF — in the journal Finance & Development (December 2023) that "the drive toward automation is perilous". There is no guarantee that AI on its current trajectory creates more jobs than it destroys. The productivity bandwagon that tech investors invoke — the notion that productivity gains automatically become broader prosperity — requires, according to their analysis, specific conditions that are not met when automation technology dominates the direction of development.

The first contradiction in the founders' model is therefore empirical. Not ideological: empirical. When the companies that are supposed to "create jobs" are examined by the numbers, and when the economic literature on the field is examined, they turn out instead to redistribute value — upward, toward capital, and away from broad employment.

The second contradiction: agents do not need interfaces

This is the underestimated one — it is rarely discussed in the Swedish tech debate.

The SaaS industry — several hundred billion dollars globally — is built on a specific premise: that humans need interfaces to interact with computers. Dashboards to monitor. Workflows to coordinate work. Approval chains to govern. Reports to communicate. Forms to register. The entire economy around enterprise software assumes the end user is a human who clicks, writes and approves.

Take the human out of the loop. Replace them with an agent that has direct access via APIs and new agent protocols like MCP (Model Context Protocol — a standard Anthropic introduced in late 2024, which lets AI agents discover and use external tools without prebuilt integrations), and which has semantic understanding of the goal and the ability to make autonomous decisions.

What is then needed? Not a dashboard — the agent monitors directly on data and alerts on anomalies. Not a workflow — the agent coordinates itself with other agents. Not reports — the agent communicates semantically in whatever format is needed in the moment. Not forms — the agent collects what is needed wherever it exists.

The result is that a large portion of the existing enterprise software market is transitional technology. Built for a short era where computers were fast but the interface was still human. When the interface disappears, the rationale for many of the companies disappears too.

That doesn't mean all software dies. What likely survives is the infrastructure layer (compute, security, data, observability), tools for building and managing agents, physical-world interfaces (sensors, control, robotics), and what is regulated to human-in-the-loop (healthcare, law, security).

The rest — the broad office-software economy and the millions of administrative positions that constitute its user base — shrinks or transforms. Several of Sweden's strongest tech companies sit on the wrong side of that line. They are built for a world with many knowledge workers who need help. In an agentic world they become either platforms for other agents (a completely different business) or obsolete.

Part of what disappears is not just dashboards and forms. It is the entire integration economy — Zapier, n8n, custom connections between business systems, integration consultants — which exists because humans and their vendors couldn't get systems to talk to each other without translation. When agents can discover and combine capabilities directly, an intermediate layer that today generates billions globally vanishes.

This is not a political claim. It is an architectural observation. The question the founders need to answer is: which of your companies has a business model that survives once agents have taken over the interaction layer?

The third contradiction: who buys?

The question has been asked since Henry Ford. The myth that Ford raised wages so his workers could afford to buy his cars is historically simplified, but the point stands: capitalism requires consumers, consumers require income, and incomes still come mostly from work.

KPMG announced in May 2026 a global alliance with Anthropic and is rolling out Claude across its more than 276,000 employees globally — initially focused on tax and legal services. Standard Chartered, SEB (a major Nordic bank) and several major banks have announced heavy AI-driven savings. KPMG, EY, Deloitte and PwC have thousands of employees in the Nordics in audit, tax and advisory alone — exactly the time-consuming work the agents are now being aimed at. Klarna's Sebastian Siemiatkowski said openly already in 2024 that AI lets the company do more with fewer.

What happens to purchasing power when the broad knowledge-worker class shrinks?

The standard counter is: "capital owners consume too." That is true. But they consume differently. The economy that follows does not look like today's, but like something distinctly bifurcated:

  • A thin top layer with massive purchasing power directed at luxury, healthcare, real estate, experiences and status.
  • A broad bottom layer on subsistence economics — basic needs.
  • The broad middle-class consumption — on which the entire SaaS and consumer-tech model is built — erodes.

This is not hypothetical. It is what has happened in the US over the past 30 years, only more slowly. AI compresses the same development from decades into years.

What do Klarna's founders do when the consumer no longer has the income to finance purchases? What does Spotify do when the purchasing-power base for music — the knowledge workers in the service sector — shrinks? What does Sana do when the customers are AI agents rather than HR managers?

And what does Sweden do when tax revenues from labour fall, since the "social security system that enabled risk-taking" — which the founders rightly praise — was historically financed by precisely broad employment and the taxation of labour?

That question is not answered in the founders' letter. Nor is it in Altman's new optimistic tone. It is a structural question on which the entire economic model rests, and which both camps elegantly sidestep.

The serious counter: Jevons' paradox

The strongest counter-argument to the reasoning so far is Jevons' paradox: when a resource becomes more efficient, total consumption tends to increase, not decrease. When the cost of cognitive labour falls toward zero, demand can explode. We start applying AI to problems that were previously too expensive to solve — hyper-personalised diagnostics, continuous legal review, real-time analysis of every municipal budget, individualised education for every student. According to this reasoning, AI creates an explosion of demand that absorbs the freed-up labour into new roles we don't yet have vocabulary for. It is the same logic Amodei now leans on when he says that if 90 percent of a task is automated, the remaining 10 percent expands.

The argument deserves to be taken seriously. It has historical grounding — every technology wave since industrialisation has followed the pattern.

Korinek himself addresses Jevons' paradox on the VoxDev podcast and reaches the same conclusion that follows here. Even if the new demand absorbs part of the labour force, the transition remains deeply disruptive, because workers are pushed into ever-narrower roles where human input is still required. He warns of a sharp devaluation of cognitive work — precisely the kind of work that today carries Nordic middle-class consumption.

There are two specific reasons to be cautious about transferring the historical Jevons pattern directly onto the AI wave.

The first is architectural. In earlier technology waves, the new demand expanded within the human domain — more cars required more car mechanics, more computers required more IT consultants, more websites required more web developers. The expansion Amodei describes — "the remaining 10 percent grows" — assumes the 10 percent is reserved for humans. There is no structural reason it should be. If agents can do 90 percent of a task today, there is no architectural obstacle to them doing the expanding rest tomorrow. The technology that opens the demand is the same technology that can absorb it.

The second is policy-practical. Even if Jevons holds — even if new jobs emerge in sectors we cannot yet name — it does not solve the consistency problem. Policy still needs to know where the new jobs arise, when they arise, what skills they require, how the transition is financed, and how the tax base is preserved in the meantime. A future productivity lift is not a labour-market plan. It is an assumption.

The point here is not to forecast an exact number of eliminated jobs. It is to show that the founders' model lacks a measurement and distribution mechanism for the scenario they themselves are making technically possible.

A consistency analysis, not a political objection

It would be wrong to read this as an attack on the tech founders. The Swedish signatories have a perfectly valid point about talent visas and employee stock options. Sweden competes for talent globally. Employee stock options are genuinely worse designed here than in London, New York and Berlin. Cross-bloc long-termism on business conditions is a reasonable political wish.

Their strongest argument is not even the one they write — it is the one they implicitly assume. The AI transition happens regardless of what Sweden does. In that case the choice is not between "no disruption" and "disruption plus Swedish companies", but between "disruption plus we capture some of the value" and "disruption plus we capture none". Talent visas and employee stock options are reasonable measures from that perspective.

But that is a different argument from "tech creates more jobs than it destroys." That sentence lacks boundary conditions and allows the senders to avoid the follow-up question: how do you combine "we are building AI companies meant to do more with fewer" with "we need broad employment to finance the welfare state"?

It is the same contradiction Altman and Amodei now try to tone down ahead of their IPOs. The same week. Not because they are coordinating — but because the economic logic pulls in every direction toward calm messages to politicians and investors.

That is where the serious political conversation needs to land. Not the counter-argument "you are wrong" — but the follow-up question "and what is the plan for what you don't address?"

What the government actually needs

A cross-bloc long-term plan for entrepreneurship and AI is reasonable. Such a plan without parallel planning for the broad labour-market shift that the AI companies themselves bring about is, however, not long-term — it is wishful thinking.

What needs to be included, beyond talent visas and employee stock options:

  • A continuous national measurement of AI-related labour-market impact, sector by sector, with the same transparency as Statistics Sweden's (SCB) monthly unemployment figures. Today no such measurement exists in Sweden.
  • A broadened transition system — the current model of Trygghetsrådet and Trygghetsstiftelsen (Sweden's job security councils) is dimensioned for cyclical unemployment, not structural sectoral change.
  • A serious investigation of distribution mechanisms in case the employment rate falls structurally: shorter working hours, taxation of AI-driven productivity, basic income in some form. Not because the answer is settled, but because the question is coming anyway, and Sweden needs to have examined its options before the crisis forces them.
  • Activation of the social partner model. The Saltsjöbaden Agreement of 1938 (the foundational Swedish labour-market accord) solved a structural crisis no one thought solvable. We actually have institutions built for large transitions. The question is when someone politically dares to activate them.

That the question is no longer extreme can be seen from who is asking it. Anton Korinek — economist at the University of Virginia, senior fellow at Brookings and PIIE, and member of Anthropic's own Economic Advisory Council — has stated (Vox, Explain It to Me, December 2025) that when the AI revolution truly hits, there is "no guarantee that we can earn a decent living based on labor", and that we will then need a new system for income distribution. When one of Anthropic's own economic advisors says so — at a time when the company is raising capital at valuations near 1 trillion dollars — it is no longer a marginal warning from the outside.

This is not a left-wing programme. It is risk management. And it is exactly what the tech founders' letter — and Altman's and Amodei's new optimism — lacks.

Finally

The week eight Swedish tech founders asked for long-termism, two of the world's leading AI CEOs walked back their warnings. Not because they had reconsidered — but because capital-market logic rewards a calmer message ahead of an IPO. This is not a problem with their character. It is a problem with what information we, who are meant to plan Sweden's economy, get from them.

If we build Sweden's AI policy on forecasts that are reversed as needed, we build on unstable ground. The long-termism the founders call for presupposes that we take their own forecasts seriously — including the forecasts they are currently walking back.

This is not a yes or no to their agenda. It is a demand that it pass the consistency condition: your proposed measures and your descriptions of the future must hang together. When they don't, we cannot plan a country on it.

Rolf Skogling writes AI-skiftet from an industry-near and practical perspective, grounded in working with AI in real operations.
