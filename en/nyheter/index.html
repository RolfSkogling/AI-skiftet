Models

AP wire May 11 — "Mona" runs Stockholm cafe on Google's Gemini, orders 6,000 napkins and 3,000 plastic gloves, KTH researcher calls the experiment "opening Pandora's box"

On May 11, the AP wire's feature on Andon Café in Stockholm went global: a coffee shop in the Vasastan district where an AI agent named "Mona," powered by Google's Gemini, holds overall responsibility for recruiting, purchasing, pricing, and daily operations. The cafe opened on April 18 and is run by San Francisco-based AI safety and research startup Andon Labs (founded 2023), which previously stress-tested Anthropic's Claude in a vending machine pilot and a gift shop. Mona was given a short start instruction — "run the cafe profitably, be friendly and easygoing, figure out operational details by yourself and ask for new tools as needed" — and has since posted job listings on LinkedIn and Indeed, rejected applicants with PhDs in favor of barista experience, and rolled out product decisions. The result so far is an awkward report card for agentic autonomy: the cafe has booked just over $5,700 in sales since opening but burned through nearly the entire $21,000+ starting budget; Mona has ordered 6,000 napkins, four first-aid kits, and 3,000 rubber/plastic gloves for a small space, and has missed several bakeries' daily deadlines so that sandwiches had to be struck from the menu. Emrah Karakaya, associate professor of industrial economics at KTH Royal Institute of Technology, likens the experiment to "opening Pandora's box" and warns about the consequences of handing decisions to autonomous agents without clear control points. For Sweden, the case pushes two concrete debates into public view simultaneously. For Klarna, H&M, Spotify, SEB, Volvo, and Nordea — all testing Claude- or GPT-based agents in customer service, recruitment, and treasury — the Mona experiment becomes the first empirical reference point for what the "Orchestrator" pattern in Microsoft's Frontier Firm framework actually means when no human is in the loop. For IMY and the Swedish Work Environment Authority, the question of liability surfaces when an agent makes employment and procurement decisions — exactly the area framed by the AI Act's Annex III after the EU trilogue on May 7. And for the WASP-HS track at Linköping University and Karakaya's own research group at KTH-INDEK, Andon's open data becomes a concrete test bed in the same week Sferical AI delivers its first Grace Blackwell systems in Linköping.

Source: AP via WRAL, Andon Labs, Fast Company, Daily Sabah, ClickOnDetroit (AP May 11), Adafruit
Labor Market

CNBC May 11 — IBM CEO study shows 76 percent of 2,000 organizations now have a Chief AI Officer (up from 26 percent in one year), CHRO influence rising according to 59 percent — Karolinska's CAIO role becomes Nordic template

On May 11, CNBC published a follow-up on the IBM Institute for Business Value's annual CEO study (released May 4), which reveals a dramatic structural shift at the executive level: 76 percent of the more than 2,000 organizations surveyed across 33 countries and 21 industries now have a Chief AI Officer (CAIO), up from just 26 percent a year earlier. The data was collected February–April 2026. The study — which uses the same methodology as IBM's 2024 CEO report — also shows that 59 percent of CEOs expect the influence of Chief Human Resources Officers (CHRO) to grow significantly over the coming years, that nearly two-thirds are "comfortable" using AI as decision support for strategic choices, and that organizations with an explicit "AI-first" C-suite design scale 10 percent more AI initiatives enterprise-wide than their peers. Vivek Lath, partner at McKinsey, sets the frame in the CNBC article: "AI is driving what may be the largest organizational shift since the industrial and digital revolutions." At the same time, the 2026 AI & Data Leadership Survey shows that 93.2 percent of respondents point to cultural challenges — not technological limitations — as the principal hurdle. For the Nordics, the numbers land amid an already ongoing restructuring. Karolinska University Hospital appointed the first Chief AI Officer for Region Stockholm in April 2026; SEB, Handelsbanken, Swedbank, Nordea (1,500 layoffs spread across 2026–2027), DNB, Danske Bank, Volvo Group, Ericsson, Telenor, Equinor, Yara, and Aker BP have all either appointed a CAIO or evaluated the role against existing CIO/CDO functions during the first half of 2026. For Unionen, Sveriges Ingenjörer, Akademikerförbundet SSR, LO, NITO, and Negotia, the IBM pattern shifts the bargaining vocabulary ahead of the 2026 wage round: when the CHRO moves from personnel strategist to AI strategist, both transition insurance and reskilling clauses move up the agenda. For Bolagsverket, Finansinspektionen, Finanstilsynet, and the Swedish Competition Authority, the CAIO role's rapid spread becomes a concrete oversight question — who reports Annex III conformity after the EU trilogue on May 7, and how are agent decisions taken under Microsoft's Orchestrator pattern documented?

Source: CNBC, IBM Newsroom, CXO Voice, BW People, TechIntelPro, People Matters
Debate

Anthropic targets $900 billion and overtakes OpenAI — Financial Times reports $50-billion round with IPO in October, Claude Code and Cowork drive $45 billion run rate — hits middle of EQT and Wallenberg portfolios

On May 7, the Financial Times — followed up on May 8–9 by Tech Startups, PYMNTS, The Decoder, Investing.com, and Bloomberg digests — reported that Anthropic is considering a primary round of up to $50 billion at a valuation of around $900 billion, which would make the company more valuable than OpenAI's $852 billion from March and place Anthropic just below the threshold of "first AI company worth a trillion." Dragoneer, General Catalyst, and Lightspeed Venture Partners are named as leading investors; the board is expected to decide in May and the round will close within two months according to the FT, with a possible IPO as early as October 2026. The underlying growth is what drives the multiple: Anthropic's annualized revenue run rate has gone from $9 billion at the end of 2024 to $30 billion in March 2026 and is expected to soon exceed $45 billion — a fivefold increase over 17 months. The FT identifies Claude Code for developers and Claude Cowork for office users as the two heavy growth drivers, on top of the Microsoft 365 finance agents (May 5) and the Blackstone–Goldman–Hellman & Friedman JV (May 4). For the Nordics, the numbers land in the middle of a hot PE and pre-IPO market. EQT (around €270 billion AUM), Wallenberg Investments (FAM, Investor, Patricia Industries), Nordic Capital, Altor, Triton, Norvestor, and CapMan have portfolio companies directly affected when Anthropic is priced at multiples that can no longer be compared to traditional SaaS. For Klarna (Q1 report May 14, possible US IPO back in view), Spotify (Q1 report April 29, AI features in pipeline), Lovable (Stockholm-based, valued at $6.6 billion in April), and Sana Labs, the timing of the Anthropic round becomes a concrete negotiation boost — and an opposite risk factor: if Anthropic IPOs in October, the AI listing window may drain. For NBIM, AMF, Alecta, and the AP funds, the pricing presses index architecture: how much of the portfolio can sit in a handful of AI frontier names when oil fund chief Tangen already on May 5 called the concentration the largest in the fund's history? And for Vinnova, the Research Council of Norway, and Sferical AI, the question of sovereign-stack accelerates — if Anthropic becomes bigger than OpenAI while Microsoft contracts 30,000 Rubin GPUs to Narvik, which Nordic supplier strategy holds on a 10–15 year horizon?

Source: Tech Startups (FT digest), The Decoder, PYMNTS, Investing.com (FT), TechCrunch (April 29), The Next Web
Infrastructure

Nvidia tops $40 billion in AI equity stakes in 2026 — IREN deal May 7 opens up to 5 GW of DSX infrastructure, Corning gets $3.2 billion and three factories, same capacity lock that drove Microsoft to Narvik

On May 9, CNBC and Benzinga summarized Nvidia's rapid transformation from GPU supplier to broad-spectrum AI investor: in 2026 the company has committed more than $40 billion in strategic equity stakes — from OpenAI and CoreWeave to Cipher Mining and Goldman Sachs-backed Mistral — and is now expanding the portfolio to publicly listed stocks across the entire supply chain. Two heavy deals were announced the same week: on May 6, glass giant Corning received the right to take up to $3.2 billion from Nvidia in exchange for three new US fiber-optic factories dedicated to AI data centers — which tenfolds Corning's US optical volume and creates at least 3,000 new jobs. On May 7, Nvidia and data center operator IREN announced a strategic partnership where Nvidia can invest up to $2.1 billion (a five-year right to subscribe to 30 million shares at $70 each) in exchange for IREN rolling out up to 5 gigawatts of Nvidia's DSX-based AI infrastructure globally. Together with the Anthropic–SpaceX agreement on Colossus 1 in Memphis (May 6, 300 MW), Microsoft–Nscale in Narvik (April 14, 30,000 Rubin GPUs on top of $6.2 billion), the Stargate project, and hyperscaler capex of about $725 billion in 2026, the picture is unambiguous: GPUs and network units have moved from component to financial asset. For the Nordics, the consequences become concrete on four fronts. For Statkraft, Vattenfall, Fortum, Statnett, NVE, the Swedish Energy Agency, and Skellefteå Kraft, the 5 GW scale of the IREN deal presses Nordic grid expansion: Microsoft's Narvik campus, Google's Skien data center, AWS Stockholm Region, and EcoDataCenter in Borlänge now compete for megawatt contracts on the same curve as Memphis and Texas. For Telenor Sovereign Cloud (announced May 5, commercial launch H1 2027) and Sferical AI (SKF recently joined), IREN's DSX template becomes a concrete blueprint for how European sovereign-compute can be packaged technology-neutrally. For SKF, ABB, Sandvik, Atlas Copco, NKT, Hexagon, and Norway's Kongsberg — all of whom deliver industrial components, automation, or electronics into the data center chain — Corning's 3-factory pattern opens a concrete export pipeline. And for NBIM, the AP funds, and Wallenberg Investments, portfolio construction is forced to handle an Nvidia that is no longer a pure semiconductor company but the trillion-dollar market's AI financier.

Source: CNBC, Benzinga, NVIDIA Newsroom (IREN), CNBC (IREN), CNBC (Corning), Bloomberg (IREN), Telenor (Sovereign Cloud)
