OpenAI trains its own attacker AI to harden GPT-5.6 against attacks

OpenAI on Wednesday unveiled GPT-Red, an internal AI model trained solely to attack the company’s own models and find security holes. The goal is to close the weaknesses before the models reach users.

GPT-Red is trained through self-play, in which the attacker and a set of defender models are trained at the same time: the attacker is rewarded for successful prompt injections while the defenders are rewarded for resisting them. The result is a highly capable attacker – in an independent test setup it succeeded on 84 percent of attempts against an older model, compared with 13 percent for human experts. OpenAI then fed the attacks into the training of GPT-5.6 Sol, which is now said to fail on just 0.05 percent of GPT-Red’s direct injection attacks and to make six times fewer errors than the previous flagship. The attacker model itself is deliberately kept internal so the capability does not fall into the wrong hands.

For the Nordics, prompt injection is becoming an ever hotter issue as AI agents are connected to email, files and banking services. Sweden’s National Cyber Security Centre at the FRA was tasked earlier in July with becoming a contact point for companies building advanced AI models, and for major banks such as SEB and DNB testing agent AI on customers, OpenAI’s figures bear directly on how safe it is to let a model handle payments and sensitive data.

Government tasks FRA’s cyber centre with countering AI-driven cyber threats

The Swedish government has tasked the National Cyber Security Centre at the Swedish Defence Radio Agency (FRA) with coordinating and strengthening cybersecurity work linked to artificial intelligence. The decision is meant to meet the threats that follow as AI models now find software vulnerabilities faster than before.

Together with the agencies concerned, the centre is to prevent, detect and counter AI-driven cyberattacks, analyse the risks and dependencies around advanced models, and represent Sweden in international and European forums. It is also to become a contact point for companies that build advanced AI models. – The advance of AI models forces us to accelerate this work further, says Minister for Civil Defence Carl-Oskar Bohlin. The assignment is to be reported orally to the Government Offices no later than 13 November.

For the Nordics the decision reflects a wider unease: as AI can both find vulnerabilities faster and be exploited in attacks, pressure grows to protect the new AI infrastructure. Norway’s Økokrim has warned that data centres can be exploited for money laundering, and with Sweden and Norway in the middle of a data-centre boom from Luleå to Narvik, the question of who protects the facilities is becoming central in both countries.

Rolls-Royce and Quantinuum test quantum computing on turbines’ hardest calculations

Quantinuum, Rolls-Royce, Riverlane and the University of Edinburgh signed a multi-year agreement on Tuesday to explore whether today’s quantum computers can speed up the fluid-dynamics calculations used to design gas turbines – one of industry’s most computationally demanding problems.

The work will run on Quantinuum’s 98-qubit Helios processor, whose performance was recently confirmed in an independent evaluation. Rolls-Royce contributes industrial design cases, Riverlane contributes error correction, and Edinburgh’s supercomputing centre EPCC contributes hybrid computing in which quantum and supercomputers work side by side. The partners will test building blocks for industrially relevant quantum algorithms and assess how they can scale on planned systems such as Sol and Apollo.

For the Nordics the effort points toward the heavy engineering industries: Siemens Energy in Finspång builds industrial gas turbines and GKN Aerospace in Trollhättan makes engine parts for Rolls-Royce among others – both with the same kind of fluid-dynamics calculations in focus. On the quantum side, Chalmers and the Wallenberg initiative WACQT drive Swedish research that could connect Nordic industry to exactly this kind of hybrid computing.

Norwegian expert warns against leaning on AI to plan your holiday

Nearly half of holidaymakers now use AI to plan their trip, but a Norwegian expert warns in NRK against blindly trusting the chatbot as a travel guide.

The problem is that language models do not always check whether something has recently changed, but answer based on what there is most text about online – and they can then both invent opening hours and describe hotel rooms that do not match reality. International reporting points the same way: AI can sound entirely confident while being wrong, and the expert advises travellers to double-check its claims against official sources before booking.

For the Nordics the timing is sensitive in the middle of the Nordic holiday month of July, when Swedes and Norwegians are planning trips like never before. Consumer authorities such as Norway’s Forbrukertilsynet and Sweden’s Konsumentverket are watching how misleading AI content affects consumers, and the example shows how quickly the technology has gone from novelty to everyday tool – with the responsibility to fact-check left to the user.

Anthropic gives US teachers free access to Claude

Anthropic on Tuesday launched Claude for Teachers, a free version of Claude for verified K-12 educators in the United States. The offering matches a subscription that normally costs $20 per month.

The service connects Claude to curriculum-aligned academic standards in all 50 states through Learning Commons and to vetted instructional materials, with ready-made tools for lesson planning and for adapting materials to students at different levels. Data is not used for model training, and student information is protected under the US privacy law FERPA. Google, OpenAI, and Khan Academy are making similar pushes into schools.

For the Nordics, the move puts pressure on education authorities: Sweden’s National Agency for Education (Skolverket) has been tracking AI use in Swedish schools since May, and the government has commissioned work to counter AI cheating in upper secondary school. As AI giants start giving away tailored teacher tools for free, pressure grows on Skolverket and Norway’s Directorate for Education (Utdanningsdirektoratet) to clarify which tools belong in Nordic classrooms – and on what terms.

Swedish health agency to probe AI risks for children and teens

The Swedish government is tasking the Public Health Agency (Folkhälsomyndigheten) with compiling knowledge on how generative AI affects the mental health of children and young people, and with producing guidance and recommendations for children, parents, and relevant organizations.

According to the Swedish Internet Foundation’s report Svenskarna och internet 2025, more than half of all children aged 8 to 19 use AI tools, and many use AI as a companion or everyday adviser. The government points to risks such as reinforced negative thought patterns, weakened capacity for human relationships, sleep disturbances, and the spread of false health information. The agency will consult with the Swedish Consumer Agency, the Swedish Agency for the Media, and the telecom regulator PTS, among others. An interim report is due by June 1, 2027, and the final report by November 15, 2027.

The assignment is among the first in the Nordics to target AI companions and mental health specifically. It builds on the Public Health Agency’s screen-time recommendations and June’s recommendation to wait until age 13 before giving children their own phone – and the Swedish guidelines could become a reference point for Norwegian authorities as similar demands are raised there.

EU's new cloud and AI act out for Swedish consultation – replies due October 2

The Swedish government has sent the European Commission's proposed cloud and AI regulation, the Cloud and AI Development Act, out for national consultation and has submitted its explanatory memorandum to the Riksdag. Consultation responses are due by October 2.

The proposal, presented by the Commission on June 3, aims to strengthen Europe's cloud and AI ecosystem on three fronts: research and development of next-generation technology, faster build-out of data centers with a focus on essential public functions, and a single EU-wide framework for assessing sovereignty in cloud services. The goal is to roughly triple the EU's data center capacity within five to seven years. In its memorandum, the government takes a positive view of the ambition to strengthen the EU's capacity, competitiveness, and digital sovereignty.

For the Nordics, the proposal is business-critical: Sweden and Norway compete for AI data centers with abundant power and cooling, from Luleå and Boden to Narvik, and faster permitting across the EU could both boost the build-out and sharpen the competition. Swedish authorities and industry players have until October 2 to respond, and the consultation round lays the groundwork for Sweden's position when negotiations begin in the Council.

The LUMI AI Factory and Finland's CSC have selected IQM to deliver a new quantum computer, named LUMI-IQ. The system, an IQM Halocene H4 with 150 qubits, is to be installed in Kajaani, Finland, by 2027.

The quantum computer will be integrated directly with the European supercomputer LUMI so that classical and quantum processors can work in the same computational flow – including for developing hybrid AI models. For the Nordics: the LUMI consortium includes Sweden through the Swedish Research Council and Norway through Sigma2, so Swedish and Norwegian researchers will get access to the machine. The US Federal Reserve is setting up a task force to assess how AI affects productivity, jobs and monetary policy. Venture capitalist Marc Andreessen, Stanford economist Charles I. Jones and Microsoft's Asha Sharma will lead the group.

OpenAI has launched GPT-Live, a new generation of voice models that can listen and speak simultaneously. The technique, known as full duplex, makes conversations more human-like: the model can interject an "mhm" or stay quiet while the user thinks.

For the Nordics: more natural AI voices affect everything from Telenor's and Telia's customer service to voice-driven services at banks such as SEB and DNB. Research

Chalmers AI predicts molecular behavior 10,000 times faster

Researchers at Chalmers and the University of Gothenburg have trained an AI model that simulates how molecules behave about 10,000 times faster than traditional molecular dynamics. The work was carried out with AstraZeneca and published in the journal Science Advances.

Instead of calculating every tiny time step, the model learns the underlying rules for how molecules change and skips the intermediate steps. It was tested on more than 12,500 organic molecules and just over 1,000 peptides and can predict binding, stability and side-effect risk. A calculation that once took weeks can shrink to minutes – but the model does not replace clinical testing.

For the Nordics, this is a cutting edge in Nordic pharmaceutical research: AstraZeneca has large operations in Gothenburg, where much of the money in early development is spent. Faster screening of candidates can shorten the path to new medicines and strengthens the case for the national AI push that Chalmers, KTH and Linköping drive through WASP.

Microsoft moves its quantum-safe encryption deadline forward to 2029

Microsoft is bringing forward its target for the transition to quantum-safe encryption: the company's most important products and services are to be protected against quantum computer attacks by 2029, four years earlier than the roadmap towards 2033 published last year.

The background is that advances in quantum research have led Microsoft to conclude that cryptographically relevant quantum computers may arrive sooner than expected. Attackers can moreover already harvest encrypted traffic today in order to decrypt it once the machines exist – a tactic known as "harvest now, decrypt later". The effort focuses on three areas: network encryption with broader adoption of TLS 1.3, stored data where organisations need crypto-agility, and cryptographic trust chains. American and French authorities are already pointing towards quantum-safe requirements around 2030 for sensitive systems.

For the Nordics, the announcement puts pressure on both authorities and industry. Norway's security agency NSM and Sweden's MSB have both highlighted the quantum transition in their guidance, and banks such as DNB, SEB and Nordea manage encrypted data with a lifespan of decades. At Chalmers, the Wallenberg centre WACQT drives Swedish quantum research, and commentators in Norway's Digi recently warned that the timeline for the quantum threat is collapsing.

Finland’s IQM unveils a new quantum code – up to 1,000 times fewer errors

The Finnish quantum-computer company IQM has presented a new method that sharply reduces errors in quantum computers. Error correction is one of the biggest obstacles on the road to quantum computers that are practically useful.

The method, called “directional tile codes,” can cut the logical error rate per computing round by up to 1,000 times compared with the widely used surface code – and at roughly the same cost, around 30 physical qubits per logical qubit. The code uses only the nearest-neighbour gates (iSWAP) already built into IQM’s Crystal processors. The work, published on arXiv, was carried out together with researchers at Freie Universität Berlin, the University of Edinburgh and the University of Mainz. IQM, which is planning a Nasdaq listing, is targeting fault-tolerant quantum computing by 2030 and a path toward a million qubits.

For the Nordics, quantum technology is a cutting-edge field. IQM is one of Europe’s leading quantum companies, and in Sweden the Wallenberg Centre for Quantum Technology (WACQT) at Chalmers is building the country’s own quantum computer under the leadership of Göran Johansson, while Denmark has the Niels Bohr Institute and large investments via the Novo Nordisk Foundation. Error correction is precisely where the field decides whether quantum computers become practically useful – and there, Nordic research is at the front.

EU countries are approaching the final step of a major overhaul of the AI Act. After the European Parliament voted in favour of the so-called Digital Omnibus package on 16 June, the Council of Ministers is expected to give its formal approval on 29 June, after which the changes will be published in the EU's Official Journal.

The package postpones the heaviest requirements on high-risk AI: stand-alone systems in Annex III of the regulation – covering recruitment, credit scoring and law enforcement – now have until 2 December 2027, and AI embedded in regulated products until August 2028. Swedish and Norwegian companies building AI services therefore need to know, already this summer, what must be labelled and documented – even if the sharpest high-risk requirements are now delayed.
