The civilisational shift

AI, robotics and the path from a work society to an economy of abundance

A longer main essay for AI-skiftet.se on why AI, cheap energy, automation and new forms of production together can force a new social contract between 2026 and 2040.

We live in an era that is still too often described in language that is too small. Public debate talks about digitalisation, automation, AI tools, efficiency, upskilling and innovation. But those concepts are no longer enough. They describe improvements within a fundamentally unchanged system. What is now emerging is bigger than that. It is not just another wave of technology, not just another step in industrial rationalisation, not just a faster computer or smarter software. It is the beginning of a civilisational shift.

In this perspective, AI is not primarily a tool among others. AI is the first technology we have built that, on a large scale, is starting to compete with the human cognitive role itself: interpreting, reasoning, planning, formulating, analysing, comparing, choosing and coordinating. Earlier machines replaced or amplified muscles, motion, mechanical precision and information storage. The new wave instead pushes against the very centre of what has made human beings economically indispensable in the modern world. When thinking, or at least parts of thinking, becomes cheap to copy, fast to scale and easy to plug into other systems, it is not just individual tasks that change. It is the underlying logic of the economy.

We are therefore not just facing a transformation of working life. We are facing the possibility that the old social order — built on scarcity, wage labour and extractive growth — gradually starts to lose its stability. If intelligence becomes nearly free to multiply, if energy becomes more abundant and cheaper across larger parts of the system, if transport becomes automated, and if parts of food and material production can be produced with biological precision and lower resource input, then it is no longer enough to talk about productivity gains. We have to talk about a society in which old assumptions about work, value, ownership and meaning begin to come undone.

This does not mean the future will automatically be bright. On the contrary, the transition can be hard, uneven and full of conflict. History shows that when technological leaps are large enough, institutions, norms and political systems rarely keep up. Old rules continue to govern a society that has already been transformed beneath the surface. Power concentrates faster than responsibility. New forms of production create value while safety nets are still built for yesterday. In such periods, the same technology that promises greater abundance in the long run can in the short run produce stress, displacement, political confusion and social unrest.

That is precisely why this period must be understood correctly. The question is no longer whether AI will affect the economy. The question is how deep that impact runs, how quickly it interacts with other exponential technologies, and whether our societies are capable of building a new contract before the old one loses its function.

When thinking becomes cheap

For most of human history, advanced cognition has been an extremely scarce resource. Societies have always had fewer people who could understand complex systems, design robust solutions, make qualified decisions and navigate large bodies of information than they actually needed. That is why education, experience and expertise have carried such high value. Anyone who could draft a contract, design a process, program a solution, optimise a flow, interpret a market or structure uncertainty was doing something that was not easily copied.

It is this scarcity that is now being challenged. AI models are not yet omniscient, error-free or evenly competent. They hallucinate, misunderstand and vary in quality depending on task, data and integration. But they are already good enough to begin pushing down the cost of qualified thinking on a broad front. They can summarise, compare, translate, formulate, structure, analyse patterns, write code, propose solutions, process large volumes of text and generate useful decision support in seconds. When combined with tools, databases, business systems and increasingly agentic workflows, they move from being assistants to becoming operational components in real processes.

This is a break with previous waves of digitalisation. The spreadsheet did not replace the economist. The search engine did not replace the analyst. Email did not replace the manager. But systems that can perform parts of cognitive work themselves change the balance of power inside an organisation. They let fewer people do more. They give senior expertise greater reach. They allow parts of work that previously required trained staff to be carried out without every step having to pass through a human hand.

In the short term this looks like a productivity boost. And that is precisely why so many underestimate its explosive force. If every qualified employee suddenly becomes twice as capable, it sounds at first like ordinary efficiency. But if the same logic continues, and if workflows gradually become more autonomous, a much larger question arises: what happens to a society in which the economic necessity of large parts of cognitive work rapidly diminishes?

It is easy to assume that new jobs will simply emerge by themselves, as in earlier technological shifts. To some extent that will happen. New services, new professions and new needs always emerge. But this time it is not just one sector or one type of machine being automated. It is the very generality of human problem-solving that is being challenged. The classic consolation has been that machines take the repetitive while humans climb the value chain. The problem now is that the machine itself is starting to climb.

From tool to system

The decisive change therefore lies not just in the capacity of the models, but in how they are wired into entire operations. As long as AI is used as a separate tool, it feels manageable. An employee opens a chat, asks for help, copies the answer and moves on. But when AI becomes embedded in processes, product development, quality assurance, supply chain, planning, customer communication, finance, HR, legal and governance, it ceases to be a peripheral aid. It becomes part of the organisation's nervous system.

Here a mental error occurs in many leadership teams and political discussions. People still ask how AI can help individual roles, when the real question is how AI changes the architecture of the whole system. The old organisation was built around the fact that humans were the bottleneck for analysis, decisions and coordination. That is why levels, meetings, reports, middle managers, specialist functions and administrative layers were created — so information could move through human brains at a reasonable pace. But if information processing and coordination become cheaper and faster, the pressure to redraw the organisation will also rise.

This does not mean that companies become staff-free overnight. It means that more and more tasks that previously justified a certain number of people, a certain number of meetings or a certain number of layers will be questioned. Many roles do not exist because they carry a uniquely human value in themselves, but because the system historically needed them to keep its information flow together. When that precondition changes, the roles change too.

This is where AI moves from being an effective technology to becoming a societal force. Because when entire organisations become less dependent on human in-between work, intermediate links and intermediate translation, broad pressure builds against the white-collar layer. This applies not only to junior tasks. It also applies to a great deal of qualified white-collar work that has long been seen as protected precisely because it was cognitive.

The factory floor often sees change first

Much of the public debate about AI still plays out in seminars, on opinion pages or in presentations about office work. But one of the clearest ways to understand what is happening is to view it from production, process and actual operations. There the meaning of the technology becomes concrete more quickly. There you can see how small improvements in analysis, planning, quality, documentation or flow control produce large effects in reality. There it is also clearly visible how valuable it becomes when someone with deep operational understanding can suddenly amplify themselves with cheap cognitive capacity.

On the factory floor, in planning, in improvement work and in daily management, AI is not primarily a question of spectacular visions. It becomes a question of seeing patterns earlier, making better decisions sooner, documenting more clearly, analysing deviations more systematically and building support around people who already know the operation. Anyone who understands process, quality and production suddenly gains a new kind of reach.

That is why industrially grounded perspectives are so important in this discussion. The AI shift will not first be felt as a philosophical idea. It will be felt as small but cumulative productivity leaps in real operations. Fewer hours spent on analysis. Fewer bottlenecks in reporting. Better decision support. Shorter lead times from problem to action. More capacity in the same organisation. Fewer excuses to wait.

This is also where you see that the technological shift is not only about replacing people. Just as importantly, it changes the relationship between individuals, competence and scale. A skilled person can suddenly do things that previously required a team. An experienced engineer can broaden their scope. An improvement leader can work much more widely. A manager can get help to see connections that previously drowned in information. In the beginning this is more a multiplier effect than a pure replacement effect. But the multiplication is itself destabilising, because over time organisations will start rethinking how many people are actually needed to reach the same result.

The old economy's hidden assumption

To understand why this becomes so transformative, you need to see the assumption the modern economy rests on: that human labour is the central link between production and distribution. People work, get paid, and then use that pay to buy what is produced. It is not the only mechanism in the economy, but it is one of the most important. Work is therefore not just a way of getting things done. It is also a way of legitimising and organising how resources are distributed.

That is precisely why the labour market has acquired such great moral weight in modern societies. Those who work are seen as contributing. Those who earn wages are seen as having earned their purchasing power. Those outside work become not only economically weaker but often symbolically weaker as well. Our entire culture is steeped in this logic. Questions of responsibility, adulthood, self-respect and social status are tightly tied to work.

But what happens when this link weakens? What happens when a growing share of economic value can be created by systems that do not require wages, holidays, working environments, careers or human motivation? Then society's distribution model begins to wobble.

There is a common belief that the market will automatically solve this by creating new jobs. In some cases it will. But the question is not whether new jobs will exist at all. The question is whether they will appear at the scale and pace required to compensate when broad layers of cognitive, administrative and coordinating work become less in demand. There it is far from certain that history will repeat itself.

The difference compared with earlier technological shifts is that AI does not just improve a particular machine or process. It works as a general amplifier of almost all information processing. It can therefore simultaneously affect economics, law, education, healthcare, design, management, research, support, administration, logistics and production. When so many functions are affected at once, the time available for adjustment becomes much shorter than what our institutions are built for.

From scarcity economy to capacity economy

At the same time, it is important to understand that AI does not operate in a vacuum. Its real significance arises in interaction with other exponential technologies — above all cheaper renewable energy, better storage, more advanced automation and, eventually, new forms of biological production. This is what makes the question not just about jobs, but about the entire foundational structure of the economy.

Our current economy is built on the fact that much of what we need has a cost at the margin. Energy costs. Transport costs. Food requires land, inputs, logistics and labour. Advanced intelligence is expensive because it is bound to educated people. Many institutions and business models are adapted to this world. They make money by managing, distributing or controlling what is limited.

But if several of these costs are pushed down at the same time, the playing field changes. If intelligence becomes cheaper, energy cheaper, transport more automated and parts of production more local and data-driven, we are gradually moving from a scarcity economy to a capacity economy. That does not mean everything becomes free. It means that more and more central functions get such low marginal cost that the old pricing logic becomes less stable.

This is one of the hardest ideas to communicate, because people easily hear the word abundance and assume it is naive techno-utopianism. But the point is not to promise paradise. The point is to direct attention to the structural consequences when certain central inputs become radically cheaper than before. A society where intelligence can be copied almost for free behaves differently from one where it is tightly bound to human education time. A society with very cheap electricity behaves differently from one where energy cost sets hard limits. A society with local, automated production behaves differently from one where everything depends on long and fragile global chains.

The decisive question therefore becomes not just how much can be produced, but who controls the productive capacity and how access is organised.

Energy as multiplier

There is a risk that AI is discussed as if it were primarily a software question. But AI is fundamentally also an energy question. Compute requires electricity. Data centres require electricity. Robots, autonomous vehicles, sensor networks and automated production require electricity. That is why developments in solar, wind and storage are not a side track but a part of the same civilisational shift.

As the cost of renewable energy continues to fall, two important effects emerge. The first is direct: it becomes cheaper to run the systems that carry the AI economy. The second is systemic: when energy becomes cleaner, more distributed and at times very cheap, it becomes possible to electrify more parts of the economy and thereby tie more sectors together on the same technical foundation.

Cheap energy lowers the threshold for automation. Cheap energy lowers the cost of processing data. Cheap energy lowers the cost of running compute-heavy models. Cheap energy can also lower the cost of water treatment, climate control, growing systems, local manufacturing and new types of biological production. Energy systems therefore act as a multiplier for the rest of the transition.

This also means that old assets risk losing value faster than markets are prepared for. Fossil infrastructure, certain older energy models and parts of the capital structure built around earlier cost levels can become less viable when new technology offers significantly lower marginal costs. The transition therefore becomes not just an opportunity but also a redistribution of power and value, where large incumbent interests have a great deal to lose.

Transport, food and broader marginal cost pressure

A similar logic appears in other sectors. Transport economics can change as electric drive, software-driven control, better batteries and gradually more autonomous operation start to lower costs and raise utilisation rates. Anyone who owns a vehicle privately is in practice paying for an expensive asset to mostly stand still. When transport is instead organised as a service, with high utilisation and lower operating costs, private ownership in certain use cases starts to look like an inefficient remnant of an earlier economy.

The food system can in time face similar pressure. Here the timelines are more uncertain and progress will be uneven, but the principle is important: if parts of food production can be moved from land-intensive, weather-dependent, biologically inefficient systems to more precise, local and technically controlled processes, then both the cost picture and the resource logic change. The effect is not only cheaper production but potentially also lower vulnerability, shorter chains and reduced dependence on certain old structures.

When many sectors face such marginal-cost pressure simultaneously, a deep civilisational change emerges. The economy then no longer revolves primarily around managing limited flows through expensive coordination of human labour. Instead it begins to revolve around how capacity, access and ownership are distributed in a system where more and more can be done more cheaply, more quickly and with less human input.

The turbulent transition

The most likely outcome is therefore not a soft post-scarcity society with harmonious adjustment. The most likely outcome is a turbulent transition in which the old system loses function before the new one has gained legitimacy. Roughly between the second half of the 2020s and 2040, this tension may become the dominant societal question.

The first phase is already here: AI amplifies individuals and pushes down the cost of cognitive work. The second phase will come when agentic workflows become more stable and start to take over larger parts of operations from beginning to end. The third phase will come when this is more closely coupled with physical automation, robotics and more self-directed operation in more environments. The fourth phase will be political: the struggle over how the value from this development is to be distributed, and which institutions should carry a society in which wage labour can no longer function as the sole basis for security and status.

In this transition, much of what is perceived today as stable will feel less self-evident. Career paths can erode. Professional identity can weaken. Educational models can begin to feel outdated. Companies will reorganise faster than people can rebuild their lives. The public sector will be squeezed between rising needs and outdated structures. Trade union models can be challenged as bargaining shifts to an economy where productivity increasingly sits in systems, data and models rather than in human hours.

The transition therefore risks creating both material and psychological instability. It is possible that some groups will become better off economically while feeling more replaceable than ever. It is also possible that other groups will be hit directly by the loss of demand for their work before any new social safety net has had time to form. In such an environment, politics can quickly become reactive, jerky and polarised.

Power at the centre

This leads to a core question that is often hidden behind technological optimism: who will own the cheap intelligence? Who will own the robotics, the energy systems, the compute capacity, the large models, the data infrastructure and the platforms where all of this capability is connected together?

It is entirely possible for the technology itself to push marginal costs down while ownership concentrates strongly upward. Such a society could produce more than ever and yet become socially harsher. It could have the capacity to give everyone security but choose to organise the abundance through new forms of artificial scarcity, subscriptions, platform power and asymmetric control. We would then not get a liberated civilisation, but rather a kind of digital feudalism where a small number of actors control the infrastructure of intelligence and the rest of society gains access on terms set by them.

This is not a theoretical side issue. It is the central power question of the AI shift. If productive capacity becomes ever less dependent on mass labour but ever more dependent on compute, energy, data and automated systems, then ownership of these assets becomes society's central line of conflict. Distribution policy can no longer rest primarily on taxing labour after the fact. It must increasingly be about how the productive systems of the future are owned, regulated and shared.

There is also a geopolitical dimension here. States and regions that control chips, energy, infrastructure, data, robotics and competence will be able to pull ahead. The difference between being a producer of the systems of the future and being a dependent user of them can become decisive. For smaller export-dependent countries this is particularly sensitive. To stand without one's own capability, without strategic understanding and without institutional speed in such a shift would be to voluntarily accept marginalisation.

Sweden risks thinking too small

The Swedish conversation about AI is still often shaped by the wrong scale. The debate gladly circles around whether schools may use generative tools, whether an agency should publish guidelines, whether a municipality should have a policy, or whether a company should train its white-collar staff. All of this can be relevant in its own way, but it does not capture the size of what is happening.

The problem is not that Sweden lacks intelligent people or technical capability. The problem is that much of the institutional discussion is still being conducted as if AI were yet another digitalisation question within existing frames. As a result the pace becomes too slow, the level of ambition too limited and the social analysis too narrow. What is missed is that AI is not just a technology that improves the system. It is pushing for the system itself to be renegotiated.

For a small, export-dependent, highly specialised country, this should be obvious. Sweden is heavily dependent on functioning industry, advanced competence, trust, education and high-quality institutions. That is precisely why we have much to gain by understanding AI early and practically. But we also have much to lose if we react slowly and let others define both the infrastructure and the rules of the game.

Sweden's strength lies in being able to combine technical know-how, industrial grounding and society-oriented problem-solving. But this requires AI to be understood as a national capacity shift, not as an isolated software issue. The labour market, education, industrial policy, energy supply, welfare, defence, public digitalisation and democratic resilience must be thought of together. A policy here and a testbed there is not enough. If the shift is civilisational, the response must also be systemic.

After work

Perhaps the deepest question is not economic but existential. What happens to the human being when work loses its role as the self-evident centre of life? Throughout modernity, the job has been more than provision. It has provided daily structure, context, status, responsibility, belonging and a way to measure oneself. Work has given people a place in the social story.

If this structure weakens, many will first think in economic terms: how do we finance security? But that is only half the question. The other half is what will hold together people's daily life, identity and self-respect when society can no longer lean as heavily on the work imperative.

A post-work society does not automatically become more humane. It can become empty, fragmented and passivising if it is not built around something more than consumption. If people are no longer needed in production in the same way, society must become much better at giving value to things that have previously stood in the shadow of wage labour: relationships, care, local responsibility, creativity, learning, civic life, environmental stewardship, spirituality, culture and voluntary common work.

Here the future of education becomes decisive. An educational system that primarily trains people for the labour market risks becoming insufficient in a world where the labour market changes faster than the curricula. The education of the future must to a greater extent develop judgement, creativity, the ability to cooperate, ethics, psychological resilience and the ability to live meaningfully in a world where machines can do more of what previously gave human beings their economic justification.

This is not a soft side issue. It is a civilisational question. If we fail to formulate meaning beyond the job, even material progress will rest on social unrest.

A new social contract

It is therefore not enough to describe the upheaval. We must also dare to talk about what needs to be built in its wake. The old societal model was not eternal. It arose under specific material conditions: industrial production, human labour as a central input, national welfare systems and an economy where most people were needed to make society function. If those conditions change, the contract must change too.

First, safety nets need to become less unilaterally dependent on wage labour over the long term. This can happen in different ways: through new forms of basic security, citizens' dividends, common funds, broadened ownership or other models that let citizens share in the productivity gains of automation. The important thing is the principle: if societal wealth is increasingly created by systems that do not require broad human employment, access to that wealth must still be widely distributed if society is to remain legitimate.

Second, the state and public institutions must build real AI capacity. It is not enough to procure point solutions or to write cautious guidelines. The state must understand the technology well enough to be able to govern, use, audit and negotiate around it. Otherwise society-bearing functions will end up dependent on external actors with greater technical competence than the institutions meant to regulate them.

Third, the question of ownership must be lifted from the margin to the centre. In a society where value increasingly arises in automated systems, we cannot pretend that ownership structure is a secondary detail. It matters greatly whether the productive capacity of the future is owned in extreme concentration or whether people, through pension funds, national funds, cooperative models or other mechanisms, gain a broader share of it.

Fourth, we need a new cultural story about human worth. As long as dignity is almost entirely tied to traditional work, every wave of automation will be experienced as an existential threat. A mature society must be able to recognise that a human being has value beyond their market function. This does not mean ambition, responsibility and achievement should disappear. It means they can no longer alone define a person's right to live a secure and meaningful life.

Risks must not be underestimated

It would at the same time be irresponsible to write about this without naming the deeper risks. The more autonomous, capable and central AI systems become, the greater the risks of abuse of power, manipulation, security problems and loss of human control. A society that becomes dependent on complex systems without understanding them becomes vulnerable. A society where a small number of actors can shape information flows, decision support and economic coordination on a global scale also becomes politically fragile.

There is also a more fundamental risk: that humanity simply moves faster than its own institutional understanding. We can build tools whose collective consequences we cannot yet survey. We can create incentives that reward fast deployment more than safety. We can find ourselves in a situation where every individual actor feels compelled to rush in order not to fall behind, even though everyone simultaneously knows that this raises the shared risk.

Technological progress must therefore be combined with an unusually high level of sobriety. We need neither panic nor blind optimism. We need strategic realism. We need to understand that the development is probably faster than many think, that the consequences are broader than most debates admit, and that passivity in this situation is not neutrality but a decision to let other forces set the rules of the game.

From work society to dignity society

If you raise your gaze high enough, the contours become clear. The era now coming to an end has been the era of scarcity. It has rested on the necessity of human toil, on production requiring much labour, on energy being expensive, on intelligence being hard to scale and on social order therefore being organisable around work as duty and as the foundation of rights.

The era that may now begin to emerge looks different. There intelligence is more copyable, production more automatable, energy cheaper, certain marginal costs lower and the coordination of society ever less dependent on human in-between work. This can become the beginning of a society of abundance. But it can also become the beginning of a very harsh transition in which old securities are torn down before new ones have been built.

Everything is therefore decided not by the technology itself, but by how we choose to organise it. The real test of our time is not whether we can create artificial intelligence that is stronger than humans on many tasks. The test of our time is whether we can build institutions, forms of ownership and cultural stories that let this new capacity benefit many instead of being locked up among a few.

That is why the AI question is at its core not technical. It is constitutional. It is about how the rules of civilisation should be rewritten when the old link between work, value and survival weakens. It is about whether we dare to leave behind an order in which a person's place is primarily legitimised through their economic function, and instead build an order in which technological abundance is used to strengthen human dignity.

If we fail, the result can be a world of concentrated power, mass insecurity and a passivised population. If we succeed, we can open the door to something previously almost unthinkable: a society where fewer people are forced to sell their entire lives to survive, where basic security is stronger, where nature can recover and where humanity's highest task is no longer to keep the machine of scarcity running.

This is what is at stake in the AI shift.

Not just how we work.

But how we live, what we value and what kind of civilisation we want to leave behind us.
